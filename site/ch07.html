CHAPTER 07

Measuring Engineering Productivity

衡量工程生產力
作者 Ciera Jaspan PDF p.151–166 Scale 規模 Trade-offs 取捨 Culture 文化
"Success means giving a stakeholder the data they need to make a decision." 所謂成功，是把決策者做決定所需的資料交到他手上。

摘要

這是「文化」篇的收尾，主題是 Google 如何用資料、而非直覺衡量並改善工程生產力。作者來自專門研究工程生產力的團隊——刻意混入認知心理學、行為經濟學等社會科學家，因為要研究的不只是「軟體製品」，還有「人」這一側（動機、誘因、處理複雜任務的策略）。

全章用一條真實案例貫穿：ch03 的 readability 制度到底值不值得？ 三個關卡：① Triage 分流——先把問題寫成具體問句，再追問五題，核心是「拿到結果（含負面）會不會導向具體行動」；② GSM 框架選指標（Goal→Signal→Metric），目標涵蓋 QUANTS 五面向以免顧此失彼；③ 用質化驗證量化並採取行動。最終結論：readability 確實值得——有它的作者程式碼被審查與提交都更快（即使已扣掉不再需要那麼多審查者）、滿意且有學到。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你想推一個「能提升生產力」的改動，卻拿不出證據。 GSM 是一套可防禦的方法：先講目標、再導出訊號與指標，讓利害關係人事先同意「這就是衡量它的最佳指標組」，杜絕事後換指標的扯皮。
你或主管愛用 LOC、commit 數、修了幾個 bug 當生產力指標。 Dijkstra 說 LOC 是「花掉的」不是「產出的」；本章警告「路燈效應」與用指標考核個人會立刻被 gaming、從此失效。
你常還沒想清楚「測來幹嘛」就先收數據。 Triage 逼你先問「拿到結果會採取什麼行動？」——若「什麼都不會改」，這次測量從一開始就是浪費。測量成功＝決策者真的用了資料。
你只信硬數據、看不起問卷/訪談。 量化＋質化「三角驗證」：median build latency 的烏龍（自動建置被誤計）正是靠質化抽樣才抓到。質化指標也是指標。

細節重點

規模 Why Measure Productivity? 為何要衡量

組織線性成長時，溝通成本卻是二次方成長（Brooks《人月神話》）。所以無法把業務範圍線性擴展到組織規模——另一條路是讓每個工程師更有生產力。
工程意涵：但改進循環本身要耗人力——目標是「不只提升生產力，還要有效率地提升」。Google 成立專責研究團隊、混入社會科學家，把全書 Scale 主軸用在「人」身上。

取捨 Triage：值得測嗎？ Is It Even Worth Measuring?

測量昂貴，且光是追蹤就會改變工程師行為、掩蓋真相。先寫成具體問句，再追問五題：
  1. 你預期什麼結果、為什麼？（承認非中立，先講預設立場以對抗偏誤）
  2. 若資料支持，會採取什麼行動？（不會行動就別測）
  3. 若得到負面結果，會採取行動嗎？——這題殺掉最多專案：只想知道、不會改方向，就不值得測。
  4. 誰、何時會根據結果行動？（並了解什麼形式的資料能說服他）
不值得測的常見情況：現在無力改變、結果很快被作廢、決策者成見不可動搖、只是虛榮指標（vanity metrics）、可用指標太不精確（如 LOC）。工程意涵：測量的成功不是證明假設對錯，而是把決策者所需的資料交到他手上；他不用，就是失敗。

框架 GSM：用目標與訊號選指標 Goals / Signals / Metrics

決定要測之後，用 GSM 由上而下選指標——LOC 顯然不行。
  • 三個好處：避免路燈效應（只在燈下找鑰匙）、事先有原則地選指標避免 metrics creep/bias、顯示覆蓋缺口（有些訊號不可測，沒關係——至少你知道）。
  • 目標非一對一：每個目標至少一個訊號、可有多個、可共用；指標是訊號的不完美代理，可用多個來三角驗證。
  • code quality 暫不可測：學界代理都沒真正抓到它 → readability 決定不把它當量化指標，只請工程師自評。

取捨 QUANTS：生產力的五個面向 目標要涵蓋全部，以免顧此失彼

團隊常忘了納入取捨——「我能讓審查飛快：把 code review 整個拿掉就好」。所以把生產力切成五個彼此取捨的面向：

驗證 Using Data to Validate Metrics 用質化驗證量化

照 GSM 跑完仍可能「指標沒抓對訊號」——用質化資料驗證量化指標。
  • median build latency 烏龍：經驗抽樣發現有人「根本沒啟動建置」——是自動工具在跑、工程師沒被卡，不該計入「典型體驗」，於是排除。
  • 量化 vs 質化：量化給規模與信心、沒有脈絡與「為什麼」；質化給敘事與下一步。兩者衝突時，通常是量化沒抓對。
  • 三個來源：readability 專屬問卷（避回憶偏誤、引入近因/抽樣偏誤）＋季度問卷＋細粒度 logs（review/shepherding/submit 時間）。
  • 鐵則：絕不用生產力指標考核個人——會立刻被 gaming。「放下衡量個人、擁抱衡量總體效應。」
採取行動：建議要「工具驅動」內建進工作流與誘因——假設工程師在有正確資料與合適工具時就會做出恰當取捨。readability 結論：整體值得，並據痛點改善了工具與流程。

關鍵字補充

GSM Framework（Goals/Signals/Metrics）
由上而下選指標：目標→訊號→指標。避免路燈效應、保持可追溯性。
QUANTS（生產力五面向）
Quality 品質、Attention 專注、iNtellectual complexity 智識複雜度、Tempo/velocity 速度、Satisfaction 滿意度。彼此取捨，確保不顧此失彼。
Streetlight Effect（路燈效應）
只用手邊好測的指標（在路燈下找鑰匙），而非真正能回答目標的指標。GSM 由上而下就是為避免它。
Traceability（可追溯性）
每個指標都能追回它代理的訊號與要量的目標。確保你知道在測什麼、為何測。
Triage（測量分流）
投入前用五問判斷「值不值得測」。核心：拿到結果（含負面）會不會導向具體行動。
Vanity Metrics（虛榮指標）
只用來支持一個你本來就要做的決定的指標。Google 最常用來勸退測量的理由。
Quantitative vs Qualitative（量化 vs 質化）
量化給規模與信心、沒有脈絡；質化給「為什麼」與敘事。衝突時通常是量化沒抓對。質化也是指標。
Experience Sampling（經驗抽樣）
在工程師正做某任務的當下打斷、問幾題，捕捉情境中的真實體驗。用來抓出 median build latency 的自動建置誤計。
Recall / Recency / Sampling Bias
問卷時點與對象造成的三種偏誤；流程後立即問可避回憶偏誤，但引入近因與抽樣偏誤。
Tool-Driven Recommendation（工具驅動的建議）
把改進內建進開發者的日常工作流與誘因，而非只叫人改變思維；改變才更可能發生。

延伸提問

  1. 挑一個你或主管最近用來談「生產力/績效」的指標（LOC、commit 數、velocity、修了幾個 bug、PR 數）。它是 Goal、Signal 還是 Metric？能追溯回哪個你真正在乎的目標嗎？還是只是「路燈下」最好測的那個？
  2. 把你最近想做的「流程/工具改動」套上 Triage 五問。最關鍵的一題：如果量出來是負面結果，你（或決策者）真的會改變決定嗎？不會的話，這是不是其實是 vanity metric？
  3. 用 QUANTS 檢查你團隊現行的「效率改善」目標：是不是只盯 Tempo/velocity，卻沒在量 Quality 或 Satisfaction？有沒有可能正在「把 code review 拿掉式」地犧牲另一面向？
  4. 你的組織有沒有把某個生產力/品質指標用在個人績效考核？有沒有被 gaming 的跡象（為衝指標而寫的水 code、灌的 commit）？對照「衡量總體、不衡量個人」。
  5. 回想上次「只信硬數據、不信問卷/訪談」的時刻。有沒有可能錯過了 median build latency 那種「量化其實沒抓對」的烏龍？你能設計一個質化抽樣去驗證最依賴的量化指標嗎？對照 ch06 Web Search。

Part II — Culture (closing)
CHAPTER 07

Measuring Engineering Productivity

Measure with data, not intuition — and only when a decision rides on it
by Ciera Jaspan PDF p.151–166 Scale Trade-offs Culture
"Success means giving a stakeholder the data they need to make a decision." In plain terms: measurement isn't about proving you're right — it's about handing the decider what they need.

Summary

This closes the "Culture" part, on how Google measures and improves engineering productivity with data, not intuition. The author is from the dedicated productivity-research team — which deliberately mixes in social scientists (cognitive psychology, behavioral economics), because what needs studying isn't just software artifacts but the human side (motivation, incentives, strategies for complex tasks).

One real case runs through it: is ch03's readability process even worth it? Three gates: ① Triage — write the question concretely, ask five things, the crux being "would a result (incl. a negative one) lead to concrete action"; ② pick metrics with GSM (Goal→Signal→Metric), covering the QUANTS five facets so you don't improve one while tanking another; ③ validate quant with qual, then act. The verdict: readability is worth it — authors with it get reviewed and submit faster (even accounting for fewer reviewers), satisfied, and feel they learned.

Why read this

You want to push a "productivity" change but can't prove it. GSM is a defensible method: state the goal, derive signals and metrics, get stakeholders to agree in advance "these are the right metrics," killing the post-hoc "use different metrics" argument.
You or your boss love LOC, commits, or bugs-closed. Dijkstra: LOC is "lines spent," not produced. The chapter warns of the streetlight effect and how using metrics for individual reviews gets gamed and destroys their value.
You collect data before thinking "what for." Triage forces "what action follows the result?" — if "nothing changes," the measurement was a waste. Success = the decider used the data.
You only trust hard data, dismiss surveys/interviews. Quant + qual triangulation: the median-build-latency blunder (auto-builds miscounted) was caught only by qualitative sampling. Qualitative metrics are metrics too.

Key details

SCALE Why Measure Productivity?

As an org grows linearly, communication costs grow quadratically (Brooks' Mythical Man-Month). So you can't scale business scope linearly with headcount — the other path is making each engineer more productive.
Takeaway: but the improvement loop costs people too — the goal is to improve productivity efficiently. Google staffs a dedicated research team with social scientists, applying the book's Scale thesis to *people*.

TRADE-OFF Triage: Is It Even Worth Measuring?

Measurement is expensive, and the mere act of tracking can change behavior and mask the truth. Write it as a concrete question, then ask five things:
  1. What result do you expect, and why? (admit you're not neutral; state priors to fight bias)
  2. If data supports it, what action follows? (no action → don't measure)
  3. If you get a negative result, will you act? — this kills most projects: just wanting to know, with no change of course, isn't worth measuring.
  4. Who acts on the result, and when? (and what form of data convinces them)
Common reasons NOT to measure: can't afford to change now, results soon invalidated, the decider's opinions are unshakable, it's only a vanity metric, or the available metrics are too imprecise (e.g., LOC). Takeaway: success isn't proving a hypothesis right/wrong — it's handing the decider the data they need; if they won't use it, it's a failure.

FRAMEWORK GSM: Goals / Signals / Metrics

Once you decide to measure, use GSM top-down — LOC clearly won't do.
  • Three benefits: avoids the streetlight effect, picks metrics principled and in advance to prevent metrics creep/bias, and shows coverage gaps (some signals are unmeasurable — fine, at least you know).
  • Not 1:1: every goal has ≥1 signal, possibly more, possibly shared; metrics are imperfect proxies, and several can triangulate one signal.
  • Code quality is currently unmeasurable: no academic proxy truly captures it → readability didn't measure it quantitatively, only self-ratings.

TRADE-OFF QUANTS: the five facets of productivity

Teams forget the trade-offs — "I can make reviews fast: just remove code reviews." So productivity is split into five mutually trading-off facets:

VALIDATE Using Data to Validate Metrics

Even after GSM, a metric may miss its signal — validate quantitative metrics with qualitative data.
  • Median-build-latency blunder: experience sampling found engineers who "didn't start a build" — automated tools did, and they weren't blocked, so it shouldn't count toward "typical experience"; excluded.
  • Quant vs qual: quant gives scale and confidence, no context or "why"; qual gives narrative and next steps. When they disagree, it's usually quant that's wrong.
  • Three sources: readability-specific survey (avoids recall bias, introduces recency/sampling bias) + quarterly survey + fine-grained logs (review/shepherding/submit time).
  • The rule: never use productivity metrics to evaluate individuals — instantly gamed. "Let go of measuring individuals; embrace the aggregate effect."
Taking action: recommendations should be tool-driven, built into workflow and incentives — assume engineers make the right trade-offs given the right data and tools. Readability verdict: worth it overall, with pain points fixed in tooling and process.

Key terms

GSM framework (Goals/Signals/Metrics)
Pick metrics top-down: goal → signal → metric. Avoids the streetlight effect; preserves traceability.
QUANTS (five facets)
Quality, Attention, iNtellectual complexity, Tempo/velocity, Satisfaction. In mutual trade-off, so you don't improve one while tanking another.
Streetlight effect
Using only easy-to-measure metrics (keys under the streetlight) instead of those answering the goal. GSM's top-down flow avoids it.
Traceability
Every metric traces back to the signal it proxies and the goal it measures, so you know what you measure and why.
Triage (of measurement)
Five questions to decide whether it's worth measuring. The crux: would a result (incl. a negative one) lead to concrete action?
Vanity metrics
Metrics used only to support a decision you'd make anyway. Google's most common reason to tell people not to measure.
Quantitative vs qualitative
Quant gives scale and confidence but no context; qual gives "why" and narrative. When they disagree, it's usually quant that's wrong. Qual metrics are metrics too.
Experience sampling
Interrupting engineers in context of a task to ask a few questions, capturing the real in-the-moment experience. Caught the auto-build miscount.
Recall / recency / sampling bias
Memory/sampling biases driven by when and whom you survey; asking right after avoids recall bias but introduces recency and sampling bias.
Tool-driven recommendation
Build improvements into the developer's daily workflow and incentives, rather than telling people to change their thinking.

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Pick a metric you or your boss uses for "productivity/performance" (LOC, commits, velocity, bugs closed, PRs). Is it a Goal, Signal, or Metric? Does it trace back to a goal you care about — or is it just the best-lit thing under the streetlight?
  2. Run your latest proposed change through the five Triage questions. The crux: if the result came back negative, would you (or the decider) actually change the decision? If not, is this a vanity metric?
  3. Use QUANTS to audit your "efficiency" goals: only watching Tempo/velocity while never measuring Quality or Satisfaction? Could you be "removing code reviews"-style sacrificing another facet?
  4. Does your org use a productivity/quality metric in individual performance reviews? Any signs of gaming (filler code, padded commits)? Per "measure the aggregate, not individuals."
  5. Recall a time you "only trusted hard data." Could you have missed a median-build-latency-style "the quant was wrong" blunder? Could you design a qualitative sample to validate the quant metric you rely on most? Per ch06's Web Search.

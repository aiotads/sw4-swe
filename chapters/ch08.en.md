# Chapter 8 — Style Guides and Rules

> *"It's the consistency of having one answer rather than the answer itself that is the valuable part."*
> In plain terms: the win is that there's *one* answer — not which answer it is.

## Summary

This opens the "Processes" part (Part III). Google governs a codebase of **2 billion+ lines, ~60,000 submissions a day, 30,000+ engineers, expected to live for decades** with a set of **per-language style guides.** Author Shaindel Schwartz draws the key distinction first: **rules are laws — strict, mandatory, universally enforceable, not to be ignored except by approved waiver; guidance is recommendations and best practices.** In a phrase: **rules are the "musts," guidance is the "shoulds."**

When defining rules, the question isn't "what rules should we have?" but "**what goal are we advancing?**" Google's goal: **manage the complexity** of its development environment under the twin pressures of scale and time. From this come five guiding principles — rules must ① **pull their weight** (not everything should be a rule); ② **optimize for the reader** (code is read far more than written; "simple to read" beats "simple to write"); ③ **be consistent** (consistency enables expert "chunking," lets tooling scale, and gives both code and org resilience to personnel churn); ④ **avoid error-prone and surprising constructs** (e.g., restrict Python reflection); ⑤ **concede to practicalities when necessary** (Emerson: "a foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds" — performance and interoperability can warrant exceptions).

Style-guide rules fall into three buckets: **avoid danger, enforce best practices, ensure consistency.** Rules aren't static — **the process to change them is solution-based, starts with community discussion, and is decided by per-language style arbiters via consensus, not voting; every rule records the pros/cons behind it** so you can tell when conditions have changed enough to reconsider (e.g., Python moving from CamelCase to snake_case, or 2-space to 4-space indents). Finally, **enforcement**: rules are valuable only if enforceable — automate technically (error checkers like clang-tidy/Error Prone, formatters like gofmt + presubmit) whenever feasible, because **"the robots are better on average than the humans by a significant amount,"** automation removes human bias, and it scales (≈ **90%** of C++ rules are auto-verifiable).

## Why read this

- **Your team still argues "two spaces or four," "80 or 100 columns" in code review.** This gives you the argument that ends those debates: **the point isn't which answer, it's having only one** — then automate it with a formatter + presubmit so the question "stops being interesting."
- **You want to introduce a team rule but aren't sure it should exist.** "Rules must pull their weight" is the test: if only one or two people get it wrong, a rule everyone must remember isn't worth it — don't promote every best practice to a mandatory rule.
- **Are you writing for the future reader or for present-you?** "Optimize for the reader; simple to read beats simple to write" is the most immediately applicable mindset — `std::unique_ptr` ownership, the `override` annotation, and local reasoning all put *intent* right at the call site.
- **You want to upgrade rules from "watched by humans" to "blocked by tools."** The chapter spells out why technical enforcement beats social (it isn't forgotten, removes bias, scales) — and which rules *should* stay human-judged (judgment-requiring and social ones, like CL size). Don't solve social problems with technical solutions.

## Key details

### Why Have Rules?
The core claim: rules exist to **encourage "good" behavior and discourage "bad"** — and "good"/"bad" **vary by organization and are subjective** (some prize small memory footprint, some new language features, some consistency above all). First recognize what your org values, then use rules and guidance accordingly.

Takeaway: as an org grows, rules shape a **common vocabulary of coding** — letting engineers focus on *what* the code says rather than *how*, and doing the "good" thing by default, even subconsciously. Rules give broad leverage to *nudge* common patterns in desired directions.

### Creating the Rules: Guiding Principles
The core claim: the key question isn't "what rules" but "**what goal**." Google's context — 30,000+ engineers of wildly varying skill, 60,000 submissions/day, 2 billion lines, alive for decades — means the goal is to **manage complexity so the codebase is resilient to scale and time.** It's a trade-off: lots of rules restrict choice, cost flexibility, even offend some people, but **the consistency and reduced conflict of an authoritative standard win out.** The five principles:

- **① Rules must pull their weight:** not everything belongs in the guide. Teaching every engineer a new rule has nonzero cost; too many rules become hard to remember, learn, and maintain. **Deliberately omit self-evident rules** — the C++ guide has no rule against `goto` because C++ engineers already avoid it; if one or two people get something wrong, burdening everyone doesn't scale. (Tooling matters: "too many" isn't the raw count but how many an engineer must *remember*; clang-format slashed the cost of formatting rules.)
- **② Optimize for the reader:** code is read far more than written — **"simple to read" beats "simple to write."** Better tedious to type than hard to read (Python restricts conditional expressions, requires descriptive names). Require **explicit in-place evidence of intent**: the `override` annotation; `std::unique_ptr` to express ownership transfer (callers must `std::move`, so readers see ownership is yielded and the pointer can't be used after the call). The goal is **local reasoning** — understand the call site without digging into the implementation. Comment rules (documentation comments = design/intent; implementation comments = justify non-obvious choices) serve the same goal.
- **③ Be consistent:** like Google offices — each has local flavor, but **your badge works at every badge reader, devices always get WiFi, every conference room's video setup has the same interface**, so a Googler can move between offices and work immediately. Code is the same: consistency lets any engineer jump into unfamiliar code and get productive fast.

#### Advantages of Consistency
- **Focus on "what's done," not "how it's presented":** consistency enables **expert chunking** (grouping info into meaningful chunks — chess experts see configurations, not individual pieces), and makes modularizing and spotting duplication easier. Many rules just guarantee "one way" (indent spaces, line length) — **the value is having one answer, not which answer.**
- **Consistency enables tooling to scale:** consistent code is easier to build tools that understand, edit, and generate code; if everyone has little pockets of difference, the tools can't run everywhere. Consistency → one expert team's tool serves the whole company.
- **Consistency scales the human side too:** lowers ramp-up time on team switches; lets cross-project roles (SREs, library engineers, code janitors) jump in efficiently.
- **Consistency gives resilience to time:** people leave and join, ownership shifts, projects merge/split — consistent code makes those transitions cheap.

#### At Scale & the Hierarchy of Consistency
The core claim: years ago the C++ guide promised to almost never change rules that would make old code inconsistent; as the codebase grew bigger and older, that promise was **consciously struck** — stating that the C++ codebase **will never again be fully consistent, nor is that the aim**, because updating rules *and* applying them to everything ever written is too big a burden (LSC tooling updates most old code but can't guarantee every corner; see [[ch22]]). **Demanding perfect consistency has reached "too much cost for the value."**

Consistency is **hierarchical**: "Be consistent" **starts locally** — file conventions > team > larger project > overall codebase; the guides include rules that explicitly **defer to local conventions** (e.g., `const` usage). But internal consistency isn't always enough — **should you match the external community?** (Counting Spaces: Google Python used 2-space indents to match C++, but Python engineers read/write Python far more than C++, so that rationale collapsed; Starlark adopted the external 4-space standard. CamelCase: early Python used CamelCase to match C++; as standalone Python apps grew and code went open source, it switched to PEP 8's snake_case.) **Once time and scale are factors, adhering to the widely accepted external standard usually pays off.**

- **④ Avoid error-prone and surprising constructs:** complex features have non-obvious pitfalls; even if current engineers understand them, future maintainers may not. E.g., Python avoids reflection (`hasattr()`/`getattr()`) — accessing attributes via strings makes "which fields are accessed" opaque, hard to test/verify, even a hidden security flaw. **We need *all* engineers able to work in the codebase, not just experts** (an SRE debugging an outage jumps into any suspect code, even in an unfamiliar language).
- **⑤ Concede to practicalities:** Emerson: "**a foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds.**" Consistency is the goal, not a blindfold. **Performance** matters (C++ bans exceptions but allows `noexcept`, which can trigger compiler optimizations); **interoperability** matters (allow `snake_case` for std-mimicking types, multiple inheritance for Windows, generated code exempt). **Consistency is vital; adaptation is key.**

### The Style Guide (what goes in)
The core claim: all rules fall into three categories:
- **Rules to avoid dangers:** technical "must/must not" rules for language features — static members, lambdas, exceptions, threading, access control, inheritance; calling out the hard-to-use and hard-to-use-correctly. Each ruling includes the weighed pros/cons and the reasoning.
- **Rules to enforce best practices:** when/where to comment (switch fall-through, empty catch, template metaprogramming), source-file structure, naming (packages/classes/functions/variables). Many formatting rules live here (whitespace, line length, brace alignment), some deferred to autoformatters (gofmt, dartfmt). Includes **limits on new, not-yet-understood features** — install safety fences first, watch waiver requests and usage patterns, then loosen once enough examples generalize good from bad (**`std::unique_ptr` case study**: initially disallowed in C++11 because move semantics were unfamiliar, with tooling to catch it; later, once engineers adjusted and it clearly served the readability goal at call sites, it was allowed — a worthwhile trade-off).
- **Rules to ensure consistency:** lots of "small stuff" (naming, indentation, import ordering) with no measurable technical winner — exactly what the community debates endlessly (**bikeshedding** / Parkinson's law of triviality). **Pick one and drop out of the debate cycle**; the important bit isn't what you chose but *that* you chose.
- **And for everything else:** much is deliberately left out (don't be clever, don't fork, don't reinvent the wheel) — guides can't take a novice to mastery; some things are intentionally assumed.

### Changing the Rules
The core claim: guides aren't static; as time passes and conditions change (a new language version, a rule people invest effort circumventing, enforcement tooling becoming too burdensome), reconsider. **Rules are backed by evidence** — adding one means discussing pros/cons and consequences and verifying it fits Google's scale, and **recording the reasoning** so you can later recognize when to change.

- **The Process:** **solution-based** — a proposal must identify a *real* problem **proven by patterns in existing Google code** (not hypotheticals), then propose a fix. Because the old decision's reasoning is recorded, you can reevaluate whether a different conclusion now fits. Most changes start with **community discussion** (language-specific style mailing lists), filtered by feedback, then sent for final approval.
- **The Style Arbiters:** each language's guide is owned by senior language experts who make final calls. Decisions are **a discussion of engineering trade-offs, not personal preference** — "nothing is 'because I think it should be'; everything is a trade-off evaluation." The C++ arbiter group is **4 people** (even! but since decisions are by **consensus, not voting**, ties don't matter).
- **Exceptions (waivers):** rules are law, but some cases warrant exemptions, judged by arbiters. **Waivers aren't granted lightly:** C++ macros must use a project prefix (macros are global, so collisions must be prevented); a request to drop the prefix just for length or project consistency is **rejected — codebase integrity outweighs project consistency.** Exceptions are allowed when rule-breaking is more beneficial (e.g., implicit conversion for transparent wrapper types).

### Guidance (the "shoulds" beyond the "musts")
The core claim: beyond rules, Google curates **guidance** — "rules are the musts, guidance is the shoulds." It focuses on what people frequently get wrong and what's new and confusing.
- **Primers:** guides are **prescriptive** (which features are allowed/forbidden); primers are **descriptive** (how to use the endorsed features) — the new engineer's go-to reference.
- **C++ Tips of the Week (TotW):** grown from **real problems encountered** (not abstract ideals), each a few minutes' read; not canon, but their broad, direct applicability makes them a "**canon of the common.**" Hugely successful internally, frequently cited in reviews.
- **`<Language>@Google 101` courses:** full-day courses on what's *different* about the language inside Google's codebase — common libraries, idioms, in-house tools — turning a good engineer into a good *Google-codebase* engineer.

### Applying the Rules
The core claim: rules are valuable only if **enforceable** — enforce **socially** (teaching, training, code review / readability, see [[ch03]]) or **technically** (tooling). For compliance checking, **strongly prefer automated technical enforcement** for three reasons:
- **It doesn't decay with time or scale:** new people don't know all the rules, rules change, projects grow new features; memory and docs both fail, but tooling in sync with rules guarantees coverage.
- **It minimizes interpretation variance:** a tool validates all inputs against one unchanging definition, **leaving no entry point for human bias.**
- **It scales:** doubling the company barely changes enforcement cost (one expert team's tools serve everyone).

But **not all rules can be automated:** some **need human judgment** ("avoid complicated template metaprogramming," "composition is often more appropriate than inheritance"), and some are **social rules** unwise to solve technically (e.g., **CL size** — favor small changes for easier review and fewer bugs, but "small" is nebulous: a one-line change across hundreds of files may be easier to review than a 20-line one with complex side effects; so there's **no auto-reject line limit** — reviewers use discretion). **For technical rules, prefer technical enforcement whenever feasible.**

- **Error Checkers:** many usage rules are enforceable by static analysis — a mid-2018 informal survey estimated ≈ **90%** of C++ rules are auto-verifiable. Surfacing warnings (often with suggested fixes) in the workflow (e.g., at code review) minimizes compliance cost — when tooling began flagging deprecated functions with in-place fixes, **new usages disappeared almost overnight.** Tools: clang-tidy (C++), Error Prone (Java) (see [[ch20]]).
- **Code Formatters:** automated checkers/formatters enforce consistent formatting, so "line lengths stopped being interesting." **"The robots are better on average than the humans by a significant amount"** (a few cases like matrices favor humans). Enforced via **presubmit checks** — before submit, a service checks whether running the formatter produces a diff; if so, the submit is rejected with fix instructions. Tools: clang-format (C++), a yapf wrapper (Python), gofmt (Go), dartfmt (Dart), buildifier (BUILD).
- **Case Study — gofmt (Sameer Ajmani):** Go was open-sourced 2009-11-10 with **gofmt from day one** (knowing it'd be nearly impossible to retrofit a standard format after release). Motivation: reviews wasted too much time arguing format; standardizing also paved the way for tools that rewrite Go without spurious diffs (gofix upgraded pre-1.0 code to 1.0, with diffs showing only the important bits). Impact: **gofmt has no config knobs and rarely changes**, every editor/IDE uses it, so nearly all Go code is formatted identically — users went from complaining about the forced standard to citing gofmt as a reason they *like* Go. **Retrofitting:** in 2012, buildifier reformatted all of Google's BUILD files — one engineer took **6 weeks** to get **200,000 BUILD files** accepted by code owners (while 1,000+ new BUILD files were added weekly), accelerated by nascent LSC infrastructure (see [[ch22]]).

Takeaway: upgrading rules from "watched by humans" to "blocked by tools" is the only scalable way to keep them consistently enforced across 30,000 people and decades.

## Key terms

- **Rules vs Guidance** — rules are enforceable "musts" (laws); guidance is advisory "shoulds" (best practices). The chapter's foundational split.
- **Style guide** — the full set of conventions governing code (not just formatting), tailored per language; the authoritative source engineers are held accountable to.
- **Pull their weight** — a rule's benefit must exceed the cost of everyone learning and maintaining it; self-evident things, or things only one or two get wrong, shouldn't be rules.
- **Optimize for the reader** — code is read more than written; "simple to read" beats "simple to write"; leave intent evidence in place (`override`, `unique_ptr`), aiming for local reasoning.
- **Hierarchy of consistency** — "be consistent" starts locally: file > team > project > overall codebase; once time and scale matter, consider matching the external community.
- **Expert chunking** — consistency lets experts see code as meaningful chunks (like chess configurations) and zero in fast.
- **Bikeshedding** — Parkinson's law of triviality: endless debate over things with no technical winner (indentation, line length). A rule's value is "pick one" to escape the cycle.
- **Style arbiters** — the per-language guide owners who decide rule changes by trade-off consensus (not voting, not preference); the C++ group is four people.
- **Concede to practicalities** — consistency isn't blind; performance (`noexcept`) and interoperability (`snake_case`, Windows multiple inheritance, generated-code exemptions) warrant exceptions. "Consistency is vital; adaptation is key."
- **Presubmit check / automated enforcement** — auto-validate compliance with a formatter/checker before submit, rejecting otherwise. Technical enforcement doesn't decay, removes bias, and scales (≈ 90% of C++ rules auto-verifiable).

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Dig up your team's last code-review argument about "format/naming/style." Was it pure **bikeshedding** (no technical winner)? Could you **automate** it with a formatter + presubmit so it "stops being interesting" forever?
2. Apply "**rules must pull their weight**" to your rule list: which rules only one or two people get wrong, yet burden everyone's memory? Which "rules" should be demoted to "guidance"?
3. Does your code "optimize for the reader"? Take a piece you wrote that felt "clever/concise" — can a colleague six months later (or an SRE debugging at 3am) read the intent **at the call site**, or must they dig into the implementation (no local reasoning)?
4. Apply the **hierarchy of consistency**: where do your "local conventions" clash with company/external standards? When would you match the external community (like Python's CamelCase → snake_case), and when hold internal consistency? Per the Time + Scale themes.
5. Inventory your **human-watched** rules: which *can and should* be automated (error checker/formatter/presubmit)? Which *should* stay human-judged (judgment-requiring or social, like CL size)? Are you accidentally "solving a social problem with a technical solution"? Per [[ch20]].

# Chapter 1 — What Is Software Engineering?

> *"Software engineering is programming integrated over time."*
> In plain terms: software engineering = programming + the dimension of time.

## Summary

This chapter is the book's **thesis**. It answers a question that sounds basic but is constantly muddled: how is *software engineering* different from *programming*? The author, Titus Winters, gives three discriminators — **time, scale, and trade-offs**. Programming is the act of producing code right now. Software engineering is what programming becomes once you add (1) the *time* over which the code is expected to live and be maintained, (2) the *scale* of people and organization producing it, and (3) the pressure of making *high-stakes decisions* under imperfect information. Together those create a whole apparatus of policies, practices, and tools.

The central concept is **sustainability**: your project is sustainable if, for the expected life span of your code, you are *able* to react to any valuable change — technical or business. The key word is *capability*, not obligation; you may choose not to upgrade, but you must be able to. Short-lived code is essentially untouched by time and can be "good enough." But for systems that live for years or decades, almost every dependency will eventually change, and the gap between "it happens to work" and "it is maintainable" gets magnified without bound by time. This chapter installs the mental framework that the remaining 24 chapters reuse.

## Why read this

- **You're upgrading from "can code" to "can engineer."** The most common L4 blind spot is treating "it runs on my machine" as *done*. This chapter hands you a ruler — **the expected life span of the code** — that redefines what *done* even means.
- **You make technical decisions for your team but can't articulate the reasoning.** The chapter decomposes "cost" into six kinds and gives one hard rule: a decision is justified only because it's *mandatory* or because it's *the best option given current evidence* — **never "because I said so."** That's shared vocabulary for managing up and mentoring.
- **You want to predict which practices are "fine now, explode at scale."** Hyrum's Law, non-scaling deprecation policies, branch proliferation — the chapter teaches the "imagine the org 10× / 100× bigger" thought experiment to spot costs that grow super-linearly *before* they bite.
- **It is the map for the whole book.** Time / scale / trade-offs run through every later chapter; understand this one and everything else has a hook to hang on.

## Key details

### Time and Change
The core claim: adding the axis of *time* is what turns programming into software engineering. A clarifying question is, **"What is the expected life span of your code?"** Reasonable answers span a factor of roughly **100,000** — from "runs once, lives minutes" to "lives for decades." Short-lived code is largely immune to time (no need to chase new OS, library, or language versions). As life span grows, **change shifts from negligible to dominant.** Somewhere between a one-off script and a decade-long project there is a transition point where the project *must* start reacting to external change — and any project that didn't plan for upgrades from day one finds that first crossing brutally painful (more baked-in hidden assumptions, engineers with no upgrade experience, and several years of upgrades crammed into one).

Engineering takeaway: sustainability is not "never change" — it is "**able to change, affordably.**" Getting through that first big upgrade *and* reaching the point where you can reliably stay current is the essence of long-term sustainability.

### Hyrum's Law
The core claim: **with a sufficient number of users of an API, it does not matter what you promise in the contract — every observable behavior of your system will be depended on by somebody.** The author likens it to entropy: you can mitigate it, never eradicate it. Given enough time and users, even the most innocuous change breaks *something*; so the value analysis of any change must include the cost of investigating, locating, and fixing those breakages.

Engineering takeaway: turn it from a slogan into routine risk assessment — your published contract defines the freedom you *want* to keep, but real-world change difficulty depends on which "unpromised but observable" behaviors users actually rely on.

### Example: Hash Ordering
The core claim: hash-container iteration order is the textbook case of Hyrum's Law. An expert asked "can I assume a fixed output order?" says "no" — but the *nuanced* answer is: **fine if your code is short-lived and the environment is frozen; wrong if you don't know the life span or can't promise your dependencies won't change.** Even if *you* don't depend on order, serializing into an RPC response can make the *caller* depend on it. Some people even use hash order as a cheap random-number generator — so removing the randomness would break *them*.

Engineering takeaway: this is the smallest example of "it works" vs. "it is correct." The author's mnemonic: **"It's programming if 'clever' is a compliment, but it's software engineering if 'clever' is an accusation."**

### Why Not Just Aim for "Nothing Changes"?
The core claim: can you simply avoid change? — *it depends.* Pure C with no external dependencies (or only ultra-stable ones like POSIX) might dodge refactors; but most projects are exposed to shifting underlying technology. Security disclosures (Heartbleed, Meltdown, Spectre) and hardware evolution (old algorithms become inefficient on new CPUs) make "assume nothing will ever change" a high-risk bet.

Engineering takeaway: **changing for its own sake is wrong, but being *unable* to change is worse.** Change isn't inherently good — but the *capability* to change is mandatory, and worth investing in to make cheap. Like a sysadmin who knows "in theory I can restore from tape" vs. actually knowing how and what it costs.

### Scale and Efficiency
The core claim: the other face of software engineering is scale. **Everything your organization relies on to produce and maintain code must scale in cost and resources — and in particular, everything you do *repeatedly* must scale in human effort.** Not just the software (CPU/memory/storage/bandwidth) but the development workflow, test clusters, version control, and build systems. If any grows super-linearly, you eventually hit a wall. Many such problems are "boiled-frog": they worsen slowly and never announce themselves as a single crisis.

### Policies That Don't / Do Scale
The core claim: spot a bad policy by imagining the org 10× / 100× bigger and asking whether one engineer's burden grows proportionally.
- **Doesn't scale:** the traditional deprecation move ("we delete the old Widget on Aug 15th, migrate yourselves") pushes cost onto every downstream user; the dev-branch reflex ("merges are destabilizing, so merge less") leads to branch explosion and a resync/test tax for everyone.
- **Scales well:** the **Churn Rule** — infrastructure teams migrate their internal users themselves (or update in place, backward-compatibly), so a few experts learn the problem deeply once and apply it to every sub-problem. **Expertise scales better than spreading the work across everyone.** And the **Beyoncé Rule**: "If you liked it, you should have put a CI test on it" — tests not in the shared CI don't count, or the infra engineer would have to chase down every team to learn how to run their tests.

### Example: Compiler Upgrade
The core claim: in theory a compiler upgrade should be cheap (languages work hard at backward compatibility); in practice it's subtle and painful. Google's legendary **2006** upgrade — five years without upgrading, thousands of engineers, most never having done one — was extremely painful. But the lesson isn't the difficulty; it's that *once they recognized the task was necessary*, they used automation, consistency, and expertise to make upgrades doable with a **constant number of engineers even as the codebase grew.** Factors affecting upgrade ease: expertise, release stability, conformity, familiarity, and policy (e.g., the Beyoncé Rule). Stagnation is an option — usually an unwise one (≈25% extra compute cost and security exposure).

### Shifting Left
The core claim: on the developer-workflow timeline (conception → design → implementation → review → testing → commit → canary → production), **the earlier ("more to the left") you catch a problem, the cheaper it is to fix.** The phrase comes from security ("shift left on security"). Bugs caught by static analysis and code review before commit are far cheaper than bugs that reach production. Since no single tool is perfect, use **defense in depth** to catch as many defects on the left as possible.

### Trade-offs and Costs
The core claim: once you understand programming, life span, and scale, what remains is *making good decisions.* "Cost" is more than money — it spans **financial, resource (CPU time), personnel (engineering effort), transaction (cost to act), opportunity (cost of *not* acting), and societal** costs. In a high-creativity, high-reward field, the limiting factor is usually **personnel cost, not money** — focus and productivity vary so much that a 10–20% swing is easy to imagine. A decision is legitimate only when it's *mandatory* or *the best option on current evidence* — not "because I said so." Aim for consensus, not unanimity.

### Examples: Markers / Distributed Builds
- **Whiteboard markers:** most companies hoard them like treasure, so meetings stall for lack of a working pen. Google stocks open supply closets — an explicit trade-off: **frictionless brainstorming matters far more than preventing someone walking off with a few markers.**
- **Distributed builds:** Google built its own system; the net savings vastly outweighed the build-and-maintain cost. But there's no fairy-tale ending — once engineers no longer *felt* the pain of slow builds, dependency bloat lost its constraint (an echo of **Jevons' paradox**: efficiency gains can *increase* consumption). Even a simple "spend compute to save engineer-time" trade had unforeseen downstream effects.

### Revisiting Decisions & SWE vs. Programming
The core claim: a hidden benefit of a data-driven culture is the ability *and* necessity of admitting mistakes. Data changes over time; long-lived projects must be able to change direction after the initial call, and deciders must have the *right* to admit error — **leaders who admit mistakes are respected more, not less.** Finally the author clarifies: software engineering is not "better" than programming; they are **two different problem domains** with distinct constraints and best practices. A few-day project needs no integration tests, CD, or SemVer; a decades-long one can't live without them. Using the *right* tool is the point.

## Key terms

- **Software Engineering vs. Programming** — Programming = producing code now. Software engineering = the policies, practices, and tools needed to keep that code useful for as long as needed *and* to enable team collaboration. The difference is dimensionality: time, scale, trade-offs.
- **Sustainability** — Being *able*, for the code's expected life span, to safely respond to every change you ought to respond to. About capability, not forcing every upgrade.
- **Hyrum's Law (Law of Implicit Dependencies)** — With enough users, all observable API behaviors get depended on. The core risk factor in assessing change difficulty; entropy-like — mitigable, not eradicable.
- **Expected Life Span** — How long code is expected to be built, run, and maintained. Reasonable answers span ~100,000×; life span decides whether "good enough" or "clean and maintainable" is appropriate.
- **Sublinear Scaling** — As org and codebase grow, the *human* cost of repeated work must grow sub-linearly; super-linear policies eventually fail.
- **The Beyoncé Rule** — "Put a CI test on it." Tests outside shared CI don't count; breakage blame falls on whoever didn't write the test, keeping infra change scalable.
- **Churn Rule** — Infra teams migrate their own internal users instead of pushing cost downstream; experts handle it centrally, which scales better.
- **Shifting Left** — Catch problems earlier in the workflow to lower fix cost; pair with defense in depth.
- **Cost (six kinds)** — Financial, resource, personnel, transaction, opportunity, societal. The limiting factor in software engineering is usually personnel, not money.
- **Boiled Frog Problem** — Inefficiency and cost worsen slowly and never spike into a single crisis, so they're ignored; only org-wide scaling awareness keeps them in check.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Take a system you maintain and honestly estimate its **expected life span.** Do your testing, dependency-management, and upgrade strategies match it? Where has "good enough" planted a long-term landmine?
2. Apply **Hyrum's Law**: which "unpromised but already-depended-on" observable behaviors does your service have (output order, timing, error-message format, performance characteristics)? Which would be most dangerous to change?
3. Run the "org 10× bigger" thought experiment on one of your team's processes (releases, access approvals, dependency upgrades). Does it grow **sub-** or **super-linearly**? If super-linear, can "centralized expertise" or automation fix it?
4. Recall a recent major technical decision. Was it "mandatory" or "best option on current evidence" — or did "everyone does it this way / because I said so" sneak in? Can you list the six costs?
5. Does your team culture allow **publicly admitting a wrong decision**? If not, what incentive (blame culture, ego) blocks it? Compare with the blameless post-mortem in [[ch02]].

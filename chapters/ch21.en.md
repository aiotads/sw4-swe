# Chapter 21 — Dependency Management

> *"Prefer source control problems to dependency management problems."*
> If you can pull a problem inside your organization and make it a source-control problem, do — it's far cheaper.

## Summary

**Dependency management** — managing the network of libraries, packages, and dependencies *you don't control* — is one of the **least understood and most challenging** problems in software engineering. Its closest relative is **version control**, and the line between them is one question: "**Does our organization control the development/update/management of this subproject?**" Hence the chapter's strongest single piece of advice: **all else being equal, prefer source control problems over dependency-management problems** — broadening "organization" (the whole company, not one team) is often a good trade-off.

The core difficulty is **thinking in terms of networks, not single dependencies.** The canonical symptom is the **diamond dependency**: libbase is used by liba and libb, both used by libuser (your code); if libbase makes an incompatible change and liba upgrades while libb hasn't, **there's no general way to put it all together.** Languages tolerate this differently (Java can "shade" by renaming, C++ has near-zero tolerance due to the One Definition Rule), but **once types are passed between dependencies, all tricks fail.**

**Importing dependencies** is nearly free in "programming" (reuse beats reimplementing), but in "software engineering" (mindful of time and maintenance) it has hidden costs. Providers should be clear about their **compatibility promise**: the C++ standard library (near-indefinite backward + ABI compatibility; SD-8), Go (source-compatible, no binary), **Abseil** (no ABI promise, but "no breaking API change without an automated refactoring tool"), **Boost** (no version-to-version promise — an experimental proving ground). Google's `third_party` story (Alice/Bob) shows: **"I got it to work" ≠ "this works in a supported fashion"** — that's Hyrum's Law.

The four theoretical models: **① Nothing Changes (static — the right starting point for most new orgs, but false over a long enough time), ② SemVer (the de facto standard, with a SAT-solver; failure to solve is "dependency hell"), ③ Bundled Distribution (like a Linux distro, where a distributor finds a compatible set), and ④ Live at Head (always depend on the latest, unpin, rely on CI and automated refactoring tools — the dependency extension of trunk-based development).** The chapter dissects **SemVer's limitations** (it's an *estimate* not a *proof*; it can both overconstrain and overpromise; the incentives are wrong; **MVS, Minimum Version Selection**, raises fidelity) and proposes an "infinite resources" thought experiment — replacing maintainer compatibility estimates with **evidence from running downstream tests.** Finally, **exporting dependencies** (the gflags and AppEngine cautionary tales): external APIs cost far more to maintain than internal ones.

## Why read this

- **You're repeatedly tormented by diamond dependencies, version conflicts, "dependency hell."** The chapter lifts it from "a single dependency" to "a **dependency network**," explaining why conflicting requirements are the core difficulty and why language tricks (shading) save functions but not types. You'll be readier to **pull problems inside the org and turn them into source-control problems.**
- **You don't know whether or how to bring in an external dependency.** The chapter gives Google's checklist (tests? passing? who provides it? compatibility promise? how long? how often breaking?) and uses the Alice/Bob `third_party` story to drive home: **importing a dependency isn't free — it's a contract requiring long-term maintenance.**
- **You treat SemVer as absolute truth.** The chapter exposes it as "a **human's lossy estimate of risk**": it can overconstrain (works fine, but blocked) and overpromise (a patch still breaks someone via Hyrum's Law). Knowing this, you'll choose dependencies and design version strategy more soberly (and meet **MVS** and "evidence over estimates").
- **You're considering open-sourcing/releasing your team's code.** The gflags and AppEngine lessons warn you: **"throw it over the wall and forget" costs reputation, and an unplanned release becomes a tax on engineering efficiency**; "don't release something you don't have a plan (and a mandate) to support long-term."

## Key details

### Why Is Dependency Management So Difficult?
The core claim: many half-baked solutions focus on too narrow a problem ("how do I import one package?") — the real trick is **managing a network of dependencies and its changes over time.** Some nodes are needed by your first-party code, some are only pulled in transitively; over a long enough period all get new versions, some updates matter. **Conflicting requirements and diamond dependencies** are central: trouble arises when two nodes have conflicting requirements and you depend on both.
- **Diamond dependency:** libbase is used by liba and libb, both used by libuser; an incompatible libbase change → liba on the new version, libb on the old → libuser can't reconcile (needs three layers).
- **Language tolerance:** Java can **shade** (rename to isolate versions); C++ has near-zero tolerance (the **One Definition Rule** → UB). But **once types pass between dependencies** (a libbase-v1 map passed to a libbase-v2 API), all renaming tricks fail — "partial solutions at best."
- **The only easy answer:** skip versions forward/backward to find something compatible; otherwise patch locally (especially hard, since the discoverer rarely knows both sides). **Policy and technology for dependency management all boil down to: "How do we avoid conflicting requirements while still allowing change among noncoordinating groups?"**

### Importing Dependencies
The core claim: reuse beats reimplementing (don't make every novice rewrite a JSON parser). But **once time matters** there's a trade-off: avoiding a development cost doesn't make importing right — there's an ongoing maintenance cost (security bugs, platform shifts, evolving networks can force an upgrade regardless of intent).
- **Compatibility promises (they vary):** the **C++ standard library** = near-indefinite backward compatibility (incl. ABI; SD-8 lists the few allowed changes); **Go** = source-compatible, no binary; **Abseil** = no ABI promise, but "**no breaking API change without an automated refactoring tool**" (~250M lines of C++ depend on it, so it doesn't change APIs lightly but must be able to); **Boost** = no version-to-version promise (an experimental proving ground with fundamentally different goals). **These are software-engineering issues, not programming issues** — you can embed promise-free Boost deep in a critical system and it works *today*; the issue is "how it changes over time."
- **Ask before importing (Google's checklist):** Does it have tests? Passing? Who provides it (reputation worth investigating)? What compatibility does it aspire to? What usage is supported? How popular? How long will we depend on it? How often does it make breaking changes? Plus internally: how complex to build ourselves? What incentives to keep it updated? Who upgrades? How hard?
- **How Google does it ("we could do better"):** the vast majority of dependencies are **internal** — so most "dependency management" is really source control (by design). External projects go in the monorepo's `third_party`. **The Alice/Bob story:** Alice, rushing a demo, downloads an OSS package and follows a simple checklist (builds with our system, no existing version, two OWNERS), neither knowing anything about the implementation. It becomes available to other teams; Charlie embeds it deep in Search infrastructure — **moving from innocuous to critical with no signal to Charlie.** One day a security flaw forces a critical upgrade: Bob's now a manager, Alice has changed teams, no one updated OWNERS, thousands depend on it transitively, no one knows the implementation — **a costly, difficult upgrade** (worse for spanning many small releases). `third_party` policies fail for this common scenario.

### Dependency Management, In Theory
The core claim: a good solution must be **flexible with time and scale** — can't assume any node is indefinitely stable, nor that no new dependencies are added. Avoiding conflicting requirements is good; doing so without assuming version stability, fan-out, cross-org coordination, or huge compute is great. Four common models:
- **① Nothing Changes (the static model):** never change (only no-break bug fixes). Not ideal (assumes indefinite stability), but **it's where every org starts and is probably right for most new orgs** — until you've proven the project will live long enough that change is necessary. Downside: false over a long enough time, with no clear warning of how long. No version selection.
- **② SemVer:** the de facto standard — `major.minor.patch` (major = breaking, minor = strictly additive, patch = low-risk fixes); requirements usually as "≥." Viewing the network as nodes + requirement edges, finding a compatible set is a **SAT-solver** problem (SemVer constraints on a network are **NP-complete**); no solution is colloquially **"dependency hell."**
- **③ Bundled Distribution:** a **distributor** gathers dependencies, finds a compatible set, and releases the collection as one unit (a Linux distro — not guaranteed to be cut from one point in time). For an outside user, "I depend on RedHat version N" instead of "72 libraries at these versions." Version selection is done by distributors.
- **④ Live at Head:** pushed by some at Google, theoretically sound but costly — the **dependency extension of trunk-based development.** Presupposes you **unpin dependencies, drop SemVer, and rely on providers to test against the whole ecosystem before committing.** Always depend on the latest, and never change anything in a way hard for dependents to adapt; unintended API/behavior changes get caught by downstream CI and shouldn't be committed; when a break must happen (security), update downstream first or **provide an automated tool to upgrade in place** (essential for closed-source downstream). Version selection = "the most recent stable version of everything."

### The Limitations of SemVer
The core claim: is a dotted-triple a **promise or an estimate**? An estimate — you can't prove compatibility from the source API alone; you must know *what* you're asking compatibility with. Treating estimates and self-attestation as absolute is a shaky foundation:
- **It can overconstrain:** if libbase has only Foo and Bar, and liba/libb use only Foo, a breaking change to Bar forces a major bump, so the SAT-solver refuses to let liba/libb use the new version — **even though they're perfectly compatible** (only the unused Bar changed). → "Can't update" even when ignoring SemVer would work, a major source of dependency hell.
- **It can overpromise:** it assumes changes fall into three buckets (breaking/additive/non-API). But where does a 1ms delay, a logging-format change, an import-order change, or a reordered "unordered" stream go? **Hyrum's Law**: "with enough users, every observable behavior of your system will be depended upon by someone" — so "safe" patch changes inevitably break someone's assumptions at scale. **"Breaking change" itself is misleading**: there are theoretically-breaking-but-safe changes (removing an unused API) and theoretically-safe-but-breaking ones (the Hyrum's Law examples). Whether a change is breaking **can only be evaluated in the context of how it's used** — information absent from the SemVer formulation.
- **Motivations:** maintainers bear only a fraction of a version bump's cost (most users are indirect, not subscribed to notifications), so the systemic incentive doesn't favor "don't break." **Go/Clojure do it justice**: a major bump is a whole new package (if you'll break backward compatibility, why pretend it's the same API?). Plus human fallibility — semantic changes need bumping as much as syntactic ones, but "behavior change vs bug fix" is computationally infeasible to discern.
- **Minimum Version Selection (MVS):** Russ Cox's variation — if liba requires libbase ≥1.7, use **1.7** (even if 1.8 exists). This "produces high-fidelity builds as close as possible to what the author developed against" — because requiring ≥1.7 almost certainly means the developer had 1.7 installed (anecdotal interop evidence). **Take the smallest step forward** (like committing an hour's work rather than dumping a year's) — a bit more practical fidelity than SemVer, without fixing its theoretical/incentive problems.
- **Does SemVer work?** At small scale, yes, provided: **providers are accurate and responsible, dependencies are fine-grained, and all API usage is within the expected.** But Google's experience suggests **you can't have all three at scale and keep them so** — compounded fidelity loss dominates, yielding both false positives (theoretically incompatible but works) and false negatives (compatible but blocked = dependency hell).

### Dependency Management with Infinite Resources
The core claim: a thought experiment — what if we had infinite compute? The industry uses SemVer for three reasons (needs only local info, doesn't assume tests/CI, is the existing practice), but none is truly necessary: dependency networks form in only two environments (within one org, within OSS), both with downstream-usage info available. SemVer's intent is "I estimate this change is easy (or not) to adopt" — there's a **better way: evidence** (run the downstream tests against a proposed change). If OSS moved to "changes accompanied by evidence of safety," compatibility truth comes from **running affected downstream tests** (prioritized by usage/maintenance/history, shifting the goal from "never break anyone" to a statistical "risk mitigation"). The needed changes are many: all dependencies have unit tests, the network (incl. reverse edges) is queryable, CI compute is available, dependencies aren't pinned, and CI factors in history/reputation. Simplified, **this is effectively the Live at Head model.**

### Exporting Dependencies
The core claim: releasing software as a dependency has two losses: **① reputation** (poorly done/unmaintained drags on the org — "community over code") and **② a tax on engineering efficiency** (out of sync; given time, all forks get expensive).
- **gflags (reputation loss):** open-sourcing a C++ flags library in 2006 was a good deed, but "we couldn't do large-scale refactoring then, couldn't move the code, and accepting external contributions is a legal issue" doomed it to "throw over the wall" or a disconnected fork; as authors left and priorities shifted, internal and external versions diverged, and it was eventually forked externally. **"Don't release things without a plan (and a mandate) to support them long-term."** Later, C++ library teams tweaked "observable-but-undocumented" internal flag details, and the few projects relying on internal/external fork equivalence broke — a multi-thousand-CPU optimization was significantly delayed, all due to Hyrum's Law (even for forked APIs across organizations).
- **AppEngine (a more serious risk):** in 2014, upgrading the Python runtime alongside the C++ compiler/stdlib effectively tied "code built with the new C++ compiler" to "code using the new Python." But many **paying customers** couldn't/wouldn't update (didn't want the new Python, or couldn't afford the 32→64-bit resource change), and since big customers paid significantly, AppEngine delayed the forced switch — forcing **every piece of C++ in AppEngine's transitive closure to stay compatible with the old compiler and stdlib for nearly three years.**

Takeaway (conclusion): **releasing an API exposes you to outsiders' competing priorities and unforeseen constraints** (especially those paying you). "With enough users, any observable of your system will be depended upon by someone" — inside Google, the monorepo and code indexing keep change possible; once you lose visibility or face external competing priorities, pure engineering trade-offs get much harder. **External users cost far more to maintain than internal ones**; when deciding whether to release, be aware of the long-term risk.

## Key terms

- **Dependency management** — managing the network of libraries/packages/dependencies you don't control; one of the least understood, most challenging problems in software engineering.
- **Source control vs dependency management** — the line is "does our org control this subproject"; prefer source-control problems (easier, cheaper).
- **Diamond dependency** — libbase used by liba/libb, both used by libuser; an incompatible libbase change makes two paths require different versions, irreconcilable.
- **Shading / One Definition Rule** — Java can rename to isolate versions; C++ has near-zero tolerance (ODR); but all tricks fail once types pass between dependencies.
- **Compatibility promise** — C++ stdlib (near-indefinite + ABI + SD-8), Go (source, no binary), Abseil (breaking API change requires an automated refactoring tool), Boost (no promise — an experimental proving ground).
- **third_party / "works ≠ supported"** — Google's directory for external dependencies; the Alice/Bob story shows importing is a long-term maintenance contract (Hyrum's Law).
- **Nothing Changes (static model)** — never change; the right starting point for most new orgs, but false over a long enough time.
- **SemVer** — major/minor/patch + "≥" + a SAT-solver; no solution = dependency hell; constraints on a network are NP-complete.
- **Bundled distribution** — a distributor finds a compatible set and releases it as one unit (a Linux distro); "I depend on version X" instead of N libraries.
- **Live at Head** — unpin, drop SemVer, providers test against the whole ecosystem before committing, with automated refactoring tools on breaks; the dependency extension of trunk-based development.
- **MVS (Minimum Version Selection)** — require ≥1.7 → use 1.7 (not 1.8); high-fidelity, close to what the author tested; take the smallest step.
- **Exporting dependencies** — the two losses of releasing externally: reputation and a tax on engineering efficiency; external users cost far more than internal (the gflags, AppEngine lessons).

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Was your last dependency conflict / "dependency hell" a **diamond dependency**? Could you pull it from a "dependency-management problem" into a "source-control problem" (broaden "organization," or vendor it in)? Per [[ch16]], [[ch18]]'s One-Version Rule.
2. Apply Google's checklist to a dependency you recently imported: tests? who provides it, what compatibility promise? how long will you depend, who upgrades, how hard? Has it, like Alice/Bob's `third_party`, **quietly gone from innocuous to critical** with no one maintaining it?
3. Do you treat SemVer as a "promise" or an "estimate"? Recall a "version said compatible but it broke" (overpromise/Hyrum's Law) or "works fine but the SAT-solver blocked it" (overconstrain) — is your dependency strategy prepared for both fidelity losses? Does **MVS** apply?
4. Of the four models (Nothing Changes / SemVer / Bundled / Live at Head), where is your project and where should it be? How long will it live — long enough that "Nothing Changes will become false"? Do you have the tests and CI to support a Live at Head direction? Per [[ch23]].
5. Are you considering **releasing** your team's code externally? Recall gflags "throw over the wall and forget" and AppEngine "paying customers wouldn't upgrade for three years" — do you have a **long-term support plan and mandate**? Are you ready to bear "external users depending on unpromised behavior via Hyrum's Law"?

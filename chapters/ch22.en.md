# Chapter 22 | Large-Scale Changes

> *"Making LSCs means making a habit of making LSCs."*
> The capability is a muscle you build by exercising it — not a one-off heroic feat.

## Summary

A **Large-Scale Change (LSC)** is any set of changes that are **logically related but cannot practically be submitted as a single atomic commit** — perhaps because it touches more files than the tooling can commit at once, because it's large enough that it would always have merge conflicts, or because your repository topology (distributed/federated repos) makes a cross-repo atomic change technically impossible. The chapter's most counterintuitive observation: **as a codebase and its engineer count grow, the largest possible atomic change shrinks** — just keeping every file current and running all affected presubmits and tests becomes harder and harder.

Since atomic changes don't scale, Google does LSCs by **divide and conquer**: automated tooling generates one giant global change, then **Rosie** shards it — along project boundaries and OWNERS rules — into **independently submittable pieces**, each running its own test→mail→submit pipeline. Testing rides the **TAP train**: many LSCs share a train and share test results to amortize cost. Culturally, Google treats these commits as **cattle, not pets**: nameless, rollback-able at any time, cheap to lose because automation can cheaply regenerate them.

Three book themes run throughout. **Scale** is central — the *human* cost of the tooling must be **sublinear** in repo size (the person-time to generate *all* required changes shouldn't balloon as the repo grows). **Time** — LSCs make "technical decisions no longer immutable" (a widely-used symbol's name, a popular class's location can be changed after the fact) and fight the **haunted graveyard**: systems so old no one dares touch them. **Trade-offs** — knowing full well that small shards rerun the same tests many times, Google deliberately accepts it because "engineer time tracking down failures is far more expensive than the compute to run extra tests." Who does this work? **Infrastructure teams** (they hold the domain knowledge, can amortize the cost, and avoid the *unfunded mandate*). The process has four phases: **Authorization → Change creation → Shard management → Cleanup**. Case studies span `scoped_ptr→std::unique_ptr` (>500,000 references), a single-character filename fix ("filling potholes"), and Operation RoseHub (>50 Googlers manually sending >2,600 patches to fix the open-source community's Mad Gadget vulnerability).

## Why read this

- **Your "largest atomic commit" is smaller than you think — and shrinking.** The opening barrage (how many files can you reliably update in one commit? could you in an emergency?) forces you to face a counterintuitive fact: **the bigger the repo and team, the lower the atomic ceiling.** The chapter hands you the full mindset and tooling view of "give up atomicity, shard instead," keeping your codebase **flexible to infrastructure change**.
- **You're stuck behind an old system no one dares touch (a haunted graveyard).** The chapter lifts the SRE mantra "No Haunted Graveyards" to the code level and offers the one antidote: **solid testing** — with tests, you can change a system of any age or complexity with confidence. This directly shapes "should I add tests, and where."
- **You want to push a better new system but expect users to migrate themselves.** The chapter explains why **organic migrations almost never finish** (engineers copy old examples; benefits are diffused so no team is motivated) and why the **infrastructure team that owns the old system** should own the migration — internalizing the externality of an unfunded mandate.
- **You treat every commit as precious.** "Cattle vs pets" reframes automated change: when changes are machine-generated en masse, an individual commit should be **a disposable head of cattle, not a pet** — don't take rollbacks personally. That's the psychological threshold for large refactoring to even work.

## Key details

### What Is a Large-Scale Change?
Core claim: an LSC is a set of changes **logically related but not practically submittable as one atomic unit** — because of too many files, because the change is big enough to always merge-conflict, or because the repo topology (distributed/federated) makes a cross-repo atomic change impossible.
- **LSCs are almost always generated by automated tooling.** Motivations fall into categories: cleaning up common antipatterns with codebase-wide analysis, replacing deprecated library features, low-level infrastructure improvements (e.g., compiler upgrades), and moving users from an old system to a new one.
- **The majority have near-zero functional impact:** widespread textual updates for clarity, optimization, or future compatibility (though they're not theoretically limited to this behavior-preserving/refactoring class).
- **Sense of scale:** at Google's size, infra teams routinely change hundreds of thousands of references; the largest cases touch **millions**. The same tools that make changing thousands of files efficient **scale down to tens of files well** — investing in LSC tooling also helps engineers making small changes.

### Who Deals with LSCs?
Core claim: **infrastructure teams** do most of the LSC execution (tools and resources are company-wide). Why not just ship a new system and order all old users to move themselves? Because it doesn't scale:
- **Domain knowledge is centralized:** the teams that build/run the underlying systems have the context to fix the references; making each consuming team relearn that expertise is globally inefficient. Centralization also enables faster recovery (errors fall into a few categories, and the migrating team can keep a playbook).
- **Nobody likes an *unfunded mandate*:** even a categorically better system has diffuse benefits, so individual teams won't migrate on their own initiative. The migration cost will be borne somewhere — centralizing and accounting for it is almost always faster and cheaper than organic migration.
- **Aligned incentives:** organic migrations rarely fully succeed (engineers copy existing code as examples). Having a team with a vested interest in removing the old system own the migration ensures it actually gets done. Funding such a team isn't an extra cost — it just internalizes the externality of an unfunded mandate, with economies of scale.

### Barriers to Atomic Changes
Core claim: in an ideal world every logical change is one atomic commit, tested/reviewed/committed independently; but as the repo and engineer count grow, that becomes infeasible (and can be infeasible even at small scale with distributed/federated repos). The barriers:
- **Technical limitations:** most VCS operations scale **linearly** with change size; small commits (tens of files) are fine, but there may not be enough memory/CPU to atomically commit thousands at once. In centralized VCSs, large commits block other writers (and, in older systems, readers). Splitting into independent chunks gets around this — at the cost of more complex execution.
- **Merge conflicts:** the larger the change, the higher the chance of conflict, compounded by the number of engineers in the repo. A small company might sneak a repo-wide change in on a quiet weekend, or pass a "global lock token"; at a global company like Google, **someone is always changing the repo**, so those tricks don't work. Fewer files → lower conflict probability → more likely to commit cleanly.
- **No Haunted Graveyards:** an SRE mantra. A haunted graveyard is a system so ancient, obtuse, or complex that **no one dares enter it** — often business-critical and frozen in time (any change might break it incomprehensibly, costing real money). They exist in codebases too: old, unmaintained, on a revenue path, wrapped in bureaucracy. **The antidote is good, old-fashioned testing** — with thorough tests you can make arbitrary changes to a system of any age and know whether they break, consigning the very notion to its own graveyard.
- **Heterogeneity:** LSCs work only when computers, not humans, do the bulk of the effort — and computers need **consistent environments** to apply transforms correctly. Many VCSs, CI systems, project-specific tooling, or formatting guidelines make sweeping changes hard. Google **embraces some consistency** (e.g., standardizing presubmit tests) and advises teams to **omit their special checks** when an LSC touches their code.
- **Testing:** every change should be tested, but bigger is harder. Google's CI runs all tests **directly impacted plus those transitively dependent** (broad coverage, but the farther a test is from the changed files, the less likely a failure is caused by the change). Small independent changes are easier to validate — finding the root cause in a 25-file change is straightforward; in a 10,000-file change it's a needle in a haystack. **The trade-off:** small shards rerun the same tests many times (especially tests depending on large parts of the codebase), but **engineer time tracking down failures is far more expensive than the extra compute**, so Google consciously makes this trade (yours may differ — worth examining).

### Case Study: Testing LSCs — Riding the TAP Train
Core claim: today a **double-digit percentage (10–20%) of changes in a project come from LSCs** — meaning a substantial amount of code is changed by people whose day job is unrelated to that project. Without good tests this work is impossible, and Google's codebase would quickly atrophy under its own weight.
- **The TAP train's core insight:** LSCs rarely interact with one another, and most affected tests pass for most LSCs. Using two facts — **① LSCs tend to be pure, narrow refactorings preserving local semantics; ② individual changes are simpler and highly scrutinized, so they're correct more often than not** — you can **test multiple changes at once and reduce total tests run**.
- **The train's five steps (started fresh every three hours):** ① for each change on the train, run a sample of **1,000 randomly-selected tests**; ② gather all that passed into one uber-change ("the train"); ③ run the **union of all tests directly affected** by the group (a large or low-level enough LSC can mean running *every* test at Google — can take >6 hours); ④ for each non-flaky failing test, **rerun it individually against each change** on the train to find the culprit; ⑤ generate a report per boarding change listing passing/failing targets — evidence that the LSC is safe to submit.
- **Code review:** LSCs still need review; large commits are tedious and error-prone to review (especially hand-generated ones — avoid). Sharding makes this much easier.

### Case Study: scoped_ptr to std::unique_ptr
Core claim: C++11 introduced `std::unique_ptr`, strictly better than Google's home-grown `scoped_ptr`, but the codebase had **over 500,000 references** to `scoped_ptr` across millions of source files — the **largest LSC attempted to that point** at Google.
- Several engineers attacked it in parallel over months, using the large-scale migration infrastructure to change references and slowly adapt `scoped_ptr` to behave more like `unique_ptr`. **At the height, they consistently generated, tested, and committed over 700 independent changes touching over 15,000 files per day**; today, with refined tooling, they sometimes hit **ten times that throughput**.
- Like almost all LSCs, it had a long tail: tracking down nuanced behavioral dependencies (another Hyrum's Law manifestation), fighting race conditions with other engineers, and uses in generated code the tooling couldn't detect — all handled manually.
- `scoped_ptr` was used as a parameter type in some widely-used APIs, making small independent changes hard. The endgame: **make `scoped_ptr` a type alias of `std::unique_ptr` → do the textual substitution between old and new alias → finally remove the old alias.**

### LSC Infrastructure
Core claim: Google invested heavily in infrastructure to make LSCs possible (tooling for creation, management, review, testing), but the **most important support has been the cultural norms and oversight that evolved around LSCs**.
- **Policies and Culture:** a monorepo + universal visibility means any engineer can edit any file and send it for review; but every edit has generation and review costs. Historically symmetric, those costs limited a single person's reach. As tooling improved, one engineer could cheaply generate many changes — and just as easily impose a burden on a huge number of reviewers. Hence a **lightweight approval process** (a committee of about a dozen, familiar with each language's nuances), whose goal is not to prohibit LSCs but to help authors produce the best ones. Culturally, code owners learned that LSCs are key to scaling engineering: **local owners understanding the change matters, but they don't hold a veto over the broader LSC.**
- **Codebase Insight:** you need large-scale analysis — textually (traditional tools) and semantically (e.g., the semantic indexer **Kythe**, answering "who calls this function?" / "which classes derive from this?"). Also compiler-based indices for AST analysis and transforms (**ClangMR, JavacFlume, Refaster** — highly parallelizable). Small changes can use perl/sed/regex/shell. **Key principle: the *human* effort of change-generation tooling must be sublinear in repo size** — it should take roughly the same person-time to generate all required changes regardless of repo size, and be comprehensive. Rule of thumb: **a change needing >500 edits is usually more efficient to do with the tools than by hand.**
- **Change Management — Rosie:** arguably the most important piece. Rosie **shards a master change into atomically-submittable pieces** and manages testing, mailing, reviewing, and committing each. It's not just a tool but an entire platform for making LSCs scale.
- **Testing / Language Support:** testing an LSC means ensuring not just that the master change is safe but that **each shard can be submitted independently and safely** (since a shard can contain arbitrary files, it runs the transitive closure of every test it might affect rather than standard project presubmits). On languages: **type aliasing and forwarding functions** are invaluable for letting existing users keep working while you migrate non-atomically; **statically typed languages are far easier to auto-change than dynamic ones** (compiler tools + strong static analysis reject invalid transforms before testing — so Python/Ruby/JS are extra hard for maintainers). **Automatic formatters (clang-format, google-java-format) are a crucial part of LSC infrastructure** — they make auto-generated changes "fit in" as if human-written; without them, large-scale automated changes would never have become the accepted status quo.

### Case Study: Operation RoseHub
Core claim: LSCs are now a large part of Google's culture and are starting to affect the broader world. In early 2017, a vulnerability in the Apache Commons library (**Mad Gadget**) let any Java app with a vulnerable version in its transitive classpath suffer remote execution — it even let a hacker encrypt the **San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency (SFMTA)** systems and shut down its operations. Because the only requirement was the wrong library somewhere in the classpath, anything depending on many GitHub open-source projects was vulnerable. Some Googlers launched their own LSC process: using **BigQuery** they identified affected projects and **sent over 2,600 patches** upgrading Commons to a fixed version. Instead of automated tools managing it, **over 50 humans** made this LSC work.

### The LSC Process (four phases)
Core claim: with the infrastructure in place, the process breaks into four (fuzzily-bounded) phases: **Authorization → Change creation → Shard management → Cleanup.** Ideally these happen after a new system/class/function is written, but **you should design the new system with a migration path in mind** (Google designs successor systems so maintainers can move users automatically).
- **Authorization:** the author fills out a brief doc explaining the reason, estimated impact (how many shards), and an FAQ; and gets "domain review" from the owners of the API being refactored. The proposal goes to an oversight committee of about a dozen. One of the most common pieces of feedback: **direct all reviews to a single "global approver"** (for mechanical LSCs, having one expert understand the change and automate its review is cheaper than every local owner reviewing). The committee is historically liberal, often approving a whole class of related changes; obvious ones can be fast-tracked. It's also the escalation/arbitration body (rarely needed). The only outright rejections have been **dangerous** (e.g., converting all NULL to nullptr) or **extremely low-value** (e.g., British↔American spelling) changes.
- **Change creation:** after approval, produce the actual edits, as automated as possible (so the parent change can be updated as users backslide into old usage or textual merge conflicts occur). For the rare case where tools can't generate it, change generation is **sharded across humans** (as in RoseHub). To preserve human readability, the output should look as human-generated as possible — hence the need for style guides and auto-formatters.
- **Sharding and Submitting — Rosie:** Rosie shards the global change by project boundaries and ownership rules into atomically-submittable pieces, then runs each through an independent test-mail-submit pipeline. It **caps outstanding shards per LSC, runs at lower priority, and negotiates acceptable load** with the rest of the infrastructure.
  - **Cattle vs Pets:** a typical handcrafted change is like raising a pet — days or weeks of polishing, intimate knowledge, pride at commit. Effective LSC handling needs high automation and produces an enormous number of changes; here, treat changes as **cattle** — nameless, rollback-able or rejectable at any time at little cost (unless the whole herd is affected). With automation, tooling can be updated and changes regenerated cheaply, so losing a few cattle is no problem. **Don't take rejection personally.**
  - **Testing (shards):** each shard runs all transitively-dependent tests through TAP, often creating high CI load. In practice **the vast majority of shards affect fewer than 1,000 tests** (of millions). For those affecting more, group them (run the union of all affected tests first, then for each shard run just the intersection of its affected tests with the first run's failures) — since the union nearly runs the whole codebase, **adding more changes to the batch is nearly free**. A drawback of running so many tests: low-probability events become near-certainties at scale — **flaky/brittle tests** are especially painful for LSC authors. Google is now more confident in a single semantic-preserving, machine-generated change than in a test with a recent flaky history — so much so that **recently flaky tests are ignored** when submitting via the automated tooling (in theory this could miss a regression caught only by a flaky→failing test, but it's rare enough to handle via human communication). Each shard must be independently committable (no interdependence, or the sharding groups dependent changes like a header and its implementation).
  - **Mailing reviewers:** Rosie uses an owners-detection service that understands **OWNERS files**, weighting each owner by their expected ability to review the shard; unresponsive owners get additional reviewers added automatically. It runs per-project precommit tools but **selectively disables certain checks** for LSCs (e.g., nonstandard change-description formatting), and **aggressively ignores presubmit failures that preexist the change** — local owners are responsible, by social contract, for keeping their codebase free of preexisting failures.
  - **Reviewing:** ideally LSCs would be reviewed as rigorously as anything else; in practice local owners trust LSC authors so much they often give only cursory review. Google evolved to **send to local owners only where their review is needed for context, not just approval**; everything else goes to a global approver, who uses **pattern-based tooling to auto-approve changes that meet expectations**, manually examining only the anomalous subset (merge conflicts, tooling malfunctions) — letting the process scale well.
  - **Submitting:** finally each change is committed (passing project precommit checks first). With Rosie, Google can effectively create/test/review/submit **thousands of changes per day** across the whole codebase — technical decisions once final (the name of a widely-used symbol, the location of a popular class) **no longer need to be final**.
- **Cleanup:** different LSCs define "done" differently (from completely removing an old system to migrating only high-value references and letting the rest disappear organically). In almost all cases you need a mechanism to **prevent reintroduction** of the removed symbol/system — Google uses the **Tricorder** framework to flag at review time when an engineer introduces a new use of a deprecated object, effectively preventing backsliding.

Engineering takeaway (conclusion): **LSCs make some technical decisions no longer immutable** — at design time you can know some decisions can be changed later, opening more possibilities; and they let core-infrastructure maintainers migrate large swaths of the codebase from old systems/language versions/idioms to new ones, keeping the codebase **consistent spatially and temporally**. All of this with **a few dozen engineers supporting tens of thousands of others**. Whatever your size, it's worth thinking about how you'd make sweeping changes — the capability gives flexibility as you scale and keeps your source malleable over time.

## Key terms

- **Large-Scale Change (LSC)** — a set of changes logically related but not submittable as one atomic unit; almost always tool-generated, usually to clean up antipatterns, replace deprecated features, upgrade infrastructure, or migrate users.
- **Atomic change** — a single indivisible change you can test/review/commit at once; the largest possible one shrinks as repo and engineer count grow.
- **Merge-conflict / linear-scaling barrier** — large changes inevitably hit a VCS's linear scaling limits and rising merge-conflict probability (compounded by engineer count).
- **Haunted graveyard** — from the SRE mantra "No Haunted Graveyards"; a critical system so old no one dares touch it, blocking migrations and upgrades; the antidote is solid testing.
- **Unfunded mandate** — externally imposed requirements without compensating resources; explains why infrastructure teams (not consuming teams) should own LSCs, internalizing the externality.
- **TAP train** — batching multiple LSCs onto one "train" to share test results and amortize cost; five steps, started every three hours, producing "safe to submit" evidence.
- **Rosie** — Google's platform that shards a master change into atomically-submittable pieces and manages each one's test-mail-submit; the core of LSC scaling.
- **Shard** — an independently-submittable small change carved out by Rosie along project/OWNERS boundaries; must be independently committable and pass its own presubmits.
- **Cattle vs pets** — a mindset contrast; an LSC's individual commit is disposable cattle, not a hand-raised pet.
- **Global approver** — an expert with repo-wide approval rights who auto-approves LSC shards with pattern-based tooling, replacing per-shard local-owner review.
- **Kythe / ClangMR / Refaster** — semantic-index and AST-level analysis/transform tools providing the codebase insight LSC tooling needs.
- **Type alias / forwarding function** — language features that let old users keep working while migrating non-atomically (`scoped_ptr→unique_ptr` transitioned via a type alias).
- **Tricorder** — a framework that flags new uses of deprecated objects at review time, preventing backsliding after an LSC cleanup.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Back to the opening barrage: **how many files can you reliably update in one atomic commit?** What constrains it (VCS capacity? merge conflicts? presubmits?)? Could you in an emergency? If that number is far smaller than your codebase, is your "divide and conquer" tooling (who's your Rosie?) ready?
2. Does your codebase have a **haunted graveyard** — a system on a critical path that's too old to touch? Is it what blocks you from upgrading compilers/libraries or removing old systems? Would you invest in tests first to free it from the graveyard? Per [[ch11]], [[ch14]].
3. When you recently pushed a better new system, did you expect **organic migration** or own the LSC yourself (as the system owner)? Recall "unfunded mandate" and "engineers copy old examples" — will organic migration actually finish in your org? Per [[ch15]].
4. Do you hold a "cattle" or a "pet" mindset toward individual LSC commits? If your automation can't yet "cheaply regenerate changes," where are you stuck — tooling (generation), testing (a TAP-train equivalent), or culture (local owners' veto and trust)?
5. Is your change-generation tooling's **human cost sublinear** in repo size? I.e., if the repo grows tenfold, does the person-time to generate all required changes balloon? Where should the ">500 edits → learn the tools" rule of thumb sit for your team? Per [[ch17]], [[ch20]].

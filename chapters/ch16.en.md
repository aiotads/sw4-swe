# Chapter 16 — Version Control and Branch Management

> *"One-Version Rules are surprisingly important for organizational efficiency. … Developers within an organization must not have a choice where to commit, or which version of an existing component to depend upon."*
> Give developers no choice of where to commit or which version to depend on — annoying for one, far better for the whole.

## Summary

This opens the "Tools" part (Part IV). Version control (VCS) is perhaps the **most universally adopted** tool in software engineering. The chapter first explains why it became a near-universal norm by making the book's thesis concrete — **software engineering is programming integrated over time** — and VCS is the engineer's primary tool for managing "source × time." Think of VCS as an extended filesystem: `VCS(filename, time, branch) => contents`. To a newcomer who's only written code "alone, for a few weeks," VCS looks like a needless "undo button for a whole project" — but its real value is **letting teams and organizations scale.**

VCS comes in two architectures: **centralized** (Subversion, Perforce, Google's Piper) and **distributed (DVCS)** (Git, Mercurial). Git dominates today ("when in doubt, use it"). But the chapter stresses: **all modern VCSs share the same core functionality, differing in UX; what matters is what you put in and how you use it** — and "how" is almost entirely **policy and convention.** The key policy concept is the **Source of Truth**: centralized VCSs bake it into the design, while DVCS needs policy to declare "which branch in which repo" is the source of truth (or you regress to "Presentation v5 - final - redlines - Josh's version v2" chaos).

The chapter's central stance: **trunk-based development (one repo, no dev branches) is a particularly scalable policy.** Long-lived **dev branches** are a "perceived safety crutch that's actually a productivity drag" — small merges beat big ones, and author-merges beat batched ones; the fix is to **rely heavily on tests and CI, keep the build green, and disable incomplete features at runtime** (DORA research shows trunk-based development strongly correlates with high-performing orgs). **Release branches** are relatively benign (abandoned eventually, minimal cherry-picks). On the Piper monorepo (~50,000 engineers, 60,000–70,000 commits per workday), Google enforces the **One-Version Rule**: **a developer adding a dependency must never have a choice of "which version"** — or a fork causes diamond dependencies, build failures, or hard runtime bugs. A monorepo makes One Version "hard to violate," but it's **not the one true way**; what matters isn't the monorepo but **adhering to One Version as much as possible** (a manyrepo or VMR works too).

## Why read this

- **You still hold "what's this week's merge strategy" meetings and someone is the "merge coordinator."** The chapter names this **pure overhead from long-lived dev branches**: branches don't buy stability, they just slow and amplify the symptom (merge risk). The fix is **trunk-based development + CI + feature flags** — "branches are a drag on productivity."
- **Your team has argued over "which version of this library" or been burned by diamond dependencies.** Here's the policy that ends it: **the One-Version Rule — no choice when adding a dependency.** Understand why forks are dangerous (shading fools functions but not types) and you'll see the value of a "consistent choke point."
- **You're torn between monorepo and manyrepo, Git or something else.** The chapter demotes this to a secondary question: **what matters is adhering to One Version and a Source of Truth**, not the repo structure or VCS. It also introduces the **VMR (virtual monorepo)** as a best-of-both-worlds direction.
- **Your newcomers see "rolling back to undo" as a high-overhead luxury.** That's mistaking "programming" for "software engineering." The chapter's one-liner — "VCS is the primary tool for managing source × time" — helps you explain why version control isn't optional.

## Key details

### What Is Version Control?
The core claim: a VCS tracks revisions of files over time, letting multiple developers work on the same files simultaneously. Early systems used **one-at-a-time file locking** to establish "which is newer"; more advanced ones treat a multi-file commit as a single unit (**atomicity**) — CVS, lacking it, was prone to corruption and lost changes. Think of VCS as an extended filesystem mapping: **`VCS(filename, time, branch) => contents`**, making *time* an explicit part of the operation (unnecessary in programming, critical in software engineering).

Takeaway: the (slight) resistance to VCS comes directly from **conflating "programming" with "software engineering"** — a newcomer who's never worked on "multi-person, multi-week" code sees a "project-wide undo" as a needless luxury. But VCS's real value is **bridging single- and multi-developer processes, letting teams and orgs scale** (even though we rarely use it as an undo). It also removes "which is newer?", automates error-prone "which changes are applied" tracking, satisfies audit/regulatory needs (a formal record of every change to every line), and triggers a moment of reflection at commit time (run static analysis, check coverage, run tests — see [[ch20]]).

### Centralized vs Distributed VCS
The core claim: **all modern VCSs share the same core functionality** (given atomic batch commits, the rest is UI) — arguing over which is "better" is mostly about UX, naming, edge features, performance. Choosing a VCS is like choosing a filesystem format: among modern-enough options the differences are minor, and **what matters far more is what you put in and how you use it.** But the big architectural split (centralized vs distributed) makes configuration, policy, and scaling easier or harder:
- **Centralized:** a single central repo (RCS locking → CVS batches and concurrent checkout → Subversion true atomic commits).
- **Distributed (DVCS):** Git, Mercurial. The core difference is "**where can you commit? which copies count as a repo?**" — any clone/fork is a repo you can commit to. Centrality is purely conceptual/policy, not enforced by the tech. DVCS aids offline work and collaboration, with especially strong open-source adoption.

Takeaway: **Git dominates today; when in doubt, use it** (there's value in doing what everyone does). But Google's main repo is a custom (massive) centralized VCS — moves toward Git were stymied by codebase/userbase scale and Hyrum's Law effects (most tools can't handle 50,000 engineers and tens of millions of commits; DVCS transmits the full history/metadata, almost entirely waste at this scale). **In Google's workflow, centrality and in-cloud storage are critical to scaling** (any developer touches only a tiny fraction of files and versions).

### Source of Truth
The core claim: centralized VCSs **bake the source of truth into the design** — the latest commit at trunk is the current version. DVCS has **no inherent** single source of truth; in theory branches can propagate unchecked, risking regression to "Presentation v5 - final - redlines - Josh's version v2." **Well-managed DVCS projects declare "one branch in one repo" the source of truth** (exactly what GitHub/GitLab practice). The book imagines "no clear source of truth": how do you ensure a release build includes everyone's last few weeks of work? What *sublinear*, non-central mechanism does that? Probably none — eventually someone keeps a list of "which features are ready," **and that bookkeeping reproduces a centralized source of truth.**

Takeaway: the Source of Truth is **relative** (the same project can have different sources of truth at Google, RedHat, and Linus — DVCS works well when orgs and their sources of truth are hierarchical and invisible to outsiders). But as long as there's **no choice or uncertainty about where to push** within an org, you avoid a large class of chaotic scaling problems. The tech is only part of it; nearly every org layers an equal amount of **policy and convention** on top — and branch management has more policy than any other topic.

### Branch Management
The core claim:
- **WIP ≈ a branch:** every piece of work-in-progress is equivalent to a branch (most explicit in DVCS; in centralized VCS, uncommitted local changes are conceptually no different from branch commits). This matters for refactoring — "rename Widget to OldWidget" has several interpretations depending on policy (just trunk? all branches? even others' uncommitted changes?).
- **Dev branches:** before consistent unit testing, fearing any change would regress elsewhere, teams treated trunk specially and used feature dev branches. **The chapter calls "heavy dev-branch use for stability" inherently misguided** — the same commits will merge to trunk eventually; **small merges beat big ones, author-merges beat batched ones**; long-lived branches make regressions harder to isolate and fix. How orgs get addicted: seeing "merging a long-lived branch reduced stability," they conclude "branch merges are risky," then slow down and coordinate the symptom (merges) — more branches, more coordination, some unlucky engineer becomes the "merge coordinator" with weekly merge-strategy meetings — **all pure overhead.** The fix: **trunk-based development, heavy tests and CI, keep the build green, disable incomplete/untested features at runtime.**
- **Release branches:** if the release cycle is longer than a few hours, a release branch representing "exactly the code that went into the release build" is sensible; critical fixes can be **cherry-picked (a minimal, targeted merge)** from trunk. **Relatively benign** — the difference from a dev branch is the expected end state: a dev branch merges back to trunk; a release branch is **eventually abandoned.** High-functioning CD orgs (releasing from trunk many times a day) barely use them (just add the fix and redeploy).

### Version Control at Google: Piper, One Version, Monorepo
The core claim: most of Google's source lives in a **single monorepo** shared by ~50,000 engineers, on the custom centralized VCS **Piper** (a distributed microservice, 80TB+ of content and metadata, **60,000–70,000 commits per workday**, ~**15 seconds** to create a client, add a file, and commit). Being in-house, Piper bakes in and enforces policies — like hierarchical **`OWNERS`** files (each level of the tree lists who can approve commits, see [[ch09]]).

- **One-Version Rule:** on top of Single Source of Truth — **"for every dependency in our repo, there must be only one version to choose."** Third-party packages: one version checked in (in steady state); internal packages: no forking without renaming/repackaging. **The core rule: a developer adding a dependency must never have a choice of "which version."** It can feel like an arbitrary impediment to one developer, yet it's critical for efficient organizational scaling.
  - **The danger of forks (multiple versions):** a team finds a bug in Abseil/Guava, forks it without renaming. If any project depends on both the original and the fork, the best case is a build failure, the worst is hard runtime bugs (linking two mismatched same-named libraries). The fork adds a "coloring/partitioning" property, making "adding a new dependency" potentially require running all tests. **Shading (Java renaming internal dependencies to hide them) works for functions but fails for types that cross packages** — no trick allowing multiple versions in one binary saves vocabulary types.
- **(Nearly) no long-lived branches:** dev branches should be minimal and short-lived (echoing Agile, DORA trunk-based, the Phoenix Project's "reduce WIP"). A new Widget should be **committed to trunk, disabled at runtime, hidden by visibility** — not living in a parallel branch where "choice" sneaks back. Of ~1,000 teams, fewer than 10 have a long-lived dev branch (for unusual "compatibility over time" needs — data formats or API compatibility); and a **build horizon** caps version skew: every production job is rebuilt and redeployed at most every 6 months.
- **Monorepo:** One Version is "**hard to violate**" in a monorepo (no deciding which version is "official," no discovering which repos matter; consistency amplifies new tools). But it's **not the one true way** — open source thrives on manyrepo. What matters isn't the monorepo but **adhering to One Version.** Tools increasingly blend fine-grained repos with monorepo behavior (Git submodules, Bazel external deps, CMake subprojects) to synthesize a **VMR (virtual monorepo)** — if all projects share secrecy/legal requirements, a true monorepo is fine; otherwise aim for the *functionality* of a monorepo with a more flexible implementation.

### Future of Version Control
The core claim: Microsoft, Facebook, Netflix, and Uber have publicly cited monorepos; DORA has published extensively. Arguments against monorepos are mostly technical limits (big repos are slow) — but Git keeps improving (shallow clones, sparse branches), so "the repo must stay small" matters less over time. The "doesn't match open source" argument is true but OSS practices come from "prioritizing freedom, lack of coordination, lack of compute," while inside an org you can assume compute, coordination, and some central authority. The author predicts the next 10–20 years: VCSs both support larger repos and offer better cross-project/org "stitching"; someone (package managers or Linux distros) will catalyze a de facto VMR standard. The closer: **version numbers are really timestamps; allowing version skew adds the dimensional complexity of *time*, which costs a lot — and we can learn to avoid it. It all starts with something logically like a monorepo.**

## Key terms

- **Version control system (VCS)** — tracks revisions of files over time; think of it as an extended filesystem mapping `VCS(filename, time, branch) => contents`. The primary tool for managing source × time.
- **Centralized vs distributed** — centralized (Subversion/Perforce/Piper) has a single central repo; DVCS (Git/Mercurial) makes any clone a committable repo. Same core function, different UX.
- **Source of Truth** — which branch in which repo is authoritative; baked in for centralized, policy-defined for DVCS; relative (can differ by org).
- **Trunk-based development** — one repo, no (long-lived) dev branches; small increments committed straight to trunk + CI + feature flags. DORA-correlated with high-performing orgs.
- **Dev branch** — a long-lived feature branch; presumed to buy stability but actually a productivity drag (big merges, hard-to-isolate regressions). Should be rare and short-lived.
- **Release branch** — represents exactly the code in a release build; relatively benign, eventually abandoned, with minimal cherry-picks; barely used by high-functioning CD orgs.
- **One-Version Rule** — no choice of "which version" when adding a dependency; only one version of each dependency to choose. Critical for efficient organizational scaling.
- **Fork without renaming** — the classic One-Version violation; causes diamond dependencies, build failures, or runtime bugs (see [[ch21]]).
- **Shading** — Java renaming internal dependencies to hide them; works for functions but fails for cross-package types — a patch over the real "multiple versions needed" problem.
- **Monorepo / VMR** — a monorepo makes One Version hard to violate but isn't the only answer; a VMR stitches fine-grained repos (Git submodules/Bazel/CMake) to approximate monorepo functionality.
- **Piper** — Google's custom centralized VCS, a distributed microservice, 80TB+, ~50,000 engineers, 60,000–70,000 commits per workday.
- **Build horizon** — every production job is rebuilt and redeployed at most every 6 months; caps potential version skew.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Does your team hold "merge strategy" meetings, have a "merge coordinator," or maintain long-lived dev branches? Tally that overhead — what would disappear under **trunk-based development + CI + feature flags**? Can you solve "trunk instability" with tests rather than branches? Per [[ch23]].
2. In your codebase, does a developer adding a dependency **have a choice of "which version"**? Have you been burned by an un-renamed fork or a diamond dependency? Apply the **One-Version Rule**: which third-party/internal packages secretly have multiple versions — a time bomb? Per [[ch21]].
3. Do you have a clear **Source of Truth** (one branch in one repo)? Or is it "after pulling a teammate's repo, I'm not sure I have all the changes"? Where does a newcomer get a "known-good" copy? Does this scale **sublinearly** as the team grows?
4. You're choosing monorepo vs manyrepo, Git or other — the chapter says that's a **secondary question.** Refocus on "can I adhere to One Version and a Source of Truth?": does your current repo structure help or hurt that? Is a **VMR** your best of both worlds?
5. Does your team treat version control as a luxury "project-wide undo," or as "the primary tool for managing source × time"? Is anyone mistaking "software development (sit and code)" for "software engineering (keep code useful over time)"? That mental-model gap is the root of VCS's value (ties to [[ch01]]).

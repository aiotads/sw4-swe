# Chapter 25 | Compute as a Service

> *"I don't try to understand computers. I try to understand the programs." —Barbara Liskov*
> CaaS abstracts away the toil of understanding and managing hardware so engineers can focus on the programs.

## Summary

After writing code you need hardware to run it — so you buy or rent it. **That, in essence, is Compute as a Service (CaaS)**: "Compute" is shorthand for the computing power needed to actually run your programs. This final chapter traces the evolution of Google's internal **Borg** (precursor to today's CaaS architectures like Kubernetes and Mesos), showing how the simple ask — "just give me hardware to run my stuff" — grows into a system that survives and scales as your organization evolves. Four parts: **① Taming the compute environment (automation of toil → automated scheduling → containerization & multitenancy → rightsizing/autoscaling); ② Writing software for managed compute (architect for failure, batch vs serving, managing state, connecting to a service, one-off code); ③ CaaS over time and scale (containers as an abstraction, one service to rule them all, submitted configuration); ④ Choosing a compute service (centralization vs customization, level of abstraction/serverless, public vs private).**

Running through it all is "**cattle, not pets**" — held to be a fundamental tool behind Google's success over the past 15 years. A pet, when broken, has a human come (usually in a panic) to nurse it back; it's hard to replace. Cattle (replica001–100) get automatically removed and replaced — **the defining trait is that a new instance can be stamped out fully automatically, with no manual setup**, yielding self-healing. But treating machines as cattle only guarantees automatic return to health; functioning smoothly *during* failures takes extra design (dynamically assigned work chunks; serving jobs draining in-flight requests on a shutdown notice). The biggest enemy of cattle is **state**: in-process state and local storage should be treated as transient, with "real storage" externalized to a replicated, persistent system (Google built GFS, Bigtable, Spanner for this). Other keys: service discovery (a layer of indirection rather than hardcoded hostnames), idempotency (the antidote to retries and duplicate requests, via client-assigned IDs), and caching ("provision the cache to meet latency goals, but provision the core application for the total load").

**Containers** were born for isolation/multitenancy (cgroups, contributed by Google into the Linux kernel in 2007) but mattered more as an **abstraction boundary** — when the machine changes, only the container layer adapts, not the application. A filesystem abstraction eases onboarding external software and lets dependencies be prepackaged; named resources (like network ports) are abstracted too (Borg's early omission led `PickUnusedPortOrDie` to be used 20,000+ times in the C++ codebase). But **Hyrum's Law hits the container abstraction especially hard**: Borg limiting PIDs to 0–32,000 became an unpromised "API" people depended on (log systems assumed five-digit PIDs; `{hostname, timestamp, pid}` was assumed unique) — fixing it is an effort still ongoing eight years later. **Borg unifying batch and serving** brings two efficiencies: serving machines become cattle (avoiding the linear management cost of every team running its own pool); and batch fills the slack serving jobs reserve for spikes, so "batch runs effectively for free." **Choosing a compute service is high-lock-in** (Hyrum's Law + the surrounding tooling ecosystem): centralization vs customization (Borg's bifurcations for Cloud VMs and Search disk made the API large and hard to test), level of abstraction (serverless brings autoscaling/scale-to-zero but demands true statelessness and cedes environment control; Google chose not to bet heavily on serverless because Borg already covers most benefits and many apps can't be truly stateless), and public vs private (outsource management overhead and scale easily, but fear lock-in; mitigate with open-source architectures / multicloud / hybrid). Conclusion: scale requires a **common shared compute infrastructure**; containers are key for sharing machines and providing an abstraction layer; using it well means designing applications as cattle.

## Why read this

- **You're still SSH-ing into machines, manually deploying and restarting processes.** The chapter starts from Jeff Dean's 2002 nightmare ("manually start and monitor processes on 50+ machines, and migrate by hand when one dies") and gives a clear evolution: simple automation (deploy scripts, self-healing restart) → automated scheduling (a central service owning the machine list) → containerized multitenancy → rightsizing/autoscaling. You'll learn "at what scale, and why, the naive solution collapses."
- **Your service is a "pet" that needs human rescue when broken.** "Cattle, not pets" is the chapter's (and scaling's) core idea: design servers to be cattle that can be stamped out fully automatically, so maintenance burden doesn't grow linearly (or worse) with fleet size. But the chapter is honest: being cattle isn't enough — **state** is the real challenge; you must treat local state as transient and externalize real storage.
- **You're choosing a compute service for your team/org (VM? containers? serverless? public cloud?).** The final section is for you: it spells out the **high-lock-in** nature (Hyrum's Law means you use every property of the system; the surrounding tooling ecosystem is hard to migrate), the centralization-vs-customization tension, serverless's real comparison point (not "VMs as pets" but "persistent containers"), and public/multi/hybrid-cloud lock-in mitigations.
- **You want to understand how terrifying Hyrum's Law is at the infrastructure layer.** The PID-32,000 case is the book's most vivid illustration: an unpromised implementation detail (the PID ceiling) depended on as a guarantee by countless systems, whose fix is an eight-year, three-phase effort that still has to backport data. It will permanently sharpen your wariness that "observable behavior = API."

## Key details

### Taming the Compute Environment
Core claim: starting from a university lab's SFTP+SSH+manual-compile-and-run, the naive solution collapses at scale. Jeff Dean's 2002 design doc captured the pain: manually starting processes on 50+ machines, ad hoc monitoring, no auto-migration when a machine dies, and a human-maintained "sign-up file" to throttle usage — leading to poor scheduling and resource contention. The evolution:
- **Simple automations:** deploying can be a shell script (evolving into more robust, parallel code); monitoring can be automated (processes export liveness/throughput metrics to shared storage or a monitoring service like Grafana/Prometheus); self-healing — an agent detects anomalies and kills the process, and a `while true; do run && break; done` wrapper restarts it (the cloud equivalent is an autohealing policy: kill and re-create a VM/container on a failed health check).
- **Automated scheduling:** the next step is automating machine assignment — the first real "service" that grows into CaaS. A central service knows all available machines, picks idle ones on demand, and auto-deploys your binary, replacing the hand-maintained sign-up file (reminiscent of early time-sharing). It can combine with failure reaction (scan logs for bad-health signals, flag for repair, try auto-fixes like reboot/disk-scan first) and reschedule work onto a new machine when one breaks. The quote came from the 2002 "Global WorkQueue" design doc.
- **Containerization and Multitenancy:** one-machine-per-program is hugely wasteful (RAM/CPU): there are usually far more job types than machine types; machines deploy slowly while program needs grow; and old machines linger into a heterogeneous fleet. The natural fix: specify each program's resource requirements (CPU/RAM/disk) and let the scheduler **bin-pack** replicas onto a machine pool. But if anyone misbehaves (a bug or organic growth eats more than specified), things break — CPU overuse causes latency blips for neighboring serving jobs; RAM overuse triggers OOM kills (Google always chooses OOM-kill-and-migrate over disk-swap latency) — plus dependency-version conflicts, exclusive use of `/tmp`, and security. So a multitenant compute service must provide **isolation**. VMs are the classic isolation solution but carry heavy overhead (a full OS to run, slow to boot), unsuited to small-footprint, short-lived batch jobs — leading Google's 2003 Borg designers to **containers** (cgroups, contributed into the Linux kernel in 2007, + chroot jails/bind mounts/union FS for filesystem isolation; open-source implementations include Docker and LMCTFY). Isolation failures keep getting discovered over time (e.g., the 2011 PID exhaustion).
- **Rightsizing and autoscaling:** Borg circa 2006 scheduled by the replica count and resource requirements an engineer typed into config — but asking humans for resource numbers is flawed (not daily-interaction numbers, time-consuming to determine, scaling in cost as services accrue, and going stale as programs grow), ending in outages where slack reserved for spikes gets eaten. The fix is to automate the settings — surprisingly hard: only recently has Google reached "more than half of resource usage across the Borg fleet determined by rightsizing automation." But even half the usage is a larger fraction of *configs*, so most engineers needn't fret over bin-packing — a success for "**easy things should be easy, complex things possible**."
- **Summary:** organizations grow along three axes (number of applications, number of replicas, size of the largest app); managing scale requires automation, which itself grows more involved over time (GPU/TPU scheduling was a major Borg change over the past decade). Even datacenter turn-up moved from "a weeks-long, risky, specialized manual process" to automation (Google is still figuring this out).

### Writing Software for Managed Compute
Core claim: the move from hand-managed machine lists to automated scheduling/rightsizing made fleet management far easier but profoundly changed how we write software.
- **Architecting for Failure / Cattle vs Pets:** shard 1M documents across 200 machines (12 days → 100 minutes), but the Borg scheduler may unilaterally kill a worker and move it (to update a kernel, a bad disk, better bin-packing — as a software author you shouldn't need to know why). "**Cattle, not pets**": a pet broken means a human panicking to nurse it, hard to replace; cattle (replica001–100) get auto-removed and replaced, the key being **a new instance stamped out fully automatically with no manual setup**, yielding self-healing. Pets → maintenance grows linearly or superlinearly with fleet size; cattle → the system auto-returns to health after failure. But cattle isn't enough: to run smoothly at a moderate failure rate, split work into dynamically-assigned chunks (1M docs → 1,000 chunks of 1,000; a worker finishes a chunk, reports, takes another; a failure loses at most one chunk — fitting Google's MapReduce/Flume of the era); serving jobs must refuse new requests while draining in-flight ones on a Borg shutdown notice, and the load balancer must understand "I can't accept new requests."
- **Batch vs Serving:** batch jobs (data processing / ML training) care about **throughput**, are short-lived (minutes to hours); serving jobs (answering search queries from a prebuilt index) care about single-request **latency**, are long-lived (restarted only on new releases), with longer startup. Batch uses MapReduce→Flume to chunk and dynamically assign for failure resistance; serving is more naturally failure-resistant (requests chunk naturally, via load balancing) but has unnatural exceptions: a "leader" server (holds system state, a new instance can't recreate it), data sharded across 100 servers each holding 1% (one down = temporarily losing that data), and a server hardcoded by hostname elsewhere.
- **Managing State:** state is the trouble with treating jobs as cattle — replacing a cattle job loses in-process state and local storage. The simplest fix is to externalize all storage; if local state is all immutable, failure resistance is relatively painless. "Is persistent storage itself cattle?" Yes — via **state replication** (analogous to RAID: treat disks as transient yet maintain state; usually 3–5 replicas, needing consensus for writes — so Google built GFS/Bigtable/Spanner). Other local-storage uses: **caching** (transient local storage betting state usually persists for better average performance — key lesson: **provision the cache for latency goals, but the core application for total load**, so losing the cache layer means higher latency, not an outage); pulling data local at warm-up; and **batched writes** (e.g., monitoring data tolerant of partial loss, or re-creatable chunk outputs; long computations can checkpoint to persistent storage in small time windows).
- **Connecting to a Service:** anything hardcoding your hostname (even as a startup parameter) makes your replicas not cattle. The fix is a layer of **indirection (service discovery)**: others refer to you by a durable identifier resolved by another system the scheduler writes to; clients look up the address and connect at startup, monitoring in the background (most solutions include load balancing). A consequence is you sometimes must **retry requests** (the server may be taken down before answering — retries need backoff, graceful degradation, and jitter to avoid cascading failures, so they belong in the RPC library, not hand-rolled). For mutating requests, duplicates are tricky — guarantee some **idempotency** (issuing twice = once); a useful tool is **client-assigned IDs** (a pizza order gets a client ID; a server seeing it again treats it as a duplicate and reports success). One surprise: the scheduler loses contact with a machine, reschedules its work elsewhere — then the machine comes back, and two processes each think they're "replica072"; they disambiguate by which the address-resolution system points to (the other self-terminates) — another reason for idempotency.
- **One-Off Code:** engineers also run one-off analyses, prototypes, custom pipelines. Often the workstation suffices (skim 1 GB of logs with a Python script in a minute or two); but skimming 1 TB (a year's worth) — waiting a day is unacceptable, whereas a distributed run on a few hundred cores in minutes is the difference between "now vs tomorrow" or even "done vs never done." A worry is wasted resources, but it's a conscious trade-off: **engineer time almost always costs more than that compute** (a thousand core-hours costs nowhere near a day of engineering — like the markers from the book's opening). The difference is compute is easy to accidentally over-grab (someone left 1,000 GCE VMs idle over vacation; a new hire debugging on a workstation spawned 8,000 full-machine workers) — solved by per-engineer quotas, or, as Google does, near-unlimited low-priority batch quota (which runs effectively for free).

### CaaS Over Time and Scale
Core claim: several aspects of how a CaaS architecture affects software across time and scale deserve a closer look.
- **Containers as an Abstraction:** containers began as isolation/multitenancy but mattered more as an **abstraction boundary between deployed software and the actual machine** — as the machine changes over time, only the container layer (one team) adapts, not the application (each team, as the org grows). Two examples: a **filesystem abstraction** lets externally-written software (open source, acquisitions) onboard without custom machine configs and lets software predeclare/prepackage dependencies (depending on machine-installed software is a leaky abstraction forcing one shared library version, making upgrades nearly impossible); and **named resources** (network ports, GPUs) — Borg's early omission of ports from the container abstraction made binaries hunt for free ports, so `PickUnusedPortOrDie` is used **20,000+ times** in Google's C++ codebase; Docker gives containers a virtual NIC via namespaces, and Kubernetes requires containers (pods) to be "real" IPs so any app can listen on any port without conflict.
- **Containers and implicit dependencies:** Hyrum's Law hits the container abstraction **harder than usual** (huge user base + users don't feel they're using an API, e.g., with the filesystem). **The PID-32,000 case:** Linux assigns PIDs in [0, PID_MAX−1], PID_MAX defaulting to 32,000 (raisable) — but Borg limiting PIDs to 0–32,000 became a depended-on implicit API guarantee (log storage assumed five-digit PIDs, broke on six digits). The fix was a multi-phase saga: ① a temporary per-container PID cap (so one thread-leaking job can't kill a whole machine); ② split the PID space for threads vs processes (few depended on the 32,000 for thread PIDs, so threads could be raised while processes stayed); ③ introduce PID namespaces for a complete per-container PID space — but a multitude of systems assumed `{hostname, timestamp, pid}` uniquely identifies a process, which would break, and that fix is **still ongoing eight years later**. The lesson isn't "use PID namespaces" but: designing a container system maintainable over time is hard, so **there's value in using one developed and used by a broader community** (where such issues have already happened and the lessons been incorporated).
- **One Service to Rule Them All:** the original WorkQueue targeted only some batch jobs sharing a pool, while serving jobs each ran a dedicated pool (the open-source equivalent: a separate Kubernetes cluster per workload type). Borg (started 2003) assimilated these into one large pool covering both serving and batch — the only pool in any datacenter. Two efficiencies: ① **serving machines became cattle** ("machines are anonymous: programs don't care which machine they run on as long as it has the right characteristics") — otherwise each serving team manages its own pool, management practices diverge, and company-wide changes (new server architecture, datacenter moves) grow ever harder; unified management avoids this linear factor (no *n* management practices, just Borg). ② **batch and serving needs are complementary**: serving must be over-provisioned (capacity for spikes/partial outages without latency loss), so its machines are underutilized; overcommitting more serving defeats the slack's purpose, but **batch jobs can fill the idle 70%**, with the policy that serving reclaims it on demand (freeze CPU, kill RAM) — since batch cares about aggregate throughput and its replicas are cattle anyway, it happily soaks up the slack. The result: batch runs effectively for free (most of the time at Google), or serving pays only for what it uses, not the failure-resistance slack.
- **Multitenancy for serving jobs:** extending managed compute to serving brings new requirements: **rescheduling must be throttled** (killing 50% of a batch job's replicas is a blip, but killing 50% of a serving job's leaves too few to serve traffic); **batch can usually be killed without warning** (lost processing is redoable), but killing serving without warning risks user-facing errors/latency, so give **several seconds' notice** to drain in-flight requests and stop accepting new ones.
- **Submitted Configuration:** the Borg scheduler receives config as RPC content; you *can* send it via a CLI with parameters stored in docs or your head — but **depending on documentation and tribal knowledge is rarely good** (both deteriorate over time, see [[ch03]]), and wrapping the CLI in a local script is still inferior to a **dedicated configuration language**. Over time a logical service grows along many axes (across datacenters; staging/dev environments; attached services like a memcached), and a standardized config language makes standard operations easy to express ("update to the new binary, taking down no more than 5% of capacity at a time"), lets other teams include standard config (otherwise swapping out memcache org-wide or pushing a security update everywhere is hard), and is a prerequisite for deployment automation (see [[ch24]]).

### Choosing a Compute Service
Core claim: few organizations will build from scratch like Google; today there's open source (Kubernetes, Mesos, OpenWhisk, Knative) and public-cloud managed offerings (GCP MIGs, AWS EC2 autoscaling, AKS/GKE, AWS Lambda/Cloud Functions). But the choice is **high-lock-in**: by Hyrum's Law, code uses every property of the system (choose VMs and teams tweak VM images; choose containers and they call cluster-manager APIs; if the architecture lets VMs/containers be pets, teams treat them so, and moving to a cattle-based solution gets hard). Borg's tiny change from bash to ash (to save memory) backfired — some teams had already replaced bash in their custom FS overlay with custom code, so switching to ash (un-overwritten) *raised* their memory, triggering alerts and a rollback. And any compute service gets surrounded by a large ecosystem of helper tools (logging/monitoring/debug/alerting/visualization/config languages/UIs) that a change would force rewriting — even enumerating them is hard. So the choice matters; it's a trade-off:
- **Centralization vs Customization:** for management overhead and resource efficiency, the best is a single CaaS for the whole fleet with only its tools (Borg's path). But a growing org has diverse needs: when Google launched GCE (VM-as-a-Service) in 2012, VMs ran in Borg containers, but "cattle" didn't suit Cloud's workloads (users treat VMs as pets) — Cloud built **VM live migration**, and Borg was adapted to avoid at-will killing of VM containers and to optimize against rescheduling, rolled out only for cloud machines (a small but real bifurcation). Another: circa 2011 a Search serving container built a giant on-disk index (multiple drives, hours to fill), but Borg assumed any bad disk meant rescheduling — high disk failure rates wrecked availability, so Borg let containers handle disk failure themselves and Search adapted to run with partial data loss. Many such bifurcations (filesystem shape/access, memory control, CPU/memory locality, special hardware, scheduling constraints) made Borg's API large, unwieldy, and hard to test (nobody knew what happened if a container requested both Cloud's eviction treatment and Search's disk treatment). After 2012 Borg spent significant time cleaning up the API (some features were unused; others spread by config copy-paste, so whitelisting marked power-user features) — still ongoing eight years on (label-based grouping isn't fully done). **Kubernetes, benefiting from the Borg cleanup without a broad legacy user base, was much more modern from the start** (though it now hits the same issues at scale).
- **Level of Abstraction: Serverless:** the story reads like "more abstraction is better," but it's not that simple. From bare-metal pets → containers as cattle, with a VM path in between (IaaS like GCE/EC2 as flexible bare metal, up to heavier container substitutes with autoscaling/rightsizing). Google's experience: managing cattle, not pets, is the scale solution; given cattle, containers are the natural choice (lighter, configurable enough to punch a hole for special hardware); VMs-as-cattle win mainly when you need to bring your own OS or have existing VM experience/workloads. **Serverless** is a higher abstraction: you write only the framework's Action/Handler (inversion of control); the framework servers are themselves multitenant, dynamically loading/unloading action code and routing requests, so individual teams no longer run servers ("serverless"). Most discussions compare serverless to "VMs as pets" (where it's revolutionary — autoscaling, low overhead, no explicit provisioning), but since you should already aim for shared multitenant cattle, serverless's right comparison is **persistent containers like Borg/K8s/Mesos**.
  - **Pros/cons:** serverless requires **truly stateless** code (no user VMs or Spanner inside; all the local-state techniques don't apply; everything is request-scope). In practice most orgs have needs that can't be served statelessly → depend on specific solutions (a managed database, a frequent serverless companion) or run both a container and a serverless solution (many serverless frameworks are built atop other layers: AppEngine on Borg, Knative on K8s, Lambda on EC2). Serverless is attractive at the **low-traffic end** (scales to zero; cost scales with traffic, whereas a K8s persistent cluster can't scale to zero and has a minimum "just being available" cost); the **high-traffic end** is limited by underlying infrastructure regardless; in between both scale, but serverless more reactively and granularly. Serverless also means **losing some environment control** (less management overhead, but missing functionality becomes a problem — e.g., the Code Jam team hand-wrote a script to hit its AppEngine frontend with an artificial spike before the contest to warm up enough instances, exactly the hand-tweaking serverless was supposed to avoid).
  - **The trade-off:** Google chose not to invest heavily in serverless — Borg is advanced enough for most serverless benefits (autoscaling, frameworks, deployment, unified logging/monitoring); it lacks aggressive scaling (scale-to-zero), but most of Google's footprint is high-traffic services where over-provisioning small ones is cheap, and Google runs many apps that can't be truly stateless (GCE, BigQuery, Spanner, long-tail search) — so one unified architecture outweighs a separate serverless stack. But that's not every org's right answer: serverless's main pull is for **small orgs/teams** (the comparison is inherently unfair — serverless lets the vendor absorb most management overhead; running one team's code on shared AWS Lambda/Cloud Run is far simpler and cheaper than standing up a GKE/AKS cluster *not* shared among many teams). Note that as an org grows it may outgrow pure serverless, so solutions with a **break-out path** (KNative → Kubernetes) are attractive.
- **Public vs Private:** public cloud = outsourcing (part of) management overhead to a provider (focus on your expertise without growing huge infrastructure expertise; providers charge above bare metal but have the expertise, amortized across customers), and scales more easily (from signing a colocation lease, to a CLI for more VMs, up to autoscaling that changes your footprint automatically — especially valuable for young orgs/products with unpredictable needs). The main concern is **lock-in** (a provider could hike prices or fail — early serverless provider Zimki shut down in 2007 with three months' notice). Mitigate by using public-cloud solutions on an open-source architecture (like Kubernetes) to preserve a migration path (though Hyrum's Law makes "guaranteeing no provider-specific parts" hard); two extensions — run a higher-level open-source solution (OpenWhisk/KNative) atop a lower-level public cloud (EC2), or go **multicloud** (e.g., GKE + AKS for K8s); plus **hybrid cloud** (part private, part public, e.g., public cloud for overflow) — all requiring well-connected environments (direct network connectivity + common APIs on both sides).

Engineering takeaway (the book's close): scale requires a **common shared compute infrastructure** (one or a few shared clusters per region), giving efficiency in management and resource costs and enabling shared tooling on top; **containers** are key both to share a machine across tasks (resource efficiency) and to provide an abstraction layer between application and OS (resilience over time); using a container architecture well means designing applications as **cattle** (nodes that can be easily, automatically replaced, scaling to thousands of instances), which requires different thinking — treating all local storage (including disk) as ephemeral and avoiding hardcoded hostnames. Google is satisfied and successful with its architecture, but other organizations will pick from the wide spectrum — pets, cattle, serverless, in managed and open-source flavors — a complex trade-off of many factors.

## Key terms

- **Compute as a Service (CaaS)** — the managed compute system that "just give me hardware to run my stuff" grows into; Borg is the precursor to Kubernetes/Mesos.
- **Cattle, not pets** — design servers as cattle that can be stamped out fully automatically, not pets a human must rescue; the root of scaling and self-healing, a core tool behind Google's 15 years.
- **Bin-packing / multitenancy** — pack replicas onto a machine pool by resource requirements to share machines; requires isolation to prevent interference.
- **Isolation / container** — the guarantee multitenancy needs; containers are lighter isolation than VMs (cgroups, contributed by Google into the Linux kernel in 2007) plus an abstraction boundary between application and machine.
- **Rightsizing / autoscaling** — automatically determine resource requirements and replica counts, sparing engineers the time-consuming, error-prone manual sizing; "easy things easy, complex things possible."
- **Architecting for failure** — chunk work for dynamic assignment; serving jobs drain gracefully on a shutdown notice; being cattle isn't enough — you must run smoothly under a failure rate.
- **Batch vs serving** — batch cares about throughput, is short-lived, killable without warning; serving cares about latency, is long-lived, needs throttled rescheduling and a shutdown notice.
- **State replication / transient local storage** — treat local state as transient and externalize real storage to a replicated persistent system (GFS/Bigtable/Spanner); provision cache for latency, core for total load.
- **Service discovery / idempotency** — a layer of indirection instead of hardcoded hostnames; the antidote to retries and duplicate requests is idempotency (client-assigned IDs).
- **Hyrum's Law (on the container abstraction)** — the PID-32,000 case: an unpromised implementation detail depended on as API, whose fix spans eight years and three phases; hence the value of a broadly-used container system.
- **One service to rule them all** — Borg unifying batch and serving into one pool: serving becomes cattle (saving linear management cost) + batch fills serving slack (running effectively for free).
- **Serverless / lock-in** — write only the Handler, framework servers are multitenant, scales to zero; demands true statelessness and cedes environment control; its comparison is persistent containers, not VM pets. Compute choices are high-lock-in (Hyrum's Law + tooling ecosystem), mitigated by open-source / multicloud / hybrid.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Is your service a "pet" or "cattle"? Can you do "one dies, automation stamps out a new one fully automatically, no SSH required"? If not, where are you stuck — non-automated deploy, or hidden **state** (in-process / local disk / hardcoded hostnames) that makes it unreplaceable? Per [[ch22]], which also uses cattle vs pets.
2. Do you treat local state as transient? What actually should be externalized to persistent storage? Is your cache "provisioned for latency goals, core path for total load," or treated as a dependency you can't lose (an outage when it dies)? Does your mutating API have **idempotency** (retries / duplicates won't error)?
3. Does your infrastructure have a "PID-32,000"-style implicit dependency — an unpromised implementation detail (port range, ID format, file layout) depended on as a guarantee everywhere? Can you estimate the cost of changing it? This is [[ch01]]'s Hyrum's Law at the infrastructure layer.
4. Are you choosing a compute service? Have you assessed the real cost of **lock-in** (Hyrum's Law uses every property; the tooling ecosystem is hard to migrate)? Are your needs truly stateless (suited to serverless) or stateful (needing persistent containers)? Small team on public-cloud serverless, big org on unified containers — which box are you in?
5. Does your org run batch and serving in separate pools, or could you **unify** like Borg to capture "serving becomes cattle (saved management cost) + batch fills the slack (effectively free)"? Have you measured the linear management cost of every team running its own pool? Per [[ch07]] on productivity measurement.

# Chapter 4 — Engineering for Equity

> *"Bias is the default."*
> In plain terms: unless you actively correct for it, the systems you build will tilt toward existing bias — not because you're malicious, but because that's the default state.

## Summary

This chapter turns the lens from "how a team collaborates internally" to "who we build products for — and who we unintentionally harm." Author Demma Rodriguez is candid: Google wrote this chapter not because it has all the answers, but because it has **failed publicly** — protecting its most vulnerable users is something Google is still learning to do. The chapter's spine is a single claim: **bias is the default.** Not because engineers are bad, but because everyone carries unconscious bias, and when an engineering team itself isn't representative (Google's engineering population is mostly male, mostly White or Asian), that bias quietly gets written into design decisions and training data — and the product ends up failing minority users.

The sharpest case is the 2015 Google Photos incident that labeled a Black user's friends as "gorillas," **and which still hadn't been properly fixed as late as 2018.** From there the author redefines what a "great engineer" is: a CS degree and the ability to write good code aren't enough; you also need **discernment** — knowing when to build something *and when not to*. The chapter gives actionable principles: **reject singular approaches** (fixing the hiring pipeline alone isn't enough — look at progression and retention too); **challenge established processes** (an internal-mobility system case study reveals that "past performance doesn't predict future performance"); and a five-step "way out" — look in the mirror, **don't build _for_ everyone, build _with_ everyone**, design for the user who'll struggle most, **measure equity rather than assume it**, and believe change is possible. The engineering trade-off running through it all: **it's better to slow down than to ship a product that harms some users (don't trade equity for short-term velocity).**

## Why read this

- **You assume "technology is neutral," so bias isn't your problem.** This chapter uses the real-world failures of Google Photos, autocomplete, and facial recognition to show that **the technology itself isn't the culprit — but a design that isn't resilient enough amplifies harm.** Every default you set and every training set you choose decides whether some group gets seen.
- **You hire, interview, or lead a team.** The chapter names it directly: if you interview people, you're responsible for understanding how biased outcomes happen; as a hiring manager you're accountable for whether your candidate slate is balanced. It translates abstract DEI slogans into quantifiable actions you can take next week.
- **You default to "build the majority case first, handle edge cases later."** The chapter calls that common method **flawed** — it gives already-advantaged users a head start and widens inequity. "Design for the user least like you" isn't political correctness; it's a best practice that makes the experience better for *everyone*.
- **You need an argument against "sacrifice everything for speed."** The internal-mobility case study is a textbook template: one engineer questioned a plausible-looking feature, triggered a review, and used *data* to overturn the assumption that "past performance predicts future performance" — costing project time in exchange for a more equitable process. That's an engineering decision made with reasons.

## Key details

### Bias Is the Default
The core claim: when engineers don't deliberately focus on users of different nationalities, ethnicities, races, genders, ages, socioeconomic statuses, abilities, and belief systems, **even the most talented staff will inadvertently fail their users.** Social scientists have recognized for decades that most people exhibit **unconscious bias** that enforces and spreads existing stereotypes. Unconscious bias is insidious and harder to mitigate than intentional exclusion — even when we want to do the right thing, we may not recognize our own bias. Organizations must therefore acknowledge bias exists and address it in their workforce, product development, and user outreach.

The key link: Google's engineering population is **mostly male and mostly White or Asian**, not representative of all the communities that use its products (citing the 2019 Diversity Report). Lacking those users on the team means **often not having the diversity needed to understand how the product affects vulnerable users.**

#### Case Study — Google Misses the Mark on Racial Inclusion
In 2015, engineer Jacky Alciné pointed out that Google Photos' image-recognition algorithms classified his Black friends as "gorillas." Google was slow and incomplete in responding — **the underlying problem still wasn't adequately addressed as late as 2018.** Three causes:
- **Incomplete dataset:** the photo data fed into the algorithm clearly **didn't represent the population.**
- **The organization itself lacked representation:** tech (and Google) has little Black representation, which biases subjective decisions in algorithm design and data collection — the org's unconscious bias left a more representative product "on the table."
- **The target market didn't include those groups:** Google's tests didn't catch the mistakes, so **its users did** — embarrassing Google and harming users.

Similar failures abound: autocomplete can return offensive or racist results; the ad system could be manipulated to show racist ads; YouTube may miss hate speech it technically bans. **The technology itself isn't to blame** — autocomplete wasn't designed to discriminate, but it **wasn't resilient enough** in its design to exclude hate-speech language, so it harmed users. The harm to Google is also clear: reduced trust and engagement; Black, Latinx, and Jewish applicants could lose faith in Google as a platform or inclusive environment, which in turn **undermines Google's own goal of improving hiring representation.**

Takeaway: "Build for everyone" is a Google brand statement, but there's a long way to go. **One fix: make the engineering org itself look more like the populations it builds for.**

### Understanding the Need for Diversity
The core claim: at Google, being a great engineer **requires bringing diverse perspectives into design and implementation**, and anyone who hires/interviews must contribute to a more representative workforce.

Two myths to disrupt:
1. **"A CS degree + work experience gives you all the skills to be a great engineer."** A degree is often a necessary foundation, but alone (even with experience) it won't make you an engineer.
2. **"Only people with CS degrees can design and build products."** Most programmers do have CS degrees and are good at building code and applying methodologies — but as the cases show, **that approach is insufficient for inclusive, equitable engineering.**

Takeaway: frame all work within the **complete ecosystem** you seek to influence — at minimum, understand your users' demographics, and deliberately focus on **people different from you**, especially those who might use your product to cause harm. The hardest users to consider are those **disenfranchised by the processes and environment** in which they access tech. The ideal is engineering teams that are **representative of their users**; absent that, individual engineers must learn to build for all users.

### Building Multicultural Capacity
The core claim: a mark of a great engineer is **understanding how products can advantage and disadvantage different groups.** Engineers are expected to have technical aptitude, but also **discernment — knowing when to build something and when not to** — including the capacity to identify and reject features that drive adverse outcomes. It's hard, because high performance involves a lot of individualism; but to succeed we must extend focus **beyond our own communities** to the next billion users and to current users our products might leave behind.

- **You wield more power than you realize:** you may build tools billions use daily — shaping how people value human lives, monitoring activity, capturing sensitive data (images of children and loved ones). The first step is to **recognize the default state of your bias** (from many societal and educational factors); only then can you see the often-forgotten users who benefit or are harmed.
- **The concrete harm in facial recognition:** AI-driven facial recognition keeps disadvantaging people of color and ethnic minorities — research isn't comprehensive enough and doesn't span enough skin tones. **If both the training data and the people building the software represent only a small subsection, you can't expect valid output.** In such cases we should be **willing to delay development** to get more complete, accurate data and a more inclusive product.
- **Even with representation, data can be biased:** data science is hard for humans to evaluate. A 2016 study found **more than 117 million American adults** are in a law-enforcement facial-recognition database; given disproportionate policing of Black communities and disparate arrest outcomes, using it could produce **racially biased error rates.** Software is deployed ever faster, but **independent testing isn't keeping up.** The fix is the integrity to slow down and minimize bias in inputs (Google now offers statistical training within AI to help ensure datasets aren't intrinsically biased).

Takeaway: shifting your focus to more comprehensive, multicultural, race-and-gender education is **not just your responsibility but your employer's.** Change doesn't mean one person learns about other cultures alone — it means each of us (as individuals or team leaders) invests in continuous development that builds not just software/leadership skills but the **capacity to understand the diverse experiences of humanity.**

### Making Diversity Actionable
The core claim: systemic equity is attainable **if we accept that we're all accountable for the systemic discrimination in tech.** Deferring or abstracting away personal accountability is ineffective and, depending on your role, irresponsible.

The chapter names a favorite "excuse" used by proponents and detractors alike: "We're working hard to fix [systemic discrimination], but accountability is hard — how do we combat [hundreds of years] of historical discrimination?" — a line that **detours into philosophical/academic conversation and away from focused efforts** to improve conditions and outcomes.

Takeaway: replace the academic conversation with **quantifiable, actionable steps.** As a hiring engineering manager, you're accountable for:
- Is your **candidate slate balanced** — are there women or other underrepresented groups in the review pool?
- After hiring, what **growth opportunities** did you provide, and is their distribution equitable?

Every tech lead/EM has the means to augment equity on their team. Acknowledge it: there are systemic challenges, but **we're all part of the system, and it's our problem to fix.**

### Reject Singular Approaches
The core claim: don't perpetuate solutions offering a single philosophy/methodology for fixing inequity — the problems are complex and multifactorial. Disrupt singular approaches **even when promoted by people we admire or who hold institutional power.**

- **"Fixing the hiring pipeline alone solves representation" is a cherished myth.** It's a fundamental step, but not the immediate issue. We must also recognize systemic inequity in **progression and retention**, alongside more representative hiring and educational disparities across race, gender, socioeconomic, and immigration status.
- **Retention is the neglected leak:** **attrition among Black+ Googlers outpaces all other groups**, confounding representation goals. To drive change, evaluate whether you've built an ecosystem where all aspiring engineers can thrive.
- **"Build for the majority first, edge cases later" is flawed:** it gives users already advantaged in tech access a head start, **increasing inequity.** Relegating consideration of all user groups to near the end of design **lowers the bar** for what a great engineer is. Instead, **build in inclusive design from the start** — it improves the experience for everyone.

Takeaway: **designing for the user least like you isn't just wise — it's a best practice.** The concrete next step is more comprehensive UX research spanning multiple languages, cultures, countries, socioeconomic classes, abilities, and ages — **focus on the hardest or least-represented use case first.**

### Challenge Established Processes
The core claim: building more equitable systems goes beyond inclusive specs — sometimes it means **challenging established processes that drive invalid results.**

Case (an internal global hiring-requisition system): the engineering/PM teams listened well to whom they considered their core users — recruiters. For efficiency, recruiters asked for a feature: as soon as an internal transfer expressed interest, **highlight their performance ratings — specifically *low* ones — to the hiring manager and recruiter.** On its face, expediting evaluation and saving time is good. But three equity questions were raised:
- Are developmental assessments a **predictive** measure of performance?
- Are the performance assessments shown to prospective managers **free of individual bias**?
- Are performance scores **standardized across organizations**?

If any answer is "no," presenting ratings could drive inequitable — therefore **invalid** — results. When a great engineer questioned whether past performance actually predicts future performance, the team did a thorough review and concluded: **candidates with a poor rating were likely to overcome it on a new team** — just as likely to earn a satisfactory or exemplary rating as those who'd never had a poor one. In short, **ratings reflect only how a person is doing in their current role at evaluation time; they don't predict future performance** and shouldn't gauge readiness for a future role. (They can, however, indicate whether someone is mis-slotted on their current team — an opportunity to support them better.)

Takeaway: the analysis **took significant project time, but the positive trade-off was a more equitable internal-mobility process.** A live example of [[ch01]]'s "engineering decisions need reasons."

### Values Versus Outcomes
The core claim: Google's core values rest on respect and a commitment to a diverse, inclusive workforce, and it continually evaluates its processes — yet year after year it **misses the mark** on a representative workforce. **The failure point isn't the company's values, intentions, or investments, but the application of those policies at the implementation level.**

Old habits are hard to break: the users you're used to designing for and getting feedback from may not represent everyone you need to reach — from wearables that don't work for women's bodies to video-conferencing that works poorly for darker skin tones. The five-step way out:
1. **Take a hard look in the mirror.** You can't "build for everyone" without a representative workforce or an engagement model that centers community feedback first. Acknowledge the public failures to protect vulnerable users from racist, antisemitic, and homophobic content.
2. **Don't build _for_ everyone. Build _with_ everyone.** We're not building for everyone yet, but we can't pack up and go home. Engage users across the spectrum of humanity and **intentionally put the most vulnerable communities at the center of design — not as an afterthought.**
3. **Design for the user who'll have the most difficulty using your product.** Building for those with added challenges makes it better for everyone. Put differently: **don't trade equity for short-term velocity.**
4. **Don't assume equity; measure it throughout your systems.** Decision-makers are also subject to bias and may be under-educated on the causes of inequity; you may lack the expertise to even scope an equity issue. **Catering to one userbase may disenfranchise another** — these trade-offs are hard to spot and may be impossible to reverse. Partner with diversity/equity/inclusion subject-matter experts.
5. **Change is possible.** From surveillance to disinformation to harassment, today's problems are overwhelming and can't be solved with past failed approaches or only the skills we already have. **We need to change.**

### Stay Curious, Push Forward
The core claim: the path to equity is long and complex, but we can and should move from merely building tools to **growing our understanding of how our products impact humanity.** Challenge your education, influence your team and managers, and do more comprehensive user research. Finally: as future great engineers, **focus first on the users most impacted by bias and discrimination**, accelerating progress through Continuous Improvement and **owning our failures** — making changes that push humanity forward without further disenfranchising the disadvantaged.

Takeaway: this echoes [[ch01]]'s "software engineering = programming integrated over time" — your design decisions today have consequences across decades and billions of people. Treat equity as an engineering property to be **continuously measured, not declared once.**

## Key terms

- **Bias is the default** — the chapter's core claim. Everyone has unconscious bias; uncorrected systems tilt toward existing stereotypes. A default-state problem, not a malice problem.
- **Unconscious bias** — the tendency to reinforce stereotypes without awareness. Harder to mitigate than intentional exclusion, because even well-meaning people may not recognize their own bias.
- **Representation** — the degree to which an engineering team reflects its users' population (race, gender, etc.). Lacking it = lacking the perspective needed to understand vulnerable users.
- **Discernment** — the great engineer's judgment beyond technical skill: knowing when to build a feature and when *not* to, and rejecting products that drive adverse outcomes.
- **Build _with_ everyone** — the correction to "build for everyone": co-design with users and center vulnerable communities instead of treating edge cases as afterthoughts.
- **Design for the least-like-you user** — start from the person who'll struggle most; it improves the experience for everyone. A best practice, not box-ticking.
- **Inclusive design** — incorporate all user groups from the start, not "majority first, edge cases later" (which widens inequity).
- **Equity vs velocity** — the chapter's core engineering trade-off. Better to slow down for more complete data than to ship something that harms users for short-term speed.
- **Progression & retention** — representation can't be fixed by the hiring pipeline alone; unequal progression and high attrition (e.g., Black+ attrition is highest) matter just as much.
- **Measure equity** — don't assume equity; measure it throughout the system. Decision-makers are biased too; serving one group may disenfranchise another, often irreversibly.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Pick a recent **default or default flow** you built (default language, default avatar, a form's gender/name fields, the canonical test account). What "typical user" does it assume? Which group is left feeling unseen or unable to use it?
2. Do your **training data / test data / beta users** represent your real user population? If not, which group is missing? Per the facial-recognition lesson — is there somewhere you *should* slow down to get more complete data?
3. Apply the internal-mobility case to your own work: does your team have a feature or process that **looks efficiency-boosting but could amplify bias** (auto-ranking, highlighted scores, résumé keyword filters)? Does it pass the three equity questions (predictive? free of individual bias? standardized across orgs?)?
4. If you interview or hire: was your last **candidate slate balanced**? How do you distinguish "we're fixing the pipeline" from "we're fixing progression and retention too"? Per [[ch03]]'s psychological safety — does your team make new underrepresented members feel welcome?
5. In the "equity vs velocity" trade-off, which does your org *actually* (not rhetorically) reward? How would you make equity a measured, reviewed engineering property — like latency or availability — rather than a slogan? Per [[ch01]]'s "data-driven, falsifiable" culture.

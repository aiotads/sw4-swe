# Chapter 5 — How to Lead a Team

> *"Traditional managers worry about how to get things done, whereas great managers worry about what things get done."*
> In plain terms: a weak manager controls the *how*; a great leader picks the *what* and trusts the team to figure out the how.

## Summary

Chapters 2–4 covered the culture and composition of teams; this one turns to **the person ultimately responsible for making it all work — the leader.** Author Brian Fitzpatrick distinguishes the two leadership roles Google recognizes: a **Manager leads *people*, a Tech Lead (TL) leads *technology*.** On small teams one person does both — a **TLM (Tech Lead Manager).** A boat without a captain is just a floating waiting room; a software project is the same — without a pilot, engineers burn valuable time waiting (or worse, writing code you don't need).

The chapter's core philosophy is **servant leadership**: the new manager's biggest urge is to "manage," but the best advice is "**above all, resist the urge to manage.**" A leader's job is to *serve* the team — remove obstacles, build consensus, and create psychological safety through humility, respect, and trust (HRT) — not to apply the **carrot and stick**, an industrial-era method that's harmful to creative work. The author runs through **antipatterns** (hire pushovers, ignore low performers, ignore human issues, be everyone's friend, compromise the hiring bar, treat the team like children) against **positive patterns** (lose the ego and apologize, be a Zen master, be a catalyst, remove roadblocks, be a teacher/mentor, set clear goals, be honest without the "compliment sandwich," track happiness). It closes with "**people are like plants**": each member needs a different amount of "water" (**motivation**) and "light" (**direction**), and the leader's job is to figure out who needs what. Motivation comes down to the three drivers of intrinsic motivation — **autonomy, mastery, and purpose.**

## Why read this

- **You're an IC who got sucked into a leadership role (manageritis).** Whether you wanted it or not, if you're good at your job you'll likely end up steering at some point. This chapter isn't trying to convince you to manage — it's about what to do when it happens, and it'll help you understand your own leaders better too.
- **You just became a TL/TLM and end each day feeling "I did nothing today."** It confronts the loss head-on: you can no longer point at a pile of code and say "that's what I did." Stop "counting apples while growing bananas" — the *output* of management is a happy, productive team, measured differently.
- **You have a low performer and you keep "hoping they'll improve."** The chapter uses the SRE motto "**hope is not a strategy**" to puncture that procrastination and gives a concrete, two-month, measurable "help them up or out" process — and explains why ignoring it drives your *high* performers away.
- **You want to be a leader people stay for and take risks for.** From "lose the ego and sincerely apologize," through the gear-chain "the leader is always on stage" effect, to "people are like plants" differentiated motivation and autonomy/mastery/purpose — it turns abstract "leadership" into things you can use in your next 1:1.

## Key details

### Managers and Tech Leads (and Both)
The core claim: every engineering team has a leader, but they're acquired in different ways. Google recognizes two roles with **similar planning skills but wildly different *people* skills**:
- **The Engineering Manager:** Google decided early that its engineering managers should have an **engineering background** (hire ex-engineers as managers, or train engineers into managers). At the highest level, a manager is responsible for the **performance, productivity, and happiness** of everyone on the team (including the TL), while meeting the business's needs — and since business and individual needs don't always align, this puts managers in a hard spot.
- **The Tech Lead (TL):** usually reports to the team's manager; owns the technical side (technology decisions, architecture, priorities, velocity, project management). Most TLs are also ICs, forcing a choice between doing something quickly themselves or delegating to a teammate (sometimes more slowly) — and **as the team grows, delegating is usually the right call.**
- **The Tech Lead Manager (TLM):** the default on small/nascent teams — one person handling both people and tech. Google's custom for large, established teams is a **pair of leaders (one TL, one manager)**, because doing both well without burning out is really hard. The TLM role is tricky and needs heavy mentoring from experienced TLMs (Google recommends a new TLM find a senior mentor in addition to taking classes).

Takeaway: don't conflate "management" with "technical leadership" — the planning is similar, the human side is not.

### Case Study: Influencing Without Authority
The core claim: getting your reports to do work is easy; getting people **outside your org — or even your product area** — to do what you think needs doing is different. This "**influence without authority**" is one of the most powerful leadership traits you can develop.
- **Jeff Dean** led only a fraction of Google's engineering team for years, yet his influence on technical decisions reaches the entire org and beyond.
- **The Data Liberation Front:** with fewer than half a dozen engineers, the author got **50+ Google products** to export their data via **Google Takeout** — with no formal executive directive. How? **Identify a strategic need, show how it links to the company's mission and existing priorities, and build a tool that lets teams integrate quickly and easily.**

Takeaway: influence doesn't come from a title — it comes from aligning to the mission and lowering the friction for others to help.

### Moving from an IC to a Leadership Role
The core claim: someone has to get in the driver's seat for the product to go anywhere; if you're the motivated, impatient type, that someone might be you. You get sucked into resolving conflicts, making decisions, coordinating people — an affliction some call **manageritis**. The chapter isn't here to convince you to manage, but to show **why the best leaders serve their team using HRT.**

### The Only Thing to Fear Is…Well, Everything
The core claim: there are many reasons people avoid managing.
- **Less coding, hard-to-quantify output:** you end a busy day thinking "I didn't do a damned thing." It's like "growing bananas but counting apples" — **don't fall into that trap**; making your team happy and productive is itself a big measure of the job.
- **The Peter Principle:** "in a hierarchy every employee tends to rise to his level of incompetence." Google avoids it by requiring you to **perform above your level (to "exceed expectations") before being promoted** to it.
- **Good reasons to lead:** first, it's a way to **scale yourself** — there's an upper limit to the code one person writes, but imagine what a great team writes under your leadership. Second, **you might just be good at it.** Someone has to lead — why not you?

> Warning (a footnote): don't force people into management as a career path — forcing a great engineer who has no desire to manage into the role means you **lose a great engineer and gain a crappy manager.** That's not just a bad idea; it's actively harmful.

### Servant Leadership
The core claim: many managers catch a "disease" — they forget the awful things their managers did and start doing the same (symptoms: micromanaging, ignoring low performers, hiring pushovers). The cure is a liberal dose of **servant leadership**: the most important thing a leader does is *serve* the team, like a butler tending a household.
- The best advice came from engineering director Steve Vinter: "**Above all, resist the urge to manage.**"
- A servant leader creates an atmosphere of HRT — removing bureaucratic obstacles the team can't, helping reach consensus, even buying dinner during late nights; they fill in the cracks and aren't afraid to get their hands dirty. **The only thing they "manage" is the team's technical *and* social health — and the social health is just as important, but often far harder to manage.**

### The Engineering Manager (history and today)
- **Manager Is a Four-Letter Word:** the pointy-haired-manager concept is a carryover from military hierarchy, adopted by the Industrial Revolution — factories needed (usually unskilled, replaceable) workers, so managers had little motivation to treat them well. This "**carrot and stick**" (mule-driver) approach survived into the modern office. But **studies repeatedly show it's ineffective and harmful for creative workers**: an assembly-line worker could be trained in days and replaced at will, but an engineer on a large codebase takes months to ramp up — they need nurturing, time, and space to think and create.
- **Today's Engineering Manager:** framed in HRT — **if a manager makes it obvious they trust an employee, the employee feels positive pressure to live up to it.** If you remember one thing: "**Traditional managers worry about *how* things get done; great managers worry about *what* gets done** (and trust their team for the how)." The Jerry story: his old manager insisted on 9-to-5 at his desk; on day one Jerry stammered an apology for leaving 15 minutes early, and the author said "as long as you get your job done, I don't care what time you leave." **Treat people like adults and they get the work done.** If someone needs babysitting to work, *that* is the real problem.
- **Failure Is an Option:** another way to catalyze a team is **psychological safety** so they can take bigger risks. Most humans are bad at evaluating risk and most companies avoid it, defaulting to conservative small wins. A Google saying: **try an impossible goal and you'll likely fail, but "failing at the impossible" usually achieves far more than succeeding at something you already knew you could do.** Rules: failure is learning, not blame; failing fast is good (little at stake), failing slow is more painful, failing in a customer-affecting way is least desirable (but has postmortem structure to learn from). For individuals: **praise success in public, give constructive criticism in private** (public criticism is ineffective, defensive-making, and usually just cruel).

### Antipatterns
The core claim: first, the destructive patterns to **avoid**:
- **Hire Pushovers:** an insecure manager hires people who aren't as smart or ambitious to cement authority — but then **the team can't move without you and you can't take a vacation.** Instead, **hire people smarter than you who can replace you**: they'll challenge you and consistently impress you, freeing you to lead more, take vacation, and grow.
- **Ignore Low Performers:** "sometimes you're the tooth fairy, other times the dentist." The SRE motto "**hope is not a strategy**" is most abused here. Ignore it and high performers carry the load, morale leaks, and eventually the high performers leave, leaving a team of those who *can't*. Dealing with it quickly puts you in a position to **help them up or out**: set a clear time frame (e.g., two months) and small, measurable goals, and check weekly. Coaching a low performer is like **helping a limping person walk, then jog, then run alongside the team** — it needs temporary micromanagement but lots of HRT, **especially respect.**
- **Ignore Human Issues:** managers (mostly promoted from technical jobs) tend to neglect the human side. The Jake/Katie/Pablo story: new dad Jake worked from home for a few weeks, just as productive, and his coworker Katie was fine — but his remote manager Pablo said "people have kids all the time, you need to go into the office." Jake was enraged and lost respect for Pablo. **A little empathy would have gone a long way.**
- **Be Everyone's Friend:** most people's first leadership role is managing former peers. **Don't confuse friendship with leading with a soft touch**: when you hold power over someone's career, they may feel pressure to artificially reciprocate friendship. You can lead and build consensus without being a close friend (or a hard-ass); **having lunch with the team** keeps you connected without discomfort.
- **Compromise the Hiring Bar:** Steve Jobs: "**A people hire other A people; B people hire C people.**" Easiest to fall for when hiring fast (pick the "best 5" of the applicants regardless of the bar) — the fastest way to build a mediocre team. The cost of finding the right person is tiny compared to the cost of one you never should have hired. **Fight tooth and nail for quality; if you keep getting substandard hires, maybe it's time to look for another job.**
- **Treat Your Team Like Children:** people act the way you treat them. Micromanaging or disrespecting their abilities tells them you don't trust them. If you *permanently* need to micromanage someone, you have a **hiring failure.** Even office supplies show trust: Google's self-service supply cabinets and "Tech Stops" trust employees to take what they need, so they "Do The Right Thing" — the cost of a childlike workforce or time wasted formally requesting cheap supplies far exceeds a few pens and USB cables.

### Positive Patterns
The core claim: positive patterns learned from Google, other leaders, and the authors' own mentors:
- **Lose the Ego:** not being a doormat — humility isn't lack of confidence. Cultivate a strong **collective team ego**. Trust the team (the people in the trenches know the details better than you): you drive consensus and direction, but leave the "how" to the people building the product — giving them **ownership and accountability.** New leaders feel they must have all the answers — you won't, and faking it loses respect fast. **Appreciate inquiry**, accept criticism openly, don't be territorial. The hardest part: **apologize when you make a mistake, and mean it** — it costs nothing, people deeply respect leaders who own mistakes, and (contrary to belief) it doesn't make you vulnerable; it earns respect.
- **Be a Zen Master:** an engineer's skepticism/cynicism is a liability in leadership. **The more people you lead, the more you must mediate your reactions and stay calm**, because the team looks to you for cues. Picture the org chart as a **chain of gears**: the IC is a tiny gear with a few teeth, the CEO a huge one with hundreds — the higher you are, a small movement spins the gears below wildly, whether you intend it or not. Hence: "**the leader is always on stage**" — your visible attitude about everything is unconsciously noticed and spreads. VP Bill Coughran never panicked — arm across chest, chin in hand, asking questions of a panicked engineer to refocus them. Which leads to the Zen trick: **ask questions.** When someone asks for advice, don't leap into "solution mode" — they want help solving it *themselves*; use HRT to ask questions and lead them to *their* answer (back to ownership). Socrates would be proud.
- **Be a Catalyst:** a catalyst accelerates a reaction without being consumed. The most common thing a leader does is **build consensus** — driving the whole process or just nudging it. Consensus-building is how unofficial leaders lead *without authority*; even with authority, dictating is less effective than building consensus. (Chasing 100% consensus is also harmful — you must be able to proceed amid some uncertainty.)
- **Remove Roadblocks:** some roadblocks are nearly impossible for the team but trivial for you. A team stuck with Legal for weeks had it solved by its manager in two hours — he **knew whom to contact**; another couldn't get server resources, and the manager sorted it that afternoon. "**Knowing the right person is often more valuable than knowing the right answer.**"
- **Be a Teacher and a Mentor:** one of the hardest things is watching a junior spend 3 hours on what you could do in 20 minutes — but teaching and letting people learn is vital. Mentoring needs no formal training, mainly three things: experience with the team's processes/systems, the ability to explain, and (most important) **the ability to gauge how much help the mentee needs** — over-explain and they'll tune you out.
- **Set Clear Goals:** obvious, yet widely ignored. Picture the product as a big truck with each member holding a rope tied to the front — you want everyone pulling *north*, not every which way. The easiest way is a **concise mission statement**; then step back, give autonomy, and check in periodically. Teams without clear goals waste energy each pulling slightly differently.
- **Be Honest:** you'll inevitably have to say "I can't tell you that" or deliver news people don't want. One manager tells new hires: "I won't lie to you, but I'll tell you when I can't tell you something or just don't know." **Strongly avoid the "compliment sandwich"** (criticism between two compliments) — most people only hear the compliments and miss the critical message that *something needs to change.* Use respect: be **kind and empathetic but direct.** The Tim story: his old manager overused the compliment sandwich and Tim never changed; the new manager said plainly (but not cruelly) "the way you interact with the team is alienating them, and we're committed to helping you fix it" — Tim improved dramatically within weeks. The Dean story: Ben used the metaphor "every decision is like **a train coming through every 15 minutes — you can't jump in front of every one**; pick the ones that really matter," helping Dean see the effect of his "train stopping." **Delivery is key to whether a message is heard or deflected.**
- **Track Happiness:** the best leaders are amateur psychologists. A concrete trick: end every 1:1 with "**What do you need?**" — a great way to ensure each person has what they need to be productive and happy. (The author froze when CEO Eric Schmidt asked him this in their first meeting — he was ready next time.) Watch happiness *outside* the office too (a colleague, Mekka, has reports rate their happiness 1–10), and don't assume people have no life outside work. Tracking happiness also means **tracking careers**: ask "where's your career in five years?" — most shrug, but everyone wants to be promoted, learn something new, launch something important, and work with smart people. **Make those implicit goals explicit** so career advice has real metrics.

### Other Tips and Tricks
- **Delegate, but get your hands dirty:** new leaders do everything themselves; veteran leaders do nothing. New leaders must work to delegate (even if it's slower) — it's how the team learns; when picking up a new team, **take on a grungy task no one wants** to earn respect fastest.
- **Seek to replace yourself:** unless you want the same job forever — hire people smarter than you, then give them responsibility and occasional leadership; you'll quickly see who can and wants to lead. (Some people prefer to be high-performing ICs — that's OK.)
- **Know when to make waves:** for hard situations where every cell screams "do nothing" (the under-par engineer, the train-stopper, the 30-hour-a-week employee) — **don't tell yourself "it'll work itself out."** These are exactly when to make the biggest waves, *now* — waiting only causes more damage.
- **Shield your team from chaos / give air cover:** outside your team is a world of chaos you never saw as an IC — it was always there; your previous manager shielded you. Share information, but don't distract the team with organizational craziness unlikely to affect them.
- **Let your team know when they're doing well:** new leads get so caught up in shortcomings they forget positive feedback.
- **It's easy to say "yes" to something that's easy to undo:** "spend a day trying a new library? sure" — but "launch a product we'll support for 10 years?" deserves more thought. **Good leaders have a sense for what's undoable — and more is undoable than you think** (technical *and* non-technical; see [[ch22]]).

### People Are Like Plants
The core claim: the author's mother-in-law raised six very different kids — "kids are like plants: cacti need little water but lots of sun; African violets need diffuse light and moist soil; tomatoes excel with a little fertilizer. Give six kids the same water, light, and fertilizer and that's *equal* — but odds are none gets what it actually needs." **Your team members are like plants too**: the variables are **motivation** and **direction** — motivate the one in a rut, give stronger direction to the distracted or uncertain, and both to the "adrift." But don't overdo either, or you'll just annoy them.

- **Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation:** per Dan Pink's *Drive*, the way to make people happiest and most productive isn't extrinsic (piles of cash) but raising **intrinsic motivation**, via three things:
  - **Autonomy:** the ability to act without being micromanaged. Give the general direction, let them decide how to get there — stronger ownership.
  - **Mastery:** the chance to improve existing skills and learn new ones. Skills are like a knife's blade — hire the sharpest, but use it for years without sharpening and it dulls.
  - **Purpose:** make the work meaningful. Many work on significant products but are kept at arm's length from the positive effects; one manager forwards customer thank-you emails straight to the team — big boost to motivation.

Takeaway: leadership is **differentiated** allocation of motivation and direction, not uniform "fairness." It's the operating frame for landing HRT in every 1:1.

## Key terms

- **Servant leadership** — the leader's most important job is to serve the team (remove obstacles, build consensus, create HRT), not control it. Mnemonic: "resist the urge to manage."
- **Manager vs Tech Lead vs TLM** — a Manager leads *people*, a TL leads *technology*, a TLM does both (default on small teams; large teams tend to split into a pair to avoid burnout).
- **Influence without authority** — getting people outside your org to do what needs doing; via alignment to the mission + lowering friction (e.g., the Data Liberation Front / Takeout).
- **Counting apples while growing bananas** — the fallacy of measuring management work by an IC's output. Management's output is the team's happiness and productivity.
- **Peter Principle** — employees tend to rise to their level of incompetence. Google guards against it by requiring you to "exceed expectations" at the higher level *before* promotion.
- **Carrot and stick** — the factory-era mule-driver style; ineffective and harmful for creative engineers who take months to ramp up.
- **Hope is not a strategy** — SRE motto, applied to low performers: don't *hope* they improve — set measurable goals and help them up or out.
- **Compliment sandwich** — criticism wrapped in two compliments. Strongly discouraged: the key message gets drowned out; use "kind but direct" instead.
- **The leader is always on stage** — a leadership position is constantly watched; your visible attitude is amplified through the "gear chain" and spreads to the team.
- **Autonomy / Mastery / Purpose** — Dan Pink's three drivers of intrinsic motivation (from *Drive*); raising intrinsic motivation beats throwing money.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Recall a recent day you finished feeling "I did nothing today." Were you "counting apples while growing bananas"? If you scored "did my team get happier and more productive today?", would the answer change?
2. Is there a low performer you're "hoping will improve"? Apply the SRE line — will you give them a two-month, small, measurable, weekly-checked "up or out" plan? What's the cost to your *high* performers of waiting?
3. Was your last piece of hard feedback wrapped in a **compliment sandwich**? Did they actually hear *what needs to change*? Rewrite it "kind but direct." Per [[ch02]]'s "you are not your code."
4. Picture each direct report as a plant: who lacks "water" (motivation), who lacks "light" (direction), who lacks both (adrift)? Last time, did you give them what they actually needed, or treat everyone identically?
5. Name a goal your team cares about right now. Is everyone really pulling the same "north"? Do you have a writable **mission statement**? Per [[ch06]] (leading at scale) and [[ch07]] (measuring direction).

# Chapter 14 — Larger Testing

> *"Larger tests cover things unit tests cannot. … A good design includes a test strategy that identifies risks and larger tests that mitigate them."*
> Unit tests aren't enough to know the whole system works — match each risk with a larger test that mitigates it.

## Summary

Unit tests are the foundation, but **they alone can't confirm the whole system works.** This chapter is about **larger tests** — an essential part of a healthy risk-mitigation strategy. Free of small-test constraints, they may be **slow** (default timeout 15 minutes or 1 hour; some run for hours or days), **nonhermetic** (sharing resources), and **nondeterministic.** Their primary reason for existing is **fidelity**: how well a test reflects the real behavior of the **SUT (system under test)** — and higher fidelity brings higher cost and risk (production is highest-fidelity *and* riskiest).

Larger tests fill five systematic gaps in unit tests: **① unfaithful doubles** (mocks that disagree with and grow stale against the real implementation), **② configuration issues** (config is Google's #1 cause of major outages; a 2013 untested network-config push caused a global outage), **③ issues under load** (performance/load/stress), **④ unanticipated behaviors and inputs** (Hyrum's Law: the effective contract is all visible behaviors), and **⑤ emergent behaviors and the "vacuum effect"** (unit tests are like theoretical physics in a vacuum — clean but divorced from reality). But larger tests also violate the "reliable, fast, scalable" virtues, and face two challenges: **ownership** and **lack of standardization** (which gets them skipped during LSCs).

A large test has four phases: **obtain a SUT → seed test data → perform actions on the SUT → verify behaviors.** A SUT is judged on two often-conflicting factors: **hermeticity and fidelity**; its forms range from single-process, single-machine, and multimachine to shared (staging/production) and hybrid. Verification comes in three forms: **manual, assertions, A/B diff.** The book lists ten kinds of large test — the most common at Google being the **A/B diff regression test** (since 2001). Finally, integrating large tests into the workflow: **make "the smallest possible test," replace sleeps with polling, drive out flakiness, make failures understandable, and give every large test a clear owner.**

## Why read this

- **You've written many unit tests but still can't say "the whole system works."** This names unit testing's **five systematic blind spots** (unfaithful doubles, configuration, load, unanticipated behavior, emergent/vacuum effects) — especially that **configuration is Google's #1 cause of major outages.** Now you know which risks demand larger tests.
- **Your test pyramid has become an "ice cream cone."** The chapter explains why: **starting with manual testing** (assuming code lives only minutes) lets manual e2e accumulate and dominate, and if the code is hard to unit-test, you've "created legacy code within days." The cure is to **move toward the pyramid in the first few days.**
- **You want to know which kinds of larger tests exist and what risk each mitigates.** The chapter gives a "SUT / data / verification" recipe for ten types (functional, perf/load, deployment config, exploratory, A/B diff, UAT, prober/canary, DiRT/chaos, user evaluation) — exactly the **test strategy** a Test Engineer must design.
- **Your larger tests are slow, flaky, unowned, and unintelligible when they fail.** The chapter gives field rules: **poll instead of time-based sleep, lower internal timeouts, prebuild dependencies, trace cross-process calls with Dapper, give failures context and a contact, and give every test an OWNERS** — or large tests "rot."

## Key details

### What Are Larger Tests?
The core claim: small tests are restricted to **one thread, one process, one machine**; larger tests aren't, so they may be **slow, nonhermetic, nondeterministic.** Why have them? Because you actually "run the real binary and try it out" to confirm your code works and that an upgrade (say, a new Google Maps API version) didn't break compatibility — **unit tests give confidence in individual functions/modules; larger tests give confidence that the overall system works as intended**, and automated tests scale in ways manual testing can't.

#### Fidelity
The core claim: the **primary reason larger tests exist is fidelity** — how well a test reflects the SUT's real behavior. By **environment**: a unit test bundles a test with a small slice of code (tested, but very unlike how production runs); production is highest-fidelity; a spectrum sits between. **Higher fidelity brings higher cost and (for production) higher risk of failure** — the key is finding the right fit. By **content**: handcrafted test data that looks unrealistic gets dismissed; data copied from production is most faithful, but creating realistic traffic before launch is hard (especially in AI, where seed data carries intrinsic bias). Handcrafted data covers a narrow range and reflects author bias — what it misses is the tests' **fidelity gap.**

#### Common Gaps in Unit Tests
The core claim: larger tests fill five gaps:
- **Unfaithful doubles:** a replacement may disagree with the doubled thing; mocks are usually defined by the engineer writing the unit test, who often didn't write the mocked thing and may misunderstand its real behavior — **a mistaken contract is an invalid one**; and mocks go **stale** (the real implementation changes with no signal to update the mock). (Mostly alleviated if teams provide fakes for their own services — see [[ch13]].)
- **Configuration issues:** a binary isn't self-sufficient (deployment config, starter scripts, config databases). **Configuration is Google's #1 cause of major outages** — a 2013 "never tested" bad network-config push caused a global outage. Config is written in config languages (not production languages), rolls out faster, and is harder to test. **Put config under version control too**, so bugs trace back to it and it can be built into large tests.
- **Issues under load:** performance/load/stress tests often send large traffic to a binary (ads real-time bidding can be thousands to millions of QPS) — hard in a typical unit-test model.
- **Unanticipated behaviors/inputs/side effects:** unit tests are limited by the author's imagination, testing only anticipated behaviors; user-found issues are mostly *unanticipated.* **Hyrum's Law**: even at 100% conformance to a stated contract, the effective user contract is **all visible behaviors** (see [[ch01]]).
- **Emergent behaviors and the "vacuum effect":** unit tests deliberately eliminate the chaos of real dependencies, network, and data for speed and reliability — **like theoretical physics in a vacuum: clean, but missing certain defect categories.**

### Why Not Have Larger Tests?
The core claim: a developer-friendly test must be **reliable** (not flaky, useful pass/fail), **fast** (doesn't interrupt the workflow), and **scalable** (efficient in pre/post-submit) — good unit tests have all three; **larger tests often violate all three** (more infrastructure → flakier; slower to set up and run; hard to scale due to resources, time, and collisions). Two more challenges: **ownership** (spanning multiple units/owners; without a clear owner a test rots) and **lack of standardization** (teams use different platforms/languages/frameworks; this gets large tests skipped during LSCs ([[ch22]]) and makes them unteachable to newcomers).

### Larger Tests and Time / Scale
The core claim: the right test types change with code lifespan. The **ice cream cone antipattern** often arises from **starting with manual testing** (assuming code lives minutes); manual e2e accumulates and dominates, and if the code is hard to unit-test, all you can write is e2e — **creating legacy code within days.** The healthy path is to **move toward the pyramid in the first few days** (make unit tests a submission requirement), then add automated integration tests and reduce manual e2e.

At scale: a microservice system's interconnections form a graph, and **each added node multiplies (even exponentially/combinatorially) the end-to-end paths** (the book's social-network SUT — 2 user groups, 2 UIs, 3 databases, an indexing pipeline, 6 servers, 14 edges — is already hard to enumerate), so you must switch to other strategies. But their value rises too: the **multiplicative effect of fidelity** — replacing two servers with low-fidelity doubles (say 10% accurate each) makes a bug 99% likely (1 − 0.1×0.1). So large tests must be **feasible at scale yet retain high fidelity.** **"The smallest possible test"**: even for integration tests, smaller is better; use **chained tests** — persist one test's output as another's input, representing the overall scenario with smaller pairwise integration tests.

### Structure of a Large Test
The core claim: most large tests follow a four-phase workflow — **① obtain a SUT → ② seed necessary test data → ③ perform actions on the SUT → ④ verify behaviors.**

- **The System Under Test (SUT):** judged on two **often-conflicting** factors — **hermeticity** (isolation from other components/traffic; high hermeticity minimizes concurrency and infrastructure flakiness) and **fidelity** (resemblance to production binaries, config, topology). The SUT's scope drives the test's scope. Forms: **single-process** (all in one binary; least faithful to production topology), **single-machine** (multiple binaries, one machine; medium tests), **multimachine** (across machines; highest fidelity, most network/machine flakiness), **shared environments (staging/production)** (lowest cost but conflicts, waits for the code to ship, and production risks users), **hybrids** (some run, some shared — practically mandatory at Google's scale).
  - **Benefits of hermetic SUTs:** in-production tests have no environment overhead but **can't run until code reaches production, so they can't block release** (too late); shared staging only runs when code is available, and "reserving" time doesn't scale. The next step is **cloud-isolated / machine-hermetic SUTs**, avoiding conflicts and reservations.
  - **Case Study | Webdriver Torso:** to verify YouTube production video rendering, scripts uploaded test videos to a **public** YouTube channel; *Wired* reported it, the media tried to solve the mystery, and Google eventually came clean with a Rickroll and Easter Egg — a reminder to **expect users to discover any test data you put in production.**
  - **Shrink the SUT at problem boundaries:** UI tests are notoriously unreliable and costly (brittle to look-and-feel changes, hard async behaviors) — if the backend has a public API, split tests at the **UI/API boundary** and drive e2e via the API. **Third-party dependencies** are another seam to split (don't let automated tests hit a real third-party API). Replacing databases with in-memory ones and dropping unneeded servers can shrink the SUT to a single machine (the most stable, easiest "integration" tests).
  - **Record/replay proxies:** you can double whole servers, but there's no guarantee the double honors the real contract. Industry uses **consumer-driven contract tests** (Pact, Spring Cloud Contracts); Google's heavy protobuf use means it uses **record/replay** instead: record traffic to external services during the large test (**Record Mode**, run continuously post-submit; must pass to generate logs) and replay during development/presubmit (**Replay Mode**). Nondeterminism means requests are matched by a matcher (like stub/mock argument matching). When client behavior changes a lot you must re-record — so make Record easy, fast, stable.

- **Test Data:** large tests need two kinds — **seeded data** (the SUT's state at the test's start) and **test traffic** (data sent during execution). Seeding is often orders of magnitude more complex than unit-test setup: **domain data** (prefilled config; missing it, a binary may fail to start), **realistic baseline** (a social network needs a realistic social graph), **seeding APIs** (writing directly to a datastore may bypass triggers/checks). Generated by **handcrafted, copied** (from production, e.g., map data), or **sampled** ("smart sampling" — minimum data for maximum coverage).

- **Verification:** three ways — **manual** (regression and exploratory; doesn't scale sublinearly — bigger system, more human time), **assertions** (`assertThat(response.Contains("Colossal Cave"))`), **A/B comparison** (run two SUTs, send the same data, compare outputs; expected behavior isn't stated, so a human must judge whether diffs are intended).

### Types of Larger Tests
The core claim: combine SUT/data/verification into tests that each mitigate different risks. The **test strategy** (the heart of a test plan) identifies the primary risk vectors and the tests to mitigate them — exactly what Google's **Test Engineer** role designs.
- **Functional (1+ interacting binaries):** SUT single-machine hermetic or cloud-isolated; handcrafted data; assertions. In microservices, bring up all relevant binaries and interact via a published API.
- **Browser / Device:** a special case of functional — the public API is the app itself; interacting via the frontend as a third party adds coverage.
- **Performance / Load / Stress:** SUT cloud-isolated; data handcrafted or multiplexed from production; diff (perf metrics). Load/stress are "highly emergent" system properties, so look as close to production as possible; tune deployment topology or run both versions on one machine to cut noise.
- **Deployment Configuration:** SUT single-machine hermetic or cloud-isolated; no data; assertions (doesn't crash). Really a smoke test of the SUT — starts successfully → passes.
- **Exploratory:** SUT production/shared staging; production or a known test universe; manual. Trained users explore new paths, looking for questionable behavior/security flaws (a manual "fuzz"); the **bug bash** (everyone manually tests together) is common. Doesn't scale sublinearly; replicate found bugs as automated tests.
- **A/B Diff Regression:** SUT two cloud-isolated environments; data usually multiplexed/sampled from production; A/B diff. **Google's most common large test** (concept dates to 1998; Google's been running it since 2001 for Ads/Search/Maps). Variants: **A-A** (find noise/flakiness), **A-B-C**. Challenges: **approval** (someone must judge whether diffs are expected), **noise**, **coverage** (generating enough traffic), **setup** (two SUTs double the complexity).
- **UAT (User Acceptance Testing):** SUT machine-hermetic or cloud-isolated; handcrafted data; assertions. Counters "Working as implemented ≠ Working as intended" (unit tests written by the coder embed the same misunderstandings); written in Cucumber/RSpec as "runnable specifications." **Google does little of it** (products built by engineers who already speak the coding languages).
- **Probers / Canary Analysis:** SUT production; production data; assertions and metric A/B diff. A **prober** runs read-only, deterministic smoke assertions against production (search google.com, verify a result returns); **canary analysis** compares health metrics of canary vs baseline during a staged rollout. Limits: issues caught already affect users; write actions mutate production.
- **Disaster Recovery / Chaos Engineering:** SUT production; production + injected faults; manual and metric A/B diff. Google's annual **DiRT (Disaster Recovery Testing)** injects faults at near-planetary scale (once simulated an earthquake isolating the Mountain View HQ); **chaos engineering** (popularized by Netflix) continuously injects background faults; Google uses homegrown **Catzilla**, thousands of times a week.
- **User Evaluation:** SUT production; production data; manual and metric A/B diff. **Dogfooding** (staff use it first), **experimentation** (unwitting subgroup vs control — the famous AdWords ad-background-shading experiment that lifted clicks), **rater evaluation** (humans score "better/worse," vital for search/ML systems with no single right answer).

### Large Tests and the Developer Workflow
The core claim: even if standard unit-test infrastructure doesn't apply, **integrate larger tests into the workflow.** Many don't belong in **TAP** (nonhermetic, too flaky, too resource-intensive), so they get a **separate post-submit continuous build**, with presubmit encouraged (direct feedback). A/B diff tests needing manual blessing can be folded in (a code-review requirement to approve UI diffs; some auto-file release-blocking bugs for unresolved diffs). Tests too large/painful run only post-submit and at release — the cost is taint enters the monorepo and you must hunt the culprit to roll back, a trade-off among developer pain, change latency, and continuous-build reliability.

- **Authoring:** clear libraries, docs, examples. Google reuses assertion libraries and built libraries for SUT interaction, A/B diffs, seeding data, and orchestrating workflows. A/B diff and production SUTs are popular because they have **lower human verification/maintenance cost** — but viewed holistically, if reconciling diffs or safeguarding production testing outweighs the savings, it's ineffective.
- **Speeding up:** engineers don't wait for slow tests. The best speedup is **reducing scope or splitting into two parallel smaller tests**; **poll (near-microsecond, with a timeout) / use event handlers / subscribe to notifications instead of time-based sleeps** (sleep-based tests all fail when the test fleet is overloaded, spiraling); **lower internal timeouts/delays** and **optimize build time** (prebuilt known-good dependencies, closer to production's "different services at different versions").
- **Driving out flakiness:** for large tests, flakiness can make them unusable — make it a high priority. Start by **reducing scope** (a hermetic, single-machine SUT has the least risk); event-driven design both speeds up and de-flakes; internal timeouts are a trade-off (higher reduces flakiness, lower can increase it). A subtle trap: production gracefully handles internal issues to protect users (no ad served rather than a 500), which looks broken to a test runner — make the failure mode obvious and timeouts tunable for tests.
- **Making tests understandable:** a good failing large test should **① clearly state what failed** (not just "Assertion failed" + a stack trace, but "expected 10 search results but got 1"), **② minimize effort to find the root cause** (stack traces are useless across processes — use **Dapper**: one request ID correlating all logs in an RPC chain), and **③ provide support and contact info.**
- **Owning:** large tests **must have documented owners** (who review changes and support failures), or they get harder to modify, slower to fix, and rot. Project integration tests → the project lead; cross-service feature tests → a feature owner (engineer, PM, or test engineer). Automate via structured info: **OWNERS files ([[ch09]])** or **per-test annotations.**

## Key terms

- **Larger test** — a test free of small-test constraints (one thread/process/machine); may be slow, nonhermetic, nondeterministic; fills unit tests' systematic gaps as part of risk mitigation.
- **Fidelity** — how well a test (environment and content) reflects the SUT's real behavior; the primary reason larger tests exist. More fidelity ⇒ more cost and risk.
- **SUT (system under test)** — the core of a large test; judged on hermeticity and fidelity (often conflicting); forms: single-process / single-machine / multimachine / shared / hybrid.
- **Hermeticity** — the SUT's isolation from other components/traffic; high hermeticity minimizes concurrency and infrastructure flakiness, often at the cost of fidelity.
- **Ice cream cone** — the many-e2e, few-unit antipattern; often from "starting with manual testing," creating legacy code within days. Cure: move toward the pyramid early (see [[ch11]]).
- **A/B diff regression test** — run two SUT versions, send the same traffic, compare outputs; Google's most common large test (since 2001); variants A-A (find noise/flakiness), A-B-C. Challenges: approval, noise, coverage, setup.
- **Record/replay** — record traffic to external services during a large test (Record Mode, post-submit), replay in development/presubmit (Replay Mode); Google's choice (protobuf-heavy) over consumer-driven contract tests.
- **Seeded data vs test traffic** — the SUT's starting state (domain data, realistic baseline) vs data sent during execution; generated handcrafted/copied/sampled.
- **Prober / canary analysis** — a prober runs read-only deterministic smoke assertions against production; canary analysis compares canary vs baseline health metrics during a staged rollout.
- **DiRT / chaos engineering / Catzilla** — DiRT is Google's annual disaster drill (planetary-scale fault injection); chaos engineering (Netflix) continuously injects background faults; Catzilla is Google's homegrown tool, thousands a week.
- **Dogfooding / experimentation / rater evaluation** — three production user-evaluation methods; experimentation's famous case is the AdWords ad-background-shading experiment that lifted clicks.
- **Dapper** — Google's tracing framework correlating all logs in an RPC chain by one request ID; lets cross-process large-test failures be traced to a root cause.
- **Test Engineer** — Google's specialized role; a core skill is designing a product's "test strategy" (risk vectors, matching larger tests).

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Inventory your system's "risk vectors": which are **structurally beyond unit tests** (unfaithful doubles? configuration? load? unanticipated inputs? emergent behavior)? Do you have a **test strategy** matching each major risk to a larger test?
2. Is your configuration (config/deploy scripts/feature flags) under **version control** and **tested**? Recall Google's "config is the #1 cause of major outages / 2013 global outage" — was your last incident code or config?
3. Does your pyramid look like a pyramid or an **ice cream cone**? Did your project "start with manual testing and create legacy code within days"? Which manual e2e tests should become automated integration tests? Per [[ch11]].
4. Take your biggest, most painful e2e test: can its **SUT** shrink (split at the UI/API boundary? record/replay for third parties? in-memory databases)? On **hermeticity vs fidelity**, where are you now and where should you move?
5. When your large tests fail, can others understand why (does the message have context? cross-process tracing like Dapper? a known contact)? Does each have a **clear owner**? Does it run presubmit, post-submit, or only at release — is that trade-off right for your team?

# Chapter 7 — Measuring Engineering Productivity

> *"Success means giving a stakeholder the data they need to make a decision."*
> In plain terms: measurement isn't about proving you're right — it's about handing the decider what they need to decide.

## Summary

This closes the "Culture" part, on how Google measures and improves engineering productivity **with data, not intuition.** Author Ciera Jaspan is from Google's dedicated engineering-productivity research team — which deliberately mixes in social scientists (cognitive psychology, behavioral economics), because what needs studying isn't just the software artifacts engineers produce but the **human side** (motivation, incentive structures, strategies for complex tasks).

One real case runs through the chapter: **is the readability process** (from [[ch03]]) **even worth it?** The process has three gates:
1. **Triage — is it even worth measuring?** Measurement is expensive and can even change engineers' behavior in ways that mask the truth. Write the question concretely, then ask five things — what result do you expect and why? If data supports it, what action follows? If you get a **negative** result, will you act? Who decides, and when? Many projects die on "a negative result wouldn't actually change the decision" — so don't measure. **Only measure when a concrete decision will be made on the outcome.**
2. **Pick meaningful metrics: the GSM framework.** Goal (the end result you want to understand, no measurement specifics) → Signal (how you'd know you achieved it, maybe unmeasurable) → Metric (a measurable proxy for the signal). Defining top-down avoids the **streetlight effect** (using only what's easy to measure), avoids picking metrics post hoc, and preserves **traceability.** Goals should cover the five facets of productivity — **QUANTS** (Quality, Attention, iNtellectual complexity, Tempo/velocity, Satisfaction) — which trade off against each other, so you don't speed up reviews while tanking quality ("I can make your review velocity very fast: just remove code reviews entirely").
3. **Validate metrics with data, then act.** Quantitative metrics give scale and confidence but no context or narrative; only qualitative research explains *why.* When they disagree, **it's usually the quantitative metric that's wrong.** The readability verdict: **worth it** — authors with readability get reviewed and submit faster (even accounting for needing fewer reviewers), are satisfied, and feel they learned.

## Why read this

- **You want to push a "productivity-improving" tool or process change but can't prove it to your boss.** This gives you GSM — a defensible method: state the goal, derive signals and metrics, and get stakeholders to agree *in advance* "these are the right metrics," killing the post-hoc "let's use different metrics" argument.
- **You or your boss love LOC, commit counts, or bugs-closed as productivity metrics.** The chapter quotes Dijkstra that LOC is "**lines spent**," not "produced," and warns about the streetlight effect and how **using productivity metrics for individual performance reviews** gets gamed instantly, destroying their value.
- **You start collecting data before thinking about "what for."** Triage's five questions force "what action follows the result?" — if the answer is "nothing changes," the measurement was a waste from the start. **Success in measurement = the decider actually used the data.**
- **You only trust "hard data" and dismiss "anecdotes/surveys."** The chapter shows quant + qual "triangulation": the median-build-latency blunder (automated builds wrongly counted) was caught only by qualitative sampling. Qualitative metrics are metrics too — and often the thing that saves your quantitative ones.

## Key details

### Why Should We Measure Engineering Productivity?
The core claim: as an organization grows **linearly**, communication costs grow **quadratically** (citing Brooks' *Mythical Man-Month*). So adding people expands business scope, but the communication overhead doesn't scale linearly — you can't scale business scope linearly with headcount.

The other path: **make each engineer more productive** — expanding scope without the matching communication overhead. To do that: understand what makes engineers productive → find inefficiencies → fix them → repeat in a continuous-improvement loop. But **the improvement loop itself costs people**: it's not worth 50 engineer-years of effort to gain 10 engineer-years of productivity. So the goal is to improve productivity **efficiently.** Google's answer: a dedicated research team that includes social scientists and takes a data-driven approach.

Takeaway: this chapter applies the book's Scale thesis to *people* — the sublinear lever of individual productivity is the counter to "communication cost exploding quadratically."

### Triage: Is It Even Worth Measuring?
The core claim: before deciding *how* to measure, decide *whether it's worth measuring.* Measurement is expensive (people to measure, analyze, disseminate), can slow the org, and **the mere act of tracking can change engineers' behavior and mask the underlying issue.** Write what you want to measure as a **concrete question** (the more concrete, the more likely you benefit), then ask five things:

- **What result do you expect, and why?** Admit you're not neutral — stating your priors up front lets you fight bias and avoid post-hoc rationalization. (The readability team's answer was "not sure": after autoformatters and static analysis, no one was certain it was still worth it.)
- **If the data supports your expectation, what action follows?** No action → don't measure. (They'd link the research on the readability FAQ to set expectations.)
- **If you get a negative result, will appropriate action be taken?** — **this question kills most of the team's projects.** Often deciders just want to *know*, but won't change course for other reasons; then it's not worth measuring. (The readability team committed: if costs outweighed benefits or benefits were negligible, they'd **kill the process** — evaluated per language.)
- **Who decides to act on the result, and when?** Ensure the requester is empowered to act, and understand **what form of data convinces them** (interview stories? surveys? logs? complex stats?). If they don't believe that form in principle, don't measure. (Java and C++ reached out; the deciders trusted self-reports for happiness/learning but wanted "hard numbers" from logs for velocity/quality → both qual and quant needed.)

**Common reasons NOT to measure:** ① you can't afford to change the process/tools now (broader context justifies delaying); ② results will soon be invalidated (e.g., just before a reorg, or tech debt in a deprecated system); ③ the decider has unshakable opinions you can't move; ④ results would only be **vanity metrics** to support something you'd do anyway (the most common reason Google tells people not to measure); ⑤ the only available metrics are too imprecise/confounded (e.g., LOC — uninterpretable).

Takeaway: **success isn't proving a hypothesis right or wrong — it's giving the stakeholder the data they need to decide; if they won't use it, the project is a failure.**

### Selecting Meaningful Metrics with Goals and Signals
The core claim: once you decide to measure, use the **GSM framework** — LOC clearly won't do:
- **Goal:** the desired end result, phrased as a high-level property, **with no reference to any metric.** Goals aren't measurable themselves, but a good set is something everyone agrees on before moving to signals and metrics.
- **Signal:** how you'd know you achieved the goal. Signals are what we'd *like* to measure, but **may not be measurable.** Not 1:1 with goals — every goal has ≥1 signal, possibly more, and goals can share signals.
- **Metric:** a **measurable proxy** for the signal — what we can actually measure, possibly imperfect. One signal may have multiple metrics to **triangulate.**

GSM gives three benefits: ① **goals → signals → metrics** avoids the **streetlight effect** (looking for keys under the streetlight — using only easy-to-measure metrics); ② **picking metrics with a principled approach in advance** prevents metrics creep and metrics bias (otherwise, when results disappoint, stakeholders propose new metrics and you can't argue); ③ it **shows coverage gaps** — running GSM reveals signals that simply aren't measurable (that's OK — at least you know). The key is **traceability:** every metric traces back to the signal it proxies and the goal it measures.

#### Goals (the QUANTS five facets)
The core claim: first pick the *right goals.* Teams often forget the **trade-offs** within productivity, causing mismeasurement (e.g., so focused on fast reviews they forget code quality — "I can make reviews fast: just remove code reviews"). So productivity is split into five components in mutual trade-off, remembered as **QUANTS:**
- **Quality of the code:** how good is the code? Are tests good enough to prevent regressions? Does the architecture mitigate risk and change?
- **Attention from engineers:** how often do they reach flow? How much do notifications distract? Does a tool encourage context-switching?
- **iNtellectual complexity:** how much cognitive load to finish a task? The inherent complexity of the problem? Any *unnecessary* complexity?
- **Tempo and velocity:** how quickly do engineers finish tasks and ship releases? How many tasks per timeframe?
- **Satisfaction:** how happy are engineers with their tools and work? Are they burning out?

(Readability's goals spanned Quality / Intellectual / Tempo / Satisfaction; **there was no Attention goal — that's OK! Not every question involves all five.**)

#### Signals
The core claim: a signal is how you'd know you hit a goal (Table 7-1). E.g., "engineers with readability rate their code quality higher than those without"; "CLs by engineers with readability are reviewed faster than those without."

#### Metrics
The core claim: metrics are measurable proxies for signals, **not the signal itself.** E.g., to measure "is code reviewed faster after readability," use **survey data + logs data** — neither gives the underlying truth, but if **they disagree, one is probably wrong and you dig deeper; if they agree, you're more confident you've reached some truth.** Some signals may have **no metric** (currently unmeasurable) — e.g., **code quality:** academia has proposed many proxies but none truly capture it, so readability **didn't capture it quantitatively** (only asked engineers to self-rate).

### Using Data to Validate Metrics
The core claim: even after GSM, a metric may not capture its intended signal — so **validate quantitative metrics with qualitative data.**
- **The median-build-latency blunder:** to capture engineers' "typical" build-latency experience, they ran an **experience sampling study** — auto-survey on build start. Some engineers replied "I didn't start a build!" — **automated tools** were starting builds the engineers weren't blocked on, so they shouldn't count toward "typical experience." They adjusted the metric to exclude these.
- **Quant vs qual:** quantitative gives **scale and confidence** (across the whole company, over time) but **no context or narrative** — it can't explain *why* an engineer used an antiquated tool, took an unusual workflow, or bypassed a standard process. Only qualitative studies provide that and the next steps. Google's experience: **when quant and qual disagree, it's usually the quantitative that missed the expected result.**
- **Three data sources:** readability combined ① a **readability-specific survey** (filled right after the process — avoids recall bias but introduces recency and sampling bias), ② a large-scale **quarterly survey** (tracking non-readability-specific metrics readability should affect), ③ fine-grained **logs** from dev tools (median review time, shepherding time, time-to-submit).
- **The rule (footnote 9):** **never use productivity metrics to evaluate individuals or identify high/low performers** — once used for performance reviews, engineers game the metrics and they lose all value. "The only way to make these measurements work is to **let go of measuring individuals and embrace measuring the aggregate effect.**"

### Taking Action and Tracking Results
The core claim: after research, always prepare a list of **recommendations** (new tool features, lower latency, better docs, removing obsolete processes, changing incentives). Ideally they're **tool-driven**: telling engineers to change their thinking or process is useless if the tools don't support it — **assume engineers will make the right trade-offs given the right data and suitable tools.**

The readability result: **worth it overall.** Engineers with readability were satisfied and felt they learned; logs showed their code was reviewed and submitted faster (**even after accounting for needing fewer reviewers**). The study also surfaced pain points, and the language teams improved the tooling and process to be faster and more transparent.

Takeaway: this is the **data ending** to [[ch03]]'s readability "trade linear for consistency" — how a mature org uses GSM + QUANTS + quant/qual triangulation to turn a lunch-table argument about "is it worth it" into evidence a decider can actually use.

## Key terms

- **GSM framework (Goals/Signals/Metrics)** — pick metrics top-down: goal (the result you want to understand) → signal (how you'd know) → metric (measurable proxy). Avoids the streetlight effect; preserves traceability.
- **QUANTS (five facets of productivity)** — Quality, Attention, iNtellectual complexity, Tempo/velocity, Satisfaction. In mutual trade-off, so you don't improve one while tanking another.
- **Streetlight effect** — using only easy-to-measure metrics (looking for keys under the streetlight) instead of those that answer the goal. GSM's top-down flow exists to avoid it.
- **Traceability** — every metric traces back to the signal it proxies and the goal it measures, so you know *what* you measure and *why.*
- **Triage (of measurement)** — five questions to decide *whether it's worth measuring* before investing. The crux: would a result (including a negative one) lead to concrete action?
- **Vanity metrics** — metrics used only to support a decision you'd make anyway. Google's most common reason to tell people not to measure.
- **Quantitative vs qualitative** — quant gives scale and confidence but no context; qual gives "why" and narrative. When they disagree, it's usually quant that's wrong. Qualitative metrics are metrics too.
- **Experience sampling** — interrupting engineers *in context* of a task to ask a few questions, capturing the real in-the-moment experience. Used to validate median-build-latency and catch the auto-build miscount.
- **Recall / recency / sampling bias** — three memory/sampling biases driven by when and whom you survey; asking right after avoids recall bias but introduces recency and sampling bias.
- **Tool-driven recommendation** — build improvements into the developer's daily workflow and incentives, rather than just telling people to change their thinking; change is then more likely to actually happen.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Pick a metric you or your boss uses for "productivity/performance" (LOC, commits, velocity, bugs closed, PR count). Is it a Goal, a Signal, or a Metric? Does it **trace back** to a goal you actually care about — or is it just the best-lit thing under the streetlight?
2. Run your latest proposed process/tool change through the five Triage questions. The crux: **if the result came back negative, would you (or the decider) actually change the decision?** If not, is this really a vanity metric?
3. Use QUANTS to audit your team's "efficiency" goals: are you only watching Tempo/velocity while never measuring Quality or Satisfaction? Could you be "removing code reviews"-style sacrificing another facet?
4. Does your org use a productivity/quality metric in **individual performance reviews**? Any signs it's being gamed (filler code, padded commits)? Per this chapter's "measure the aggregate, not individuals."
5. Recall a time you "only trusted hard data, not surveys/interviews." Could you have missed a median-build-latency-style "the quant was wrong" blunder? Could you design a **qualitative sample** to validate the quantitative metric you rely on most? Per [[ch06]]'s Web Search balancing latency with data.

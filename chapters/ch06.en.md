# Chapter 6 — Leading at Scale

> *"The urgent are not important, and the important are never urgent."*
> In plain terms: the things screaming for your attention are rarely the things that actually matter.

## Summary

Chapter 5 was about going from an IC to leading one team; this one is the next step — **going from leading one team to leading a set of related teams.** Author Ben Collins-Sussman notes that all the best practices still apply — you're still a "servant leader," just serving a larger group — but the problems you solve become **bigger and more abstract.** You're forced "higher level," less able to get into technical details, pushed to go *broad* rather than *deep.* It's frustrating at every step (you mourn the lost details), until one day you notice you're having far more impact as a leader than you ever did as an IC — a satisfying but bittersweet realization.

The chapter runs on "**the three Always of leadership**": **Always Be Deciding, Always Be Leaving, Always Be Scaling.**
- **Always Be Deciding:** managing a team of teams means making more decisions at higher levels, almost all of which are about **finding the right trade-offs of the moment.** Three steps: identify the blinders → identify the key trade-offs → decide and iterate. The Web Search latency case study shows drawing "quality / latency / capacity" as a "Good, Fast, Cheap — pick two" triangle of tension (Fig 6-1).
- **Always Be Leaving:** your job isn't to solve the problem yourself, but to **build an organization that solves it without you** — don't make yourself a single point of failure (SPOF / bus factor). Build a "self-driving team" by dividing the problem space, delegating subproblems to leaders, and adjusting iteratively — and by having teams own the *problem*, not a *product.*
- **Always Be Scaling:** the reward for success is **more work and responsibility** (the cycle of success is really a spiral, Fig 6-2 — each loop compresses the problem to half and takes on a parallel one). So scaling must be approached *defensively* — protecting your scarcest resources (**time, attention, energy**) via Eisenhower's "important vs urgent," Marie Kondo's "keep only the critical 20%, deliberately drop the other 80%," and taking real vacations.

## Why read this

- **You just went from "leading a team" to "leading several teams" and feel increasingly useless.** You can't write code or touch the details, and you suspect your engineering expertise is depreciating. This chapter says: your value has *switched tracks* — from "chopping trees" to "seeing the whole forest and pointing at which trees to cut" — and your impact is actually far bigger.
- **You're the team hero and every hard problem lands on your desk.** That's a danger signal: you've made yourself a SPOF. The chapter's litmus test — "on your last week-plus vacation, did you keep checking work email?" — leads into how to build a "self-driving team" so you can safely "Always Be Leaving."
- **You firefight all day and never have time for strategy.** The urgent email is always easier to answer than the important long-term plan. The chapter gives you Eisenhower's "important vs urgent" and the Marie-Kondo "drop balls" mindset — focus only on the critical 20% that *only you* can do.
- **You're burning out and don't know how to stop.** It treats "protecting your energy" as a leadership skill: it takes three days to forget work and a week to truly recharge; disable work apps with one button; take a mental-health day when needed — because a leader's bad mood sets the tone for the whole team.

## Key details

### Always Be Deciding
The core claim: managing a team of teams means making **more decisions at higher levels**; your job shifts from "how to solve an engineering task" to "high-level strategy," and those decisions are **almost all about finding the right trade-offs.**

- **The Parable of the Airplane:** a plane is overfilled by 10,000 gallons of fuel; the captain says either wait an hour for a truck, or 20 people get off to even the weight. An irate first-class passenger offers "$40 to anyone who gets off now"; 20 people happily take $800 and leave. Then the plane's computer fails and it's towed back — and those 20 just grabbed the last seats on an earlier 8 a.m. flight. **It's a story about trade-offs — which apply to human behavior too, sometimes with unforeseeable consequences that bite you.**
- The leader's highest-level job is to guide people through **difficult, ambiguous** problems — ones with no obvious solution, maybe unsolvable. If writing code is chopping trees, leading is "**seeing the forest through the trees**" and finding a workable path. Three steps:
  - **Identify the Blinders:** people steeped in a problem for years wear "blinders" — "this is how we've always done it" — having lost the ability to question the status quo. **Your fresh eyes are the advantage:** you can see the blinders, ask questions, consider new strategies.
  - **Identify the Key Trade-Offs:** important, ambiguous problems have no "silver bullet," only the **best answer for the moment**, which almost certainly involves trade-offs. Your job is to call them out, explain them, and help decide the balance.
  - **Decide, Then Iterate:** make the best decision for *this month*; reevaluate and rebalance next month — it's iterative. **If you don't frame it as "continuous rebalancing of trade-offs," teams fall into searching for the perfect solution — "analysis paralysis."** Lower the stakes: "let's try this and see; next month we can undo it or decide differently."

#### Case Study — Web Search "latency" (the Good/Fast/Cheap triangle)
Thousands of Google engineers spent years improving search-result "quality" (from 10 blue links to images, videos, Wikipedia fact boxes, interactive UI), with a side effect: **the product got slower.** Even as networks and computers got faster over a decade, the search page's **latency increased** — even a 10 ms rendering increase matters to engagement. It's not any one team's fault, but a **long, collective poisoning of the commons.**

Everyone's blinder was that the only fix was a latency "**code yellow**" every two-three years (drop everything to optimize) — but latency crept back within a month or two. The change came from stepping back and reevaluating the trade-offs — finding that pursuing quality has **two costs:** to the user (more data → more latency) and to Google (more compute → more "serving capacity"). Leadership had carefully traded quality vs capacity but **never treated latency as a full citizen** in the calculus. As the joke goes, "**Good, Fast, Cheap — pick two.**" Drawing it as a triangle of tension between Good (quality), Fast (latency), and Cheap (capacity) (**Figure 6-1**) let them experiment with new balances — treating latency as a **first-class goal**, letting data scientists quantify exactly how much latency hurts engagement, and making data-driven trade-off decisions. **We're always deciding whether quality/latency/capacity are in balance, and iterating monthly.**

### Always Be Leaving
The core claim: this famous line from former Google engineering director Bharat Mediratta sounds terrible at face value. Its real meaning: **your job isn't just to solve an ambiguous problem, but to get your organization to solve it by itself, without you present.** Do that, and you're free to move to a new problem, leaving a trail of self-sufficient success.

The antipattern is making yourself a **SPOF (single point of failure)** — Google's **bus factor** ([[ch02]]). Litmus test: imagine you, the leader, disappear from a hard problem your team is progressing on — does the team keep succeeding? Simpler test: **on your last week-plus vacation, did you keep checking work email?** (Most leaders do.) Ask why. If things fall apart without your attention, you've likely made yourself a SPOF — fix it.

The three parts of building a "**self-driving team**":
- **Dividing the Problem Space:** hard problems are made of hard subproblems; the obvious move is a team per subproblem. The risk: subproblems change over time, and rigid team boundaries can't adapt. Prefer a **looser structure** — subteams can resize, people can migrate, assigned problems can morph. Walk the line between "too rigid" and "too vague." (Search latency was split into "treating symptoms" and "treating causes" — and **those teams owned the *problems*, not specific solutions!**)
- **Delegating Subproblems to Leaders:** delegation goes against our instincts for efficiency ("if you want it done right, do it yourself"), but it's **the most effective way to train self-sufficient leaders** — give them a task, let them fail, repeat. Before spending 20 minutes fixing a nagging issue, stop and ask the critical question: "**Am I really the only one who can do this?**" Unless it's truly on fire, hand it off (even if they're slower). Conversely, each day ask: "**What can I do that nobody else on my team can?**" Good answers: shield teams from politics, encourage, foster HRT, "manage up" — but the most common and important is "**I can see the forest through the trees**" — define high-level strategy (technical *and* organizational). You continuously map the forest, then assign the tree-cutting.
- **Adjusting and Iterating:** once the machine self-drives, use a *gentle touch.* The *Debugging Teams* parable: a retired master makes a chalk X on a broken machine; his $10,000 invoice itemizes "$1 for the chalk, **$9,999 for knowing where to put it.**" Picture your team as a blimp drifting in a direction: don't micromanage constant corrections — spend the week watching and listening, then make one small, precise chalk mark and a critical "tap" to adjust course. Good management is **95% observation and listening, 5% critical adjustments in just the right place.** Regress into micromanagement and you're a SPOF again — "**Always Be Leaving** is a call to **macromanagement.**"
- **Take Care in Anchoring a Team's Identity:** a common mistake is putting a team in charge of a specific **product** rather than a general **problem.** A product is a solution; solutions are short-lived and replaceable. A well-chosen **problem can be evergreen.** Anchoring identity to a solution ("we're the team that manages the Git repos") breeds angst when change comes — the team digs in and defends its solution. If it owns the problem ("we provide version control"), it's free to experiment with different solutions over time.

### Always Be Scaling
The core claim: not the offensive "maximize your impact" kind of scaling, but **defensive, personal** scaling — because **your most precious resource is your limited pool of time, attention, and energy.** Aggressively expand responsibilities without protecting your sanity and the scaling is doomed.

- **The Cycle of Success:** a standard pattern — **Analysis** (identify blinders, find trade-offs, build consensus) → **Struggle** (start moving, prepare for failures and iteration; mostly herding cats) → **Traction** (smarter decisions, real progress, morale up, organization self-driving) → **Reward.** But the reward isn't just a pat on the back — it's **a whole new problem!** The reward for success is more work and responsibility, and usually **no more people.** So you enter the "**compression stage**": manage the original problem with half the people in half the time, freeing the other half for the new work. So the cycle is really a **spiral (Figure 6-2)** — Larry Page would call it "**uncomfortably exciting.**" Compression is about scaling not just your team's efficiency but your own time and attention.
- **Important Versus Urgent:** in leadership, work becomes reactive firefighting; the higher you go, the more escalations — you're the "`finally` clause" at the end of a long list of code blocks, and every channel feels like a DoS attack on your attention. Eisenhower's 1954 line: "**I have two kinds of problems, the urgent and the important. The urgent are not important, and the important are never urgent.**" Techniques: **① Delegate** urgent things back to other leaders (good training, even if slower); **② Schedule dedicated time** — block 2+ hours for important-but-not-urgent work (team strategy, leaders' career paths, cross-team collaboration); **③ Find a tracking system** that works (e.g., David Allen's *Getting Things Done* "inbox zero" — try systems until one clicks).
- **Learn to Drop Balls:** counterintuitive for engineers. Since dropping *some* balls is inevitable, **better to drop certain balls deliberately than accidentally.** Via Marie Kondo's *The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up:* most people declutter wrong — they toss the useless bottom 20% but the other 80% still feels cluttered; the real work is **identifying the critical top 20% and tossing the other 80%.** Split your balls into three: the bottom 20% (neither urgent nor important — delete), the middle 60% (a mixed bag), the top 20% (critical, only you can do). **Focus strictly on the top 20% and give yourself explicit permission to drop the other 80%.** Two amazing things happen: ① subleaders often pick up the middle 60% automatically; ② if something there is truly critical, it migrates back up into your top 20% anyway.
- **Protecting Your Energy:** scaling is exhausting. Stamina builds with age (like marathon training), and leaders learn to manage energy deliberately:
  - **Take real vacations:** a weekend isn't a vacation — it takes three days to forget work, a week to truly recharge; but check work email once and the recharge is ruined. **Only possible if you've built a self-driving organization.**
  - **Make it trivial to disconnect:** leave the work laptop at the office; remove work apps, or use a phone "work profile" you can gray out with one button.
  - **Take real weekends, too:** truly sign out Friday night, do what you love, sign in Monday.
  - **Take breaks during the day:** the brain runs in ~90-minute cycles (BRAC); get up and walk — tiny recharges cut stress noticeably.
  - **Give yourself permission to take a mental-health day:** sometimes you're just in a terrible mood for no reason — and as a leader, **your bad mood sets the tone for everyone** and leads to bad decisions. Better to turn around, go home, and do nothing than to do active damage.

Takeaway: managing energy is as important as managing time. The whole chapter redefines "leadership" from "how many problems can I solve" to "**can I build a system that solves problems without me — without burning myself out in the process.**"

## Key terms

- **The Three Always** — Always Be Deciding (find trade-offs, iterate), Always Be Leaving (build a self-driving org), Always Be Scaling (protect time/attention/energy). The chapter's spine.
- **Blinders** — the "this is how we've always done it" assumptions people develop after being steeped in a problem, losing the ability to question the status quo. A newcomer's fresh eyes are an advantage.
- **Good, Fast, Cheap — pick two (quality/latency/capacity)** — improving one costs at least one other. Web Search could only balance it data-drivenly once latency became a first-class goal (Fig 6-1).
- **Analysis paralysis** — getting stuck chasing the "perfect solution." Cured by framing decisions as "continuous rebalancing," lowering stakes, and encouraging iteration.
- **SPOF / bus factor** — making yourself an indispensable bottleneck. "Always Be Leaving" exists to eliminate it (see [[ch02]]).
- **Self-driving team** — an organization that solves a class of ambiguous problems without you present; built via dividing problems, delegating, and gentle iteration.
- **Own the problem, not the solution** — anchor team identity to a *problem* ("provide version control"), not a *product* ("manage the Git repos"), so it can switch solutions over time.
- **Macromanagement** — the opposite of micromanagement; 95% observation/listening + 5% precise critical adjustments (the chalk-mark / blimp metaphor).
- **Important vs urgent** — Eisenhower's framework. To fight reactive firefighting: delegate, schedule dedicated time, use a tracking system; reserve time for important-but-not-urgent.
- **Drop balls deliberately** — Marie-Kondo-style: identify the critical top 20% and let the other 80% go; the middle 60% is often picked up by subleaders, and truly critical items migrate back up.

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Apply the chapter's litmus test: on your last week-plus vacation, **did you keep checking work email**? If you vanished for a month, would your team keep progressing? If not, what's the "only-I-can-do-it" link that makes you a SPOF?
2. Does your team own a **problem** or a **product/solution**? If a better approach appeared, would your team embrace it — or "dig in" and defend the current solution because it's become part of the team's identity?
3. Run your to-do list through Marie Kondo's three piles: which 20% is "critical, only you"? Will you give yourself explicit permission to **deliberately drop** the other 80%? Which of those actually come back on their own?
4. Is there a metric in your team that, like Web Search "latency," is treated as an unavoidable side effect — a **long, collective poisoning of the commons** (build times? tech debt? on-call load?)? Draw it as your own "Good/Fast/Cheap triangle" — which "first-class goal" have you been ignoring?
5. Honestly audit last week: what % went to "urgent" vs "important-but-not-urgent" (strategy, growing leaders)? Could you, as the chapter suggests, **block two dedicated hours** for the latter? Per [[ch05]]'s "delegate but get your hands dirty" and [[ch07]]'s metrics.

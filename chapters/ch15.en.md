# Chapter 15 — Deprecation

> *"Removing things is often more difficult than building them to begin with, because existing users are often using the system beyond its original design."*
> Deleting is harder than building — because people depend on the system in ways it was never designed for.

## Summary

**All systems age.** Even though bits don't rot, new technologies, libraries, languages, and an evolving ecosystem render old systems obsolete — and obsolete systems demand ongoing maintenance, esoteric expertise, and ever more work as they diverge from their surroundings. **Deprecation** is the process of orderly migration away from, and eventual removal of, obsolete systems. It belongs to software engineering (managing a system over time), not mere programming. The chapter's foundational premise echoes [[ch09]]: **code is a liability, not an asset** — value comes from the *functionality* code provides, so what we should maximize isn't lines of code but **functionality delivered per unit of code** — and removing systems no longer needed is one of the easiest ways to do that.

**Why is deprecation so hard?** ① **Hyrum's Law**: more users means more depend on it in unforeseen ways — removal is the *ultimate change*, shaking loose unexpected dependents; ② a new system is necessarily *different* (or there'd be no reason to migrate), so a one-to-one match is rare; ③ **emotional attachment** ("I like this code!"); ④ **politics and funding** (the cost of letting a system limp along is not readily observable, making it hard to convince stakeholders — use [[ch07]] research techniques for evidence). Hence **evolving a system in place (in-place refactoring) is usually cheaper than full replacement** — Google finds wholesale migrations to brand-new systems extremely expensive and routinely underestimated.

Deprecation is best **planned at design time** (like a nuclear plant designed for decommissioning, with funds allocated) — ask: how easily can users migrate to a replacement? Can I replace parts incrementally? Deprecation is a *continuum*, with **advisory** (no deadline, no enforcement — "hope is not a strategy") at one end and **compulsory** (a deadline with an enforcement mechanism, staffed by a dedicated team through completion) at the other. On management: have an **explicit process owner** (without one, nothing progresses), set **incremental milestones** (don't make "complete removal" the only one), and use three tool categories (**discovery / migration / backsliding prevention**). Deprecation warnings must be both **actionable and relevant** (surfacing at the right moment), or they cause **alert fatigue**.

## Why read this

- **Your codebase is full of old systems nobody dares turn off and nobody maintains.** This explains why you should turn them off (**code is a liability**; multiple coexisting systems slow the new one's evolution) and why it's so hard (Hyrum's Law, emotional attachment, politics and funding). Then "removal" becomes a legitimate move in your toolbox.
- **You nudge colleagues to a new system by slapping a deprecation warning on the old one and walking away.** The chapter is blunt: "**hope is not a strategy**" — advisory deprecation rarely makes people migrate; the old system exerts a "conceptual/technical pull." To actually finish, you need **compulsory deprecation + a staffed team.**
- **You're assigned a thankless "cleanup" project with no visible progress.** Here's how to manage it: **set incremental milestones** (celebrate deleting a key subcomponent — don't wait until the lights go off), **centralize expertise in one team**, and treat it as great "important-not-urgent" 20%-time work.
- **You're designing a new system.** A rare but crucial lens: **design for eventual decommissioning** (like a power plant) — ask now "how easily can users migrate away? can I replace parts incrementally?" — and remember "**don't start projects your organization isn't committed to support for its expected lifespan.**"

## Key details

### Why Deprecate?
The core claim: the starting point is **"code is a liability, not an asset."** Code has costs — at creation, but many more across **maintenance** (operational resources to keep it running, effort to update it as ecosystems evolve). **Old doesn't mean obsolete** (LaTeX has improved over decades and isn't obsolete); deprecation best suits systems that are **demonstrably obsolete with a comparable replacement** (more efficient, more secure, more maintainable, or just bug-fixed). Two systems doing the same thing seem harmless, but **maintenance costs grow substantially over time**: they may need to interface (complex transformation code), grow to depend on each other, and **coexistence slows the new system's evolution** (it must stay compatible with the old).

Takeaway: don't fixate on "how much code" or "how big the codebase," but maximize **functionality delivered per unit of code** — and one of the easiest ways isn't writing more code but **removing code and systems no longer needed.** But note: an organization has a **limit on simultaneous deprecation work** (the road-paving analogy — close every road for paving at once and nobody moves; focused crews finish faster). Choose deprecation projects carefully and commit to finishing them.

### Why Is Deprecation So Hard?
The core claim:
- **Hyrum's Law (see [[ch01]]):** more users, more who depend on it in unforeseen ways — their usage "happens to work" rather than being "guaranteed to work." Removal is the **ultimate change** (not changing behavior but removing it entirely), shaking loose unexpected dependents.
- **A new system is necessarily different:** deprecation isn't usually an option until a comparable (or better) replacement exists; but "better" means "different" (or there'd be no migration incentive) — so one-to-one matches are rare and every old use must be reevaluated in the new context.
- **Emotional attachment:** a change aversion — "I like this code!" is hard to overcome when someone built it over years. Google's mitigation: make the source repo **searchable historically, not just at trunk** (removed code can be found again, see [[ch17]]), easing the "gone forever" anxiety.
- **Politics and funding:** staffing and time to remove obsolete systems cost real money, while the cost of "doing nothing" isn't readily observable — hard to sell to stakeholders, especially when it dents new-feature work. Use [[ch07]] research techniques for concrete evidence.

Takeaway: given the difficulty, **evolving in place (in-place refactoring) is usually cheaper than full replacement** — incrementality doesn't avoid deprecation but breaks it into smaller, manageable chunks that yield incremental value. Google finds wholesale migrations to new systems **extremely expensive and routinely underestimated.** (An old Google joke: there are two ways to do things — the deprecated one and the not-yet-ready one.)

### Deprecation During Design
The core claim: deprecation can be planned when a system is first built — language, architecture, team composition, even company policy and culture all affect how easily a system can later be removed. **"Designing for eventual deprecation" may be radical in software but is common elsewhere**: a nuclear plant's design accounts for decommissioning after its service life, even allocating funds. Software is rarely so thoughtful (engineers prefer building to maintaining; culture rewards fast shipping; and planning a creation's demise is psychologically hard). Encourage teams to ask: **① how easily can my consumers migrate to a potential replacement? ② how can I replace parts of my system incrementally?** (Much relates to how a system provides and consumes dependencies — see [[ch16]].)

Takeaway: **whether to support a project long-term is decided the moment the organization decides to build it.** Once a system exists, the only options are to support it, carefully deprecate it, or let it break when some external event hits it. In short: **don't start projects your organization isn't committed to support for its expected lifespan** — even choosing to deprecate has costs, but they can be mitigated by planning and investment in tools and policy.

### Types of Deprecation
The core claim: deprecation isn't a single process but a **continuum** — from "we hope to turn this off someday" to "this goes away tomorrow, be ready." Broadly, two regions:
- **Advisory:** no deadline, not high priority (no resources committed), a.k.a. aspirational. Usually **lacks enforcement** — "we hope clients migrate but can't force them" (as SRE says: "**Hope is not a strategy.**"). It's good for **advertising a new system and encouraging early adopters** (but the new system isn't a beta — it must handle production loads and support new users indefinitely). The benefits must be **transformative, not incremental**: users won't migrate on their own for marginal gains. Advisory **nudges** users in a direction but **can't be counted on to do the bulk of migration** — just posting a warning and walking away rarely causes active migration; the old system exerts a "conceptual/technical pull" on new uses.
- **Compulsory:** the active encouragement comes with a **removal deadline** (depend on it past that date and your system breaks). Counterintuitively, compulsory deprecation scales best by **localizing migration expertise in a single team of experts** (usually the team removing the old system) — they have incentives to help and build org-wide tools (often the [[ch22]] LSC tooling). To work, the **schedule needs an enforcement mechanism**: empower the team to break noncompliant users after ample warning, or customer teams will deprioritize it. But **compulsory deprecation without staffing** reads as an "unfunded mandate," feels mean-spirited, and like "running to stay in place" — so **strongly staff compulsory deprecations through completion.** Even with policy behind it, political hurdles remain (if the last user is critical infrastructure the whole org depends on, would you break it for an arbitrary deadline?). Discover unknown dependencies via **planned outages of increasing duration** (like DiRT) or by renaming implementation-only symbols to see who breaks.

#### Deprecation Warnings
The core claim: marking systems deprecated programmatically helps, but "mark it and hope" doesn't work. Warnings accumulate, especially transitively (A→B→C, where C's warning surfaces when building A), until users ignore them all — health care calls this **alert fatigue**. Any warning needs two properties: **① actionability** — the user can take a relevant action (replace a call with its updated counterpart; an email listing data-migration steps); **② relevance** — it surfaces *when the user actually does the action* (warn while the engineer writes code using the function, not weeks after check-in; send a data-migration email months before removal, not the weekend before). Resist warning on everything — Google marks things deprecated liberally but uses ErrorProne/clang-tidy to **limit warnings to newly changed lines** (see [[ch20]]); more intrusive warnings (deprecated targets in the dependency graph) are added only for compulsory deprecations with an active migration team.

### Managing the Deprecation Process
The core claim: managing a deprecation resembles other software projects, but differs in a few ways:
- **Process owners:** without an explicit owner, deprecation rarely makes meaningful progress (no matter how many warnings). The alternatives are worse: never deprecate (commit to maintaining every old system forever) or delegate to users (degrades to advisory, never organically finishing). **Centralizing** lets expertise actually reduce costs. **Abandoned projects** (original owners moved on, the obsolete one chugging in the basement as a critical dependency) won't fade on their own — they need deprecation experts whose **primary goal is removal** (not a side project, which always loses on priority). Such "important-not-urgent" cleanup is great **20%-time** work.
- **Milestones:** new systems have clear milestones; deprecation can feel like its only milestone is complete removal — the team feels no progress until the lights go off, and that moment is least noticed externally (no users remain). **Don't make complete removal the only measurable milestone** (it may not even happen); set **concrete, measurable, value-delivering incremental milestones** and celebrate them (deleting a key subcomponent) as you would product wins.
- **Deprecation tooling** — three categories: **Discovery:** Code Search ([[ch17]]) and **Kythe** statically find who uses a library and sample what behaviors customers unexpectedly depend on; logging/runtime sampling find dynamic dependencies; the **global test suite serves as an oracle** for whether all references to an old symbol are gone ([[ch11]]). **Migration:** the LSC process and tooling ([[ch22]]) update the codebase en masse to new libraries/services. **Backsliding prevention:** an often-overlooked but critical piece — without it, deprecation becomes whack-a-mole (users keep adding new uses, the team keeps migrating), unproductive and demoralizing. Micro-level: **Tricorder** notifies at review time that you're adding a call into a deprecated system, with a suggested replacement (owners add `@deprecated`-style annotations to control messaging; sometimes a push-button fix). Macro-level: build-system **visibility whitelists** prevent new dependencies (auto-pruned as systems migrate away).

## Key terms

- **Deprecation** — the orderly migration away from, and eventual removal of, obsolete systems; software engineering (managing over time), not programming.
- **Code is a liability** — value comes from functionality, not code itself; maximize functionality per unit of code, with removal one of the easiest ways (echoes [[ch09]]).
- **Obsolete ≠ old** — deprecate the demonstrably obsolete with a comparable replacement; an enduring system like LaTeX is old but not obsolete.
- **Advisory deprecation** — no deadline, no enforcement, not high priority; good for advertising and nudging, but can't be counted on for migration (benefits must be transformative).
- **Compulsory deprecation** — a removal deadline with an enforcement mechanism; must be staffed by a dedicated team through completion, or it's an "unfunded mandate."
- **Hope is not a strategy** — an SRE maxim; merely posting a deprecation warning rarely causes migration.
- **In-place refactoring vs replacement** — including turndown costs, evolving in place is usually cheaper than a new system; wholesale migrations are extremely expensive and underestimated.
- **Actionability & relevance** — a good warning's two properties: it enables an action and surfaces at the right moment; otherwise alert fatigue.
- **Alert fatigue** — warnings (especially transitive) accumulate until users ignore them all; hence limit warnings to newly changed lines (ErrorProne/clang-tidy).
- **Backsliding prevention** — stopping new uses of what's being removed; micro via Tricorder + `@deprecated`, macro via build-system visibility whitelists.
- **Process owner** — deprecation needs an explicit owner with removal as the primary goal; otherwise it degrades to advisory or perpetual maintenance.
- **Discovery / migration / backsliding (three tool categories)** — Code Search/Kythe + global tests (discovery), LSC (migration), Tricorder/whitelists (backsliding prevention).

## Questions to push your thinking

1. Inventory the old systems "nobody dares turn off and nobody clearly owns": are they truly *obsolete* (with a comparable replacement) or merely *old*? By "functionality per unit of code," which has the best ROI to remove? Per [[ch09]]'s "code is a liability."
2. Was your last migration push just a deprecation warning posted and abandoned (advisory), or a **deadline + a staffed team** (compulsory)? Recall the outcome — did "hope is not a strategy" prove true? How strong was the old system's "conceptual pull"?
3. Are your deprecation warnings **actionable and relevant**? Do they tell the user the next step and surface *while they write code using it*? Or are they ignored **alert fatigue**? Can you **limit them to newly changed lines** like Google?
4. Is the new system you're designing **designed for eventual decommissioning**? Ask the power-plant questions: how easily can users migrate away? can parts be replaced incrementally? Is your org truly committed to **support its expected lifespan**?
5. Assigned a deprecation project: what **incremental milestones** would you set (rather than waiting for the final lights-off)? Which three tool categories (discovery/migration/backsliding) would you use? Can you **centralize expertise in one team** and win the authority and staffing to break users? Per [[ch22]] LSCs.

CHAPTER 08

Style Guides and Rules

風格規範與規則
作者 Shaindel Schwartz PDF p.169–192 Scale 規模 Time 時間 Trade-offs 取捨
"It's the consistency of having one answer rather than the answer itself that is the valuable part." 真正有價值的，是「只有一個答案」這件事本身，而不是那個答案是什麼。

摘要

這是「流程」篇的開場。Google 用各語言獨立的風格指南治理超過 20 億行、每天約 6 萬次提交、3 萬多名工程師、存活數十年的程式碼。先釐清關鍵區分：規則（rules）是法律——強制、可普遍執行、除非核准否則不得違反；指引（guidance）是建議。一句話：規則是「musts」，指引是「shoulds」。

定規則時要問的不是「該有哪些規則」，而是「要推進什麼目標」——Google 的目標是在規模與時間壓力下管理複雜度。由此導出五原則：規則必須①值回票價、②為讀者最佳化（簡單讀勝過簡單寫）、③保持一致、④避開易錯/意外的構造、⑤必要時向現實妥協。規則分三類（避開危險、強制最佳實務、確保一致），由 style arbiters 以共識拍板、附理由。最後談執行：能技術性自動化就盡量自動化（error checker、formatter＋presubmit），因為「機器平均明顯比人類好」、消除偏誤、可擴展（C++ 約 90% 規則可自動驗證）。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你的團隊還在 review 裡為「兩空格還是四空格」吵。 終結這類爭論的根本論點：重點不是選哪個答案，而是「只選一個」；再用 formatter＋presubmit 自動化，讓它「不再有趣」。
你想引進一條規範卻不確定該不該存在。 「規則必須值回票價」給你判準：若只有一兩人會弄錯，加一條全員都要記的規則並不划算——別把所有最佳實務都升格成強制規則。
你在寫給「未來的讀者」還是「現在的自己」？ 「為讀者最佳化、簡單讀勝過簡單寫」最可立即套用——unique_ptr 所有權、override 標註、local reasoning 都是把意圖留在現場。
你想把「靠人盯」升級成「靠工具擋」。 本章說清為何技術執行優於社會執行，以及哪些規則該留給人判斷（社會性的，如 CL 大小）——別用技術方案解社會問題。

細節重點

為何 Why Have Rules? 為什麼要有規則

規則的目的是鼓勵「好」、勸阻「壞」——而「好/壞」因組織而異、是主觀的。先認清組織重視什麼，再用規則去鼓勵/勸阻。
工程意涵：規則塑造共同的程式詞彙，讓工程師專注「程式要說什麼」而非「怎麼說」，並下意識地做對的事。

原則 五條指導原則 Guiding Principles

關鍵問題不是「該有哪些規則」，而是「要推進什麼目標」。脈絡：3 萬+ 工程師、6 萬次/日提交、20 億行、數十年——目標是讓程式碼庫對規模與時間有韌性。這是取捨：限制選擇換取一致性與減少衝突。
  1. 值回票價：不收不證自明的規則（C++ 沒有禁 goto）；只有一兩人會錯就別加。
  2. 為讀者最佳化：程式被讀多於被寫，「簡單讀」勝過「簡單寫」；用 override、unique_ptr 把意圖留在現場，追求 local reasoning。
  3. 保持一致：像辦公室——識別證到處能刷、視訊介面到處一樣；讓專家 chunking、讓工具化擴展、對時間有韌性。
  4. 避開易錯/意外的構造：限制 Python 反射（hasattr/getattr）；要全部工程師都能工作，不只專家。
  5. 向現實妥協：Emerson「愚蠢的一致性是小心靈的妖怪」——效能（noexcept）、互通性（snake_case、Windows 多重繼承、產生碼豁免）可破例。
規模下：C++ 指南已刪去「永不改會讓舊碼不一致的規則」的承諾——要求完美一致已到「成本超過價值」（LSC 工具能更新大部分舊碼但無法保證每處，見 ch22）。

內容 The Style Guide：三類規則 放什麼進指南

  • 避開危險：基於技術理由「必須/不得」的特性規則，每條附 pros/cons 與理由。
  • 強制最佳實務：註解、檔案結構、命名；對「新且未充分理解的特性」先架安全圍籬（std::unique_ptr 案例：C++11 初期禁用 → 工程師適應後因清楚表達所有權而放行）。
  • 確保一致：命名、縮排、import 排序等「小事」——技術上無優劣（bikeshedding）；選一個就跳出爭論，重要的是「我們選了」。
  • 其餘的：很多最佳實務刻意不寫（別耍小聰明、別 fork、別重造輪子）——有些刻意假設讀者已具備。

演進 Changing the Rules 修改規則

指南不是靜態的。每條規則都附權衡理由，這樣條件改變時才知道何時該重新評估（如 Python CamelCase→snake_case）。
  • 流程是解決方案導向：提案要用現有程式中的真實模式證明問題（非假設），始於社群討論。
  • Style Arbiters：各語言資深專家，以工程取捨的共識（非投票、非個人偏好）拍板；C++ 為 4 人組。
  • Exceptions/Waiver：不輕易給——macro 前綴若只為嫌長而請求豁免會被駁回（程式碼庫完整性勝過專案一致性）。

指引 Guidance：musts 之外的 shoulds primer / TotW / 101

Primers（描述性，解釋認可特性怎麼用）、C++ Tips of the Week（TotW）（從實際問題長出、是「canon of the common」、常在 review 被引用）、<Language>@Google 101 課程（把「好工程師」補成「好的 Google 程式碼庫工程師」）。

執行 Applying the Rules 社會性 vs 技術性執行

規則的價值在於可被執行。檢查合規時強烈偏好技術自動化：不會隨時間/規模流失、消除人為偏誤、可擴展（規模翻倍成本幾乎不變）。
  • Error Checkers：C++ 約 90% 規則可自動驗證；就地給警告＋建議修正，新的 deprecated 用法「一夜消失」。用 clang-tidy／Error Prone（見 ch20）。
  • Code Formatters：「機器平均明顯比人類好」；用 presubmit 強制（clang-format、yapf、gofmt、dartfmt、buildifier）。
  • gofmt 案例：Go 2009 開源第一天就附 gofmt、無設定旋鈕；機器改寫與人手寫無從分辨（gofix）。2012 buildifier 用 6 週讓 20 萬個 BUILD 檔被接受。

關鍵字補充

Rules vs Guidance（規則 vs 指引）
規則是強制可執行的「musts」（法律），指引是建議性的「shoulds」（最佳實務）。本章根本二分。
Style Guide（風格指南）
治理程式碼的完整慣例集合（不只格式），各語言獨立；工程師被問責的權威來源。
Pull Their Weight（值回票價）
規則效益須大於「全員學習與維護」成本；不證自明、或只有一兩人會錯的事不該成為規則。
Optimize for the Reader（為讀者最佳化）
程式被讀多於被寫，「簡單讀」勝過「簡單寫」；把意圖留在現場（override、unique_ptr），追求 local reasoning。
Hierarchy of Consistency（一致性的階層）
從局部開始：檔案 > 團隊 > 專案 > 整體；時間與規模成為因素時再考慮與外部一致。
Expert Chunking（專家組塊）
一致性讓專家把程式看成有意義的「塊」（如棋型）而非個別片段，快速抓重點。
Bikeshedding（瑣事爭論）
Parkinson 瑣事定律：對沒有技術優劣的小事無止盡爭論。規則的價值是「選一個」跳出循環。
Style Arbiters（風格仲裁者）
各語言指南 owner，以工程取捨的共識（非投票、非個人偏好）拍板；C++ 為 4 人組。
Concede to Practicalities（向現實妥協）
一致性不盲目；效能（noexcept）、互通性（snake_case、產生碼豁免）值得破例。
Presubmit Check / Automated Enforcement（提交前檢查／自動執行）
提交前自動驗證合規否則拒絕。技術執行不會流失、消除偏誤、可擴展（C++ 約 90% 規則可自動驗證）。

延伸提問

  1. 翻出你團隊最近一次關於「格式/命名/風格」的爭論。它是否屬於「沒有技術優劣、只是 bikeshedding」？能不能用 formatter＋presubmit 把它自動化，從此讓它「不再有趣」？
  2. 用「規則必須值回票價」檢查你的規範清單：有沒有哪幾條其實只有一兩人會弄錯、卻加重了所有人的記憶負擔？哪些「規則」其實該降級成「指引」？
  3. 你的程式有沒有「為讀者最佳化」？挑一段你覺得「聰明/簡潔」的程式——半年後的同事（或 SRE 半夜除錯）能在呼叫點就讀懂意圖嗎？還是得去翻實作（缺 local reasoning）？
  4. 套用「一致性的階層」：你的團隊有沒有「局部慣例」與「公司/外部標準」衝突處？什麼情況你會選擇與外部一致（像 Python 改 snake_case），什麼情況守內部一致？
  5. 盤點你靠人盯的規則：哪些其實可以也應該自動化（error checker/formatter/presubmit）？哪些該留給人判斷（社會性的，如 CL 大小）？你有沒有不小心「用技術方案解社會問題」？對照 ch20。

Part III — Processes (opening)
CHAPTER 08

Style Guides and Rules

The win is having one answer — not which answer it is
by Shaindel Schwartz PDF p.169–192 Scale Time Trade-offs
"It's the consistency of having one answer rather than the answer itself that is the valuable part." In plain terms: the win is that there's one answer — not which answer it is.

Summary

This opens the "Processes" part. Google governs 2 billion+ lines, ~60,000 submissions/day, 30,000+ engineers, alive for decades with per-language style guides. The key distinction: rules are laws — strict, mandatory, universally enforceable, no waiver-free exceptions; guidance is recommendations. In a phrase: rules are the "musts," guidance the "shoulds."

When defining rules, ask not "what rules" but "what goal" — Google's is to manage complexity under scale and time. Five principles: rules must ① pull their weight, ② optimize for the reader (read beats write), ③ be consistent, ④ avoid error-prone/surprising constructs, ⑤ concede to practicalities. Rules fall into three buckets (avoid danger, enforce best practices, ensure consistency), decided by style arbiters via consensus with recorded reasoning. Enforcement: automate technically whenever feasible (error checkers, formatters + presubmit) — "robots beat humans on average," remove bias, and scale (≈ 90% of C++ rules auto-verifiable).

Why read this

Your team still argues "two spaces or four" in review. The argument that ends it: the point isn't which answer, it's having one; then automate with a formatter + presubmit so it "stops being interesting."
You want a team rule but aren't sure it should exist. "Rules must pull their weight" is the test: if only one or two get it wrong, a rule everyone must remember isn't worth it — don't promote every best practice to a rule.
Writing for the future reader or for present-you? "Optimize for the reader; read beats write" is the most applicable mindset — unique_ptr ownership, the override annotation, and local reasoning put intent at the call site.
You want to upgrade "watched by humans" to "blocked by tools." The chapter spells out why technical enforcement beats social, and which rules should stay human-judged (social ones, like CL size) — don't solve social problems technically.

Key details

WHY Why Have Rules?

Rules encourage "good" and discourage "bad" — and "good/bad" vary by org and are subjective. Recognize what your org values, then use rules accordingly.
Takeaway: rules shape a common vocabulary, letting engineers focus on *what* code says rather than *how*, doing the good thing by default.

PRINCIPLES The Five Guiding Principles

The key question isn't "what rules" but "what goal." Context: 30k+ engineers, 60k submissions/day, 2B lines, decades — the goal is resilience to scale and time. A trade-off: restrict choice for consistency and less conflict.
  1. Pull their weight: omit self-evident rules (no rule against goto); if one or two get it wrong, don't add it.
  2. Optimize for the reader: read beats write; use override, unique_ptr to put intent in place, aiming for local reasoning.
  3. Be consistent: like offices — your badge works everywhere; lets experts chunk, lets tooling scale, gives resilience to time.
  4. Avoid error-prone/surprising constructs: restrict Python reflection (hasattr/getattr); *all* engineers must work in the code, not just experts.
  5. Concede to practicalities: Emerson's "foolish consistency" — performance (noexcept), interoperability (snake_case, Windows multiple inheritance, generated-code exemptions) warrant exceptions.
At scale: the C++ guide struck its "never change rules that make old code inconsistent" promise — demanding perfect consistency reached "too much cost for the value" (LSC updates most old code but can't guarantee every corner; see ch22).

CONTENT The Style Guide: three buckets

  • Avoid danger: technical "must/must not" feature rules, each with weighed pros/cons.
  • Enforce best practices: comments, file structure, naming; safety fences for new, not-yet-understood features (std::unique_ptr case: disallowed in C++11 → allowed once engineers adjusted and it clearly expressed ownership).
  • Ensure consistency: naming, indentation, import order — no technical winner (bikeshedding); pick one and move on, the value is *that* you chose.
  • And everything else: much is deliberately left out (don't be clever, don't fork, don't reinvent) — some things are intentionally assumed.

EVOLUTION Changing the Rules

Guides aren't static. Every rule records its pros/cons so you can tell when to reconsider (e.g., Python CamelCase→snake_case).
  • Solution-based process: a proposal must prove a real problem with patterns in existing code (not hypotheticals), starting with community discussion.
  • Style arbiters: senior per-language experts decide by trade-off consensus (not voting, not preference); the C++ group is four.
  • Exceptions/waivers: not granted lightly — a macro-prefix waiver just for length is rejected (codebase integrity > project consistency).

GUIDANCE Guidance: the "shoulds" beyond the "musts"

Primers (descriptive — how to use endorsed features), C++ Tips of the Week (TotW) (grown from real problems, a "canon of the common," cited in reviews), <Language>@Google 101 courses (turning a good engineer into a good Google-codebase engineer).

ENFORCE Applying the Rules

Rules are valuable only if enforceable. For compliance, strongly prefer technical automation: it doesn't decay with time/scale, removes human bias, and scales (doubling the company barely changes cost).
  • Error checkers: ≈90% of C++ rules auto-verifiable; in-place warnings + suggested fixes made new deprecated usages "disappear overnight." clang-tidy / Error Prone (see ch20).
  • Code formatters: "robots beat humans on average"; enforced via presubmit (clang-format, yapf, gofmt, dartfmt, buildifier).
  • gofmt case: Go shipped gofmt on day one in 2009, no config knobs; machine-edited code is indistinguishable from human (gofix). In 2012 buildifier got 200,000 BUILD files accepted in 6 weeks.

Key terms

Rules vs Guidance
Rules are enforceable "musts" (laws); guidance is advisory "shoulds" (best practices). The chapter's foundational split.
Style guide
The full set of conventions governing code (not just formatting), per language; the authoritative source engineers are accountable to.
Pull their weight
A rule's benefit must exceed the cost of everyone learning and maintaining it; self-evident things shouldn't be rules.
Optimize for the reader
Read beats write; "simple to read" over "simple to write"; leave intent in place (override, unique_ptr), aiming for local reasoning.
Hierarchy of consistency
Starts locally: file > team > project > overall codebase; once time and scale matter, consider matching the external community.
Expert chunking
Consistency lets experts see code as meaningful chunks (like chess configurations) and zero in fast.
Bikeshedding
Parkinson's law of triviality: endless debate over things with no technical winner. A rule's value is "pick one" to escape the cycle.
Style arbiters
Per-language guide owners who decide by trade-off consensus (not voting, not preference); the C++ group is four.
Concede to practicalities
Consistency isn't blind; performance (noexcept) and interoperability (snake_case, generated-code exemptions) warrant exceptions.
Presubmit check / automated enforcement
Auto-validate compliance before submit, rejecting otherwise. Doesn't decay, removes bias, scales (≈90% of C++ rules auto-verifiable).

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Dig up your team's last argument about "format/naming/style." Was it pure bikeshedding (no technical winner)? Could you automate it with a formatter + presubmit so it "stops being interesting"?
  2. Apply "rules must pull their weight" to your rule list: which rules only one or two get wrong, yet burden everyone's memory? Which "rules" should be demoted to "guidance"?
  3. Does your code "optimize for the reader"? Take a piece that felt "clever/concise" — can a colleague six months later (or an SRE at 3am) read the intent at the call site, or must they dig into the implementation?
  4. Apply the hierarchy of consistency: where do "local conventions" clash with company/external standards? When match the external community (like Python's → snake_case), when hold internal consistency?
  5. Inventory your human-watched rules: which can and should be automated? Which should stay human-judged (social, like CL size)? Are you "solving a social problem with a technical solution"? Per ch20.

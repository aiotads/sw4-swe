CHAPTER 02

How to Work Well on Teams

如何在團隊裡好好合作
作者 Brian Fitzpatrick PDF p.55–70 Culture 文化 · HRT Scale 規模 Trade-offs 取捨
"Software engineering is a team endeavor." 軟體工程，是一項團隊事業。

摘要

這是「文化」篇的開場，主題是你唯一能完全掌控的變數——你自己。工程師常想「把寫到一半的程式碼藏起來」，根源是不安全感：害怕別人看到未完成的作品而評判自己。這份不安全感餵養了天才迷思（The Genius Myth）——把團隊成就歸功於單一英雄（Linus、Guido、賈伯斯、Michael Jordan），但真相是「一個火花 + 一支英雄團隊」的集體勞動。

核心主張：軟體開發是團隊事業，健康協作的地基是三支柱——謙遜（Humility）、尊重（Respect）、信任（Trust），簡稱 HRT。幾乎任何社交衝突的根因都能追溯到 HRT 之一的缺席。作者把 HRT 落地為可操作行為：放下自我、學會給與受批評（你不是你的程式碼）、快速失敗並迭代、無究責事後檢討、學會耐心、對影響保持開放。最後 Google 把模糊的「Googley」明確定義成行為量表，避免它退化成「跟我一樣」的無意識偏見。

為什麼要讀這一篇

技術很強卻在團隊卡關。 殘酷事實：多數工作不需要天才級智力，但 100% 的工作都需要最低限度的社交能力。決定職涯成敗的，是你跟人協作得多好。
想把「孤狼工作法」的風險講清楚。 「躲起來做到完美再公開」感覺安全，實則把時間豪賭在「可能做錯方向」上。bus factor、早期回饋、快速迭代給你說服自己也說服別人的論證。
帶人或做 code review 常踩人的地雷。 Joe 的故事示範「技術正確、社交災難」的審查；本章教你把「你錯了」改成「我有點困惑」——用謙遜把問題指向自己。
想建立能容錯、能學習的文化。 無究責事後檢討、「Failure is an option」、示弱即力量，是讓團隊敢冒險、敢認錯的制度基礎。

細節重點

文化 Help Me Hide My Code 幫我把程式碼藏起來

Google Code 收到大量「隱藏分支／先隱藏專案／清掉歷史」請求，共同主題是不安全感——害怕別人評判進行中的作品。
工程意涵：別把「想藏」當個人怪癖，它是團隊文化健康與否的訊號燈；不安全感是更大問題的「症狀」。

文化 The Genius Myth 天才迷思

Linus 不是獨自寫出 Linux——他寫的是核心雛形，真正成就是領導並協調數千人。天才迷思 = 把團隊成功歸因於單一個人的傾向。

現實檢查：你大概不是天才；即使是，天才也會犯錯，且只解決分析問題、不解決「人」的問題，照樣是糟糕的隊友。

工程意涵：天才迷思是不安全感的變體——怕被看見草稿就露餡。但炫技解決不了協作問題。

取捨 Hiding Considered Harmful 藏起來是有害的

自行車變速器寓言：你閉門造車數月，鄰居找朋友一起做，更快做出更好成品、還一眼看出你早該修的缺陷。獨自工作本質上更冒險。
  • Early Detection 早期偵測：愈早求回饋愈能降低風險。奉行「Fail early, fail fast, fail often」。
  • Bus Factor 公車係數：「需要多少人被公車撞到，專案才會徹底完蛋」。只有你懂 = 高風險；靠文件 + 主/副負責人提高它。
  • Pace of Progress 前進步調：工程師在緊密回饋迴路裡最好（像逐步編譯）；團隊是專案層級的回饋迴路——「眾目睽睽，讓專案保持切題不偏航」。
  • 辦公室案例：獨立辦公室與開放大平面都走極端；最佳解是 4–8 人小房間，再用 token/耳機/「Breakpoint Mary」協定保留專注時間。
工程意涵：在「專注獨處」與「高頻寬團隊連線」間找平衡是藝術，但「藏」永遠是錯的一端。

文化 It's All About the Team 一切都是團隊的事

程式界的「獨行工匠」極罕見；改變世界的成就幾乎都是「靈感火花 + 英雄式團隊努力」。偉大團隊善用超級明星，但整體永遠大於部分之和。

文化 The Three Pillars · HRT 社交三支柱

對幾乎任何社交衝突做根因分析，最後都能追溯到 謙遜 / 尊重 / 信任 之一的缺席。

文化 Why Do These Pillars Matter? 為何這些支柱重要

和可預測的編譯器相處的確比和人相處容易，但別低估「玩社交遊戲」的力量——這不是操弄，而是建立關係把事情做成。
工程意涵：關係的壽命永遠長過專案；Hamming 的故事——對人友善換來關鍵時刻的全力相助。

文化 HRT in Practice HRT 的實踐

  • 放下自我：追求集體的自我（collective ego）而非個人榮耀；堅持「照我的方式」會在整個職涯付出持續的小代價。
  • 給與受批評：區分「對作品的建設性批評」與「對人格的攻擊」。鐵則「你不是你的程式碼」。把「你寫錯了」改成「我被這段搞糊塗了，換 xyzzy 模式會不會更清楚？」
  • 快速失敗並迭代：CEO「我才剛花一千萬訓練你」的故事；Google「Failure is an option」，Google X 把失敗寫進激勵——比賽誰能推翻最多點子。
  • 無究責事後檢討：不是道歉/藉口/指責清單，而要寫「學到什麼、將改變什麼」。包含摘要、時間線、主因、影響、修復與預防行動項（含負責人）、教訓。
  • 學會耐心：作者與 Karl（由上而下 vs 由下而上）靠信任、尊重、耐心發明新協作法，救了專案也救了友誼。
  • 對影響保持開放：愈開放被影響愈能影響人；示弱（承認「我不知道」）是謙遜與當責的外顯，長期反而提升地位。隊友是協作者不是對手。

文化 Being Googley 所謂的「Googley」

「Googley」從未明確定義，導致它在招聘/考核中變成無意識偏見來源——可能悄悄等於「跟我一樣」。

Google 把它明確化成量表：在模糊中茁壯、重視回饋、挑戰現狀、以使用者為先、在乎團隊、做對的事，並逐漸不再用模糊的「Googley」。

工程意涵：對期待要具體——模糊的文化詞彙是偏見的溫床。

原文引用

"Software engineering is a team endeavor."軟體工程，是一項團隊事業。
全章論點的一句話總結，直接反駁「天才程式設計師」的幻想。
"Bus factor (noun): the number of people that need to get hit by a bus before your project is completely doomed."公車係數（名詞）：需要有多少人被公車撞到，你的專案才會徹底完蛋的人數。

給與受批評的核心心法——把對作品的批評和對人格的攻擊切開。

關鍵字補充

HRT（謙遜、尊重、信任）
健康協作的三支柱。幾乎所有社交衝突的根因都是其中一項缺席；文化篇反覆援引的基礎模型。
The Genius Myth（天才迷思）
把團隊集體成就歸因於單一英雄的傾向。它是不安全感的變體，會讓人不敢分享未完成的作品。
Bus Factor（公車係數）
專案徹底失敗前需要「失去」的關鍵人數。靠文件、主/副負責人、知識分散提高，降低單點知識風險。
Fail early, fail fast, fail often
透過緊密回饋迴路盡早暴露錯誤，把修正成本壓到最低；對應第 1 章的 shifting left。
You are not your code（你不是你的程式碼）
把自我價值與創作產出脫鉤，才能健康地給與接受批評。
Blameless Post-Mortem（無究責事後檢討）
對事不對人的根因分析文件，重點是「學到什麼、將改變什麼」，而非道歉與指責。
Collective Ego（集體的自我）
把個人榮耀感轉化為團隊成就感與群體榮耀，避免破壞性的個人自我。
Constructive Criticism（建設性批評）
針對「作品如何改進」而非「人格如何不堪」的回饋；以尊重為底色，用謙遜措辭表達。
Googleyness（Google 特質）
明確化後的行為量表：在模糊中茁壯、重視回饋、挑戰現狀、使用者為先、在乎團隊、做對的事。
Pair Programming（結對程式設計）
兩人共用一台機器協作；風格衝突需靠 HRT 與耐心化解，必要時改良協作方式而非硬配。

延伸提問

  1. 誠實面對：你最近一次「想做完美再給人看」是什麼時候？背後的不安全感是什麼？早一週給同事看，最壞與最好分別會怎樣？
  2. 算一下你負責的關鍵模組的 bus factor。如果是 1，你打算用什麼最小成本（文件？副負責人？結對？）把它變成 2？
  3. 回想最近給出或收到的一則 code review。它針對「作品」還是碰到了「人格」？用「把問題指向自己」的謙遜句式重寫一遍。
  4. 你的團隊有無究責事後檢討嗎？最近一次故障檢討，是「學到什麼、將改變什麼」，還是淪為找戰犯？
  5. 你的組織用哪些模糊文化詞（culture fit、夠不夠 senior）在招聘或考核？它們會不會正悄悄變成「跟我一樣」的偏見？該如何像 Googleyness 一樣明確化？

Part II — Culture
CHAPTER 02

How to Work Well on Teams

Great software is built by teams, not lone geniuses
by Brian Fitzpatrick PDF p.55–70 Culture · HRT Scale Trade-offs
"Software engineering is a team endeavor." In plain terms: great software is built by teams, not lone geniuses.

Summary

This opens the "Culture" part, and its subject is the one variable you fully control — yourself. Engineers often want to hide work-in-progress code, and the root cause is insecurity: fear of being judged on unfinished work. That insecurity feeds the Genius Myth — crediting a team's success to a single hero (Linus, Guido, Jobs, Michael Jordan), when the truth is "a spark of inspiration followed by a heroic team effort."

The central claim: software development is a team endeavor, and the foundation of healthy collaboration is three pillars — Humility, Respect, Trust (HRT). Root-cause almost any social conflict and you'll find a missing pillar. The author makes HRT operational: lose the ego, give and take criticism (you are not your code), fail fast and iterate, run blameless post-mortems, learn patience, and stay open to influence. Finally Google replaces the vague "Googley" with an explicit rubric so it can't degrade into "just like me" — an engine of unconscious bias.

Why read this

Strong technically, stuck on the team. Blunt fact: most work needs no genius-level intellect, but 100% of it requires a minimal level of social skill. What makes or breaks your career is how well you collaborate.
You need to articulate the lone-wolf risk. "Hide until perfect" feels safe but bets your time on maybe building the wrong thing. Bus factor, early feedback, and fast iteration give you the argument.
You lead or review code and keep stepping on landmines. Joe's story shows a technically-fine, socially-disastrous review; turn "you're wrong" into "I'm confused" — humility points the question at yourself.
You want a fault-tolerant, learning culture. Blameless post-mortems, "Failure is an option," and vulnerability-as-strength are the institutional basis for daring to take risks and admit mistakes.

Key details

CULTURE Help Me Hide My Code

Around 2008, Google Code got a flood of "hide branches / start hidden / wipe history" requests, all rooted in insecurity — fear of being judged on work in progress.
Takeaway: don't treat "wanting to hide" as a quirk — it's a warning light about team-culture health; insecurity is a symptom of a bigger problem.

CULTURE The Genius Myth

Linus didn't write Linux alone — he wrote a proof-of-concept kernel; his real achievement was leading and coordinating thousands. The Genius Myth = attributing a team's success to one individual.

Reality check: you're probably not a genius; even geniuses err, and elite analytical skill is worthless if you can't solve human problems — being a genius is no excuse for being a jerk.

Takeaway: the myth is another face of insecurity. Cleverness doesn't solve collaboration problems.

TRADE-OFF Hiding Considered Harmful

The bike-gear-shifter parable: you tinker alone for months; your neighbor builds it with friends, moves faster, and instantly spots the flaw you missed. Working alone is inherently riskier.
  • Early Detection: the more early feedback you solicit, the lower the risk. Live by "fail early, fail fast, fail often."
  • Bus Factor: "people who must be hit by a bus before the project is doomed." Only-you-understand-it = 1 = dangerous; raise it with docs + primary/secondary owners.
  • Pace of Progress: engineers thrive in tight feedback loops (like incremental compiling); a team is the project-level loop — "many eyes keep the project relevant and on track."
  • Offices: private offices and giant open-plan are both extremes; the best answer is small rooms of 4–8, plus protocols (tokens, headphones, "Breakpoint Mary") to protect focus.
Takeaway: balancing focus-time and high-bandwidth team connection is an art — but hiding is always the wrong end of it.

CULTURE It's All About the Team

Lone craftspeople are extremely rare; world-changing results are almost always "inspiration + heroic team effort." Great teams use stars well, but the whole is always greater than the sum of its parts.

CULTURE The Three Pillars · HRT

Root-cause almost any social conflict and you'll find a missing Humility / Respect / Trust.

CULTURE Why Do These Pillars Matter?

A predictable compiler is easier company than messy humans, but don't underestimate "playing the social game" — not manipulation, but building relationships to get things done.
Takeaway: relationships always outlast projects; Hamming's anecdote — being friendly to the secretaries earned superb help when he needed it.

CULTURE HRT in Practice

  • Lose the ego: aim for a collective ego (team pride) over personal glory; insisting "my way" costs you a small, steady tax across your career.
  • Give and take criticism: separate critique of the work from attacks on the person. The rule: "you are not your code." Rewrite "you got it wrong" as "I'm confused — would the xyzzy pattern be clearer?"
  • Fail fast and iterate: the CEO who "just spent $10M training you"; Google's "Failure is an option," and Google X competing to disprove ideas fast.
  • Blameless post-mortem: not apologies or scapegoats, but "what we learned and will change." Includes summary, timeline, primary cause, impact, fix & prevention action items (with owners), lessons.
  • Learn patience: the author and Karl (top-down vs bottom-up) used trust, respect, and patience to invent a new workflow — saving the project and the friendship.
  • Be open to influence: the more open you are, the more you can influence; vulnerability ("I don't know") is humility and accountability, and raises your standing. Teammates are collaborators, not competitors.

CULTURE Being Googley

"Googley" was never defined, so it became a source of unconscious bias in hiring/reviews — quietly meaning "just like me."

Google made it explicit: thrives in ambiguity, values feedback, challenges the status quo, puts the user first, cares about the team, does the right thing — and moved away from the vague term.

Takeaway: be specific about expectations — vague culture words breed bias.

Quotes (original + gloss)

The one-line thesis, refuting the genius-programmer fantasy.
"Bus factor (noun): the number of people that need to get hit by a bus before your project is completely doomed."A measure of how concentrated your project's knowledge is; a bus factor of 1 means one absence kills it.
Classic term for single-point-of-knowledge risk.

The core mindset for giving and receiving criticism.

A Google motto inverting "failure is not an option."

Key terms

HRT (Humility, Respect, Trust)
The three pillars of healthy collaboration; nearly every social conflict traces to a missing one. The base model the Culture part keeps invoking.
The Genius Myth
Attributing a team's collective success to a lone hero; a variant of insecurity that discourages sharing unfinished work.
Bus Factor
How many key people a project can lose before it's doomed; raise it via docs, primary/secondary owners, and knowledge spread.
Fail early, fail fast, fail often
Surface mistakes early via tight feedback loops to minimize fix cost; echoes Ch.1's shifting left.
You are not your code
Decouple self-worth from output so you can give and take criticism healthily.
Blameless post-mortem
A no-blame root-cause document focused on "what we learned and will change," not apologies or scapegoats.
Collective ego
Channel personal pride into team accomplishment instead of individual self-promotion.
Constructive criticism
Feedback about how the work can improve, delivered with respect and humble phrasing.
Googleyness
The explicit rubric replacing vague "Googley": thrives in ambiguity, values feedback, challenges status quo, user-first, cares about the team, does the right thing.
Pair programming
Two people, one machine; style clashes resolved with HRT and patience, adapting the workflow rather than forcing a bad fit.

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Honestly: when did you last want to "perfect it before showing anyone"? What insecurity drove that? If you'd shared a week earlier, what's the worst — and best — that could happen?
  2. Compute the bus factor of a key module you own. If it's 1, what minimal investment (docs? secondary owner? pairing?) raises it to 2?
  3. Recall a code review you gave or got. Was it about the work or did it touch the person? Rewrite it with the "point the question at yourself" phrasing.
  4. Does your team run blameless post-mortems? Was the last incident review about "what we learned and will change," or a hunt for a scapegoat?
  5. Which vague culture words (culture fit, "senior enough") does your org use in hiring/reviews? Could they be turning into "just like me" bias? How would you make them explicit, like Googleyness?

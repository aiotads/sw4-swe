CHAPTER 21

Dependency Management

依賴管理
作者 Titus Winters PDF p.457–486 Scale 規模 Time 時間 Trade-offs 取捨
"Prefer source control problems to dependency management problems." 能用版本控制問題就別用依賴管理問題。

摘要

依賴管理——管理「我們不控制的函式庫/套件/依賴網路」——是軟體工程最不被理解、最具挑戰的問題之一。它與版本控制的分野是：「這子專案是否由我們組織控制？」由此導出最強建議：寧可面對版本控制問題，也不要面對依賴管理問題（把「組織」定義得更廣通常划算）。

核心難題是以「網路」而非「單一依賴」思考。經典症狀是 diamond dependency（鑽石依賴）：libbase 被 liba/libb 用、兩者又被 libuser 用；libbase 不相容變更 → 沒有通用辦法兜起來（Java 可 shading、C++ 因 ODR 幾乎零容忍，但型別跨依賴傳遞時全失效）。匯入依賴在程式設計幾乎免費、在軟體工程有隱藏成本；提供者該講清相容性承諾（C++ stdlib／Go／Abseil／Boost）。

四種模型：①Nothing Changes（靜態，新組織的起點）②SemVer（事實標準、SAT-solver、dependency hell）③Bundled Distribution（如 Linux distro）④Live at Head（不釘版本、靠 CI＋自動重構工具，trunk-based 的延伸）。深入剖析 SemVer 的侷限（估計非證明、會 overconstrain 也 overpromise、誘因不對；MVS 提升保真度），並以「無限資源」思想實驗主張以跑下游測試的實證取代估計。最後談匯出依賴（gflags、AppEngine）：外部使用者維護成本遠高於內部。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你被鑽石依賴、版本衝突、dependency hell 反覆折磨。 本章把它拉高到「依賴網路」層次；語言技倆（shading）只能救函式救不了型別。看懂後你會更敢把問題收進組織內變成版本控制問題。
你不知道該不該、如何引入外部依賴。 Google 的判準清單＋Alice/Bob 的 third_party 故事點破：匯入依賴不是免費的，是一份需長期維護的契約（Hyrum's Law）。
你把 SemVer 當絕對真理。 它只是「人對風險的有損估計」：會 overconstrain（能動卻被擋）也 overpromise（patch 照樣弄壞別人）。認識 MVS 與「以測試取代估計」的方向。
你在考慮把程式碼對外發布。 gflags 與 AppEngine 的教訓：「丟過牆就忘」損聲譽、無計畫發布變成工程效率的稅；別發布沒有長期支援計畫的東西。

細節重點

難題 鑽石依賴與衝突需求 Diamond Dependency

核心難題是以「網路」而非「單一依賴」思考；衝突需求的經典症狀是鑽石依賴（需至少三層）。
匯入依賴：重用勝過重造，但考慮時間就有維護成本。相容性承諾：C++ stdlib（近無限期＋ABI）、Go（原始碼相容無二進位）、Abseil（破壞性 API 變更必附自動重構工具）、Boost（不保證）。Google `third_party` 的 Alice/Bob 故事：「我讓它能動 ≠ 這是被支援地運作」。

模型 四種依賴管理模型 Nothing Changes / SemVer / Bundled / Live at Head

好方案須對時間與規模都有彈性、避免衝突需求。四種常見模型，差別在「版本選擇」怎麼做：
SemVer 的侷限：它是估計非證明——會 overconstrain（liba/libb 只用 Foo，libbase 改 Bar 卻被擋）也會 overpromise（patch「安全」變更照樣因 Hyrum's Law 弄壞別人）；誘因不對（維護者只承擔一小部分成本）。MVS（最小版本選擇）：要求 ≥1.7 就用 1.7、走最小的一步、貼近作者測過的版本。SemVer 只在小規模、提供者準確、依賴細粒度、用法在預期內時行得通。

匯出 無限資源、匯出依賴 以測試取代估計、gflags/AppEngine

  • 無限資源思想實驗：若有無限運算，就以「跑下游依賴的測試」這個實證取代 SemVer 的估計——這實際上就是 Live at Head。需要：所有依賴有單元測試、網路（含反向邊）可查、CI 資源、不釘版本、納入歷史/聲譽。
  • 匯出依賴的兩種損失：①聲譽（做不好/不維護就拖累——「community over code」）②工程效率的稅（不同步、夠久所有 fork 都變貴）。
  • gflags：2006 開源旗標庫，因無法重構、法律問題注定「丟過牆」，內外版本分歧、最終被外部 fork；後來內部改了「可觀察但沒文件」的實作，依賴等價性的少數專案爆掉（Hyrum's Law）。
  • AppEngine：Python 32→64-bit＋C++ 升級綁在一起，但付費客戶不肯升級，逼得傳遞閉包裡每段 C++ 都得相容舊版近三年。
結論：對外發布 API 會暴露於外部競爭優先序與 Hyrum's Law 慣性；外部使用者維護成本遠高於內部。別發布一個你沒有長期支援計畫（與授權）的東西。

Part IV — Tools
CHAPTER 21

Dependency Management

If you can make it a source-control problem, do — it's far cheaper
by Titus Winters PDF p.457–486 Scale Time Trade-offs
"Prefer source control problems to dependency management problems." If you can pull a problem inside your organization and make it a source-control problem, do.

Summary

Dependency management — managing the network of libraries/packages/dependencies you don't control — is one of software engineering's least understood, most challenging problems. The line from version control: "Does our org control this subproject?" Hence the strongest advice: prefer source control problems over dependency-management problems (broadening "organization" is often a good trade-off).

The core difficulty is thinking in networks, not single dependencies. The canonical symptom is the diamond dependency: libbase used by liba/libb, both by libuser; an incompatible libbase change → no general way to reconcile (Java can shade, C++ has near-zero tolerance via the ODR, but all tricks fail once types pass between dependencies). Importing is nearly free in programming but has hidden costs in engineering; providers should be clear about their compatibility promise (C++ stdlib / Go / Abseil / Boost).

Four models: ① Nothing Changes (static, a new org's starting point) ② SemVer (the de facto standard, a SAT-solver, dependency hell) ③ Bundled Distribution (like a Linux distro) ④ Live at Head (unpin, rely on CI + automated refactoring tools — the trunk-based extension). It dissects SemVer's limits (an estimate not a proof; overconstrains and overpromises; wrong incentives; MVS raises fidelity) and an "infinite resources" experiment arguing for evidence from downstream tests over estimates. Finally, exporting dependencies (gflags, AppEngine): external users cost far more than internal ones.

Why read this

You're repeatedly tormented by diamond dependencies and dependency hell. The chapter lifts it to a "dependency network" view; language tricks (shading) save functions but not types. You'll be readier to pull problems inside the org as source-control problems.
You don't know whether or how to import a dependency. Google's checklist + the Alice/Bob third_party story: importing isn't free — it's a long-term maintenance contract (Hyrum's Law).
You treat SemVer as absolute truth. It's "a human's lossy estimate of risk": it overconstrains (works, but blocked) and overpromises (a patch still breaks someone). Meet MVS and "evidence over estimates."
You're considering releasing your code externally. The gflags and AppEngine lessons: "throw it over the wall and forget" costs reputation; an unplanned release is a tax on efficiency; don't release without a long-term support plan.

Key details

DIFFICULTY Diamond dependencies & conflicting requirements

The core difficulty is thinking in networks, not single dependencies; the canonical symptom of conflicting requirements is the diamond dependency (needs three layers).
Importing: reuse beats reimplementing, but time brings maintenance cost. Compatibility promises: C++ stdlib (near-indefinite + ABI), Go (source, no binary), Abseil (no breaking API change without an automated refactoring tool), Boost (no promise). Google's `third_party` Alice/Bob story: "I got it to work ≠ this works in a supported fashion."

MODELS Four dependency-management models

A good solution must be flexible with time and scale and avoid conflicting requirements. Four common models, differing in how "version selection" works:
SemVer's limits: it's an estimate, not a proof — it overconstrains (liba/libb use only Foo, but a Bar change is blocked) and overpromises ("safe" patch changes still break someone via Hyrum's Law); the incentives are wrong (maintainers bear only a fraction of the cost). MVS (Minimum Version Selection): require ≥1.7 → use 1.7, the smallest step, closest to what the author tested. SemVer works only at small scale, with accurate providers, fine-grained dependencies, and expected usage.

EXPORTING Infinite resources & exporting dependencies evidence over estimates; gflags/AppEngine

  • The infinite-resources experiment: with infinite compute, replace SemVer estimates with the evidence of "running downstream tests" — effectively Live at Head. Needs: all dependencies have unit tests, a queryable network (incl. reverse edges), CI compute, unpinned dependencies, and history/reputation factored in.
  • The two losses of exporting: ① reputation (poorly done/unmaintained drags you down — "community over code") ② a tax on engineering efficiency (out of sync; all forks get expensive over time).
  • gflags: a 2006 flags-library open-sourcing was doomed to "throw over the wall" (no refactoring, legal issues); versions diverged and it was forked externally; later internal changes to "observable-but-undocumented" details broke the few projects relying on fork equivalence (Hyrum's Law).
  • AppEngine: tying a Python 32→64-bit upgrade to a C++ upgrade, but paying customers wouldn't update, forcing every C++ in the transitive closure to stay compatible with old versions for nearly three years.
Conclusion: releasing an API exposes you to outsiders' competing priorities and Hyrum's Law inertia; external users cost far more to maintain than internal ones. Don't release something you don't have a plan (and mandate) to support long-term.

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Was your last conflict / dependency hell a diamond dependency? Could you pull it from a "dependency-management problem" into a "source-control problem" (broaden "organization," or vendor it in)? Per ch16, ch18.
  2. Apply Google's checklist to a dependency you imported: tests? compatibility promise? how long, who upgrades, how hard? Has it, like Alice/Bob's, quietly gone from innocuous to critical with no maintainer?
  3. Do you treat SemVer as a "promise" or an "estimate"? Recall an overpromise (said compatible but broke) or overconstrain (works but blocked) — is your strategy prepared for both? Does MVS apply?
  4. Of the four models, where are you and where should you be? Is your project long-lived enough that "Nothing Changes becomes false"? Do you have the tests/CI for a Live at Head direction? Per ch23.
  5. Considering releasing externally? Recall gflags "throw over the wall" and AppEngine "paying customers wouldn't upgrade for three years" — do you have a long-term support plan and mandate? Ready to bear external Hyrum's Law dependence?

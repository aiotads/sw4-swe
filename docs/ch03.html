CHAPTER 03

Knowledge Sharing

知識分享
作者 Nina Chen、Mark Barolak PDF p.71–96 Culture 文化 Scale 規模 Trade-offs 取捨
"Your organization should be able to answer most of its own questions." 你的組織，應該有能力回答自己大部分的問題。

摘要

這章講組織如何讓「專家腦袋裡的知識」流動到「每個需要它的人」手上。作者開門見山：你的組織比網路上任何陌生人都更懂自己的問題領域，所以應該能自答大部分問題——前提是同時擁有知道答案的專家與散播知識的機制。這些機制從最簡單（提問、寫下來）到結構化（教程、課程）都有，但最重要的前提是一種學習文化，而學習文化需要心理安全感——讓人敢承認自己不懂。

全章先盤點知識不流動的六種病灶（缺乏心理安全感、資訊孤島、SPOF、全有全無的專業度、鸚鵡學舌、鬧鬼墓園），再給出一條光譜式解方：知識分享必須次線性擴展（scale sublinearly）。從一對一的人類專家（高頻寬但不擴展），到群組／郵件列表／問答系統（更擴展但更可怕），再到組織級的權威資訊源（開發指南、go/ 連結、codelabs、靜態分析）。書寫知識與部落知識互補，沒有單一解。最後以 Google 的 readability 收尾——透過 code review 標準化的師徒制，刻意用「線性擴展換一致性」的取捨，並有 EPR 研究證實對工程速度淨正面。

為什麼要讀這一篇

團隊有個「什麼都只有他懂」的人，你覺得危險卻說不清。 這章給你精確詞彙——SPOF、bus factor、全有全無的專業度——讓你把風險講成可管理的工程問題，而非「等他離職再說」。
你（或新人）卡住時不敢問。 「我再多試一下就好」是初學者最大的錯。本章把「敢問」拉高到團隊效能第一要素（研究：心理安全感是高效團隊最重要因子），並教你在大群組裡維持那份安全感。
想接手沒人敢碰的 legacy 程式碼。 「看不懂乾脆重寫」的衝動，用 Chesterton's fence 正面拆解：先搞懂它為什麼在那裡，再決定要不要動——直接決定你重構決策的品質。
想說服主管「寫文件、教別人」值得投資。 本章把知識分享講成非對稱取捨——少數人投入、多數人長期受惠——並給出 peer bonus、readability 等真實的制度化誘因。

細節重點

取捨 Challenges to Learning 學習的挑戰

缺乏學習文化時會冒出一組可辨識的病灶，且隨公司擴張而惡化。這六個詞是你的「組織健康聽診器」。
  • 缺乏心理安全感：怕在他人面前冒險/犯錯而被罰，表現為恐懼文化、迴避透明。
  • 資訊孤島：各部分各自為政——衍生資訊破碎（局部全貌）、資訊重複（重造輪子）、資訊歪斜（各做各的、可能衝突）。書中比喻：不是單一全域最佳解，而是一堆局部最佳解。
  • SPOF（單點失效）：關鍵資訊只在一人手上；與 ch02 的 bus factor 同源，常因「我來幫你就好」的善意而生。
  • 全有全無的專業度：團隊只有「全懂」與「新手」、中間真空。專家全包不帶人會自我增強。
  • 鸚鵡學舌：模仿而不理解——無腦複製模式或程式碼。
  • 鬧鬼墓園：因恐懼與迷信而沒人敢碰的程式區域。

規模 Philosophy 哲學

軟體工程＝多人開發多版本程式（multiperson development of multiversion programs）。人是核心，程式碼只是建造產品的一小部分產出。每個專家都曾是新手。
  • 一對一專家建議：無價、可客製，但不擴展——專家放假/換隊就斷線。
  • 書寫知識：能擴展到全組織，代價是較一般化、較不貼合個案、有維護成本。
  • 部落知識：存在於「個人知道」與「已被寫下」之間的縫隙；寫下並維護＝從單點解放成人人可取用。
工程意涵：即使一切完美文件化仍需真人——專家能綜合知識、判斷適用性與時效、知道去哪找或誰知道。部落與書寫知識互補，最佳配比因組織而異、隨成長改變。

文化 Setting the Stage: Psychological Safety 鋪好舞台：心理安全感

心理安全感是學習環境的關鍵；要學習就得先承認有不懂的東西。Google 研究：心理安全感是高效團隊最重要的一塊。
  • 師徒制：新人（Noogler）一進來就配 mentor——刻意不是隊友、主管或 tech lead，職責含答疑與協助 ramp-up，是「不知道還能問誰」時的安全網。導師＝在 Google 滿一年的志願者。
  • 大型群組裡的安全感：向一大群陌生人提問很可怕（問題可能被存檔多年），需求被放大。關鍵是讓互動合作而非對抗。
推薦模式（合作）反模式（對抗）
基本問題/錯誤被引導到正確方向基本問題/錯誤被挑刺，提問者被責備
解釋的目的是幫提問者學會解釋的目的是炫耀自己的知識
回應友善、有耐心、有幫助回應居高臨下、尖酸、無建設性
互動是「一起找解法」的討論互動是分出「贏家/輸家」的爭論
Recurse Center 社交規則：不假裝驚訝、不要「其實啊…」、不要後座駕駛、不要微歧視。工程意涵：反模式常是無意間冒出的；把上表貼在團隊頻道置頂，勝過任何「要友善」的口號。

文化 Growing Your Knowledge 增長你自己的知識

知識分享從你自己開始。全章若只帶走一句：永遠在學、永遠在問。新人 ramp-up 約需六個月。
  • Ask Questions 提問：初學者最大的錯是卡住卻不求助。沒有某個神奇的一天你會突然全會——資深者也有不懂的領域，這沒關係。領導者更該示範：懂得愈多，愈知道自己不懂的有多少。回答端的耐心與善意讓人敢來求助。
  • Understand Context 理解脈絡：學習也包含理解既有設計背後的決策。接手難懂的 legacy 別急著重寫——套 Chesterton's fence：移除/改變前，先搞懂它為何存在。
工程意涵：工程師太快喊「這很爛！」。先理解脈絡，再判斷改動是否仍合理：合理就動，不合理就把推理寫給未來的讀者。許多風格指南刻意附 rationale，讓人理解而非死背（見 ch08）。

規模 Scaling Your Questions: Ask the Community 讓提問擴展：問社群

一對一頻寬高但規模有限。幫未來的自己：學到就寫下來；幫未來的新人：把它分享出去。 三種社群媒介各有取捨、互補：
  • 群組聊天：一次問很多人、快速來回，旁觀者也能學、可存檔可搜。分主題型（開放、人多、答得快）與團隊型（小、對新人安全）。缺點：缺結構，事後難萃取。
  • 郵件列表：topic-users@。易對廣受眾分享、可搜尋存檔、更有結構，被內部搜尋引擎 Moma 索引。找到答案要貼回列表。取捨：適合需脈絡的複雜問題、不適合快速來回；舊答案時效難判。
  • YAQS：Google 內部版 Stack Overflow，能連到程式碼、討論機密；有用的答案被推升、可編輯保鮮。
工程意涵：選媒介就是選取捨。書中也誠實點名 Google「email-heavy」文化暗面：偏好 email 往往只因文化習慣，未必因為它更好——選工具時的提醒。

規模 Scaling Your Knowledge: You Always Have Something to Teach 你總有東西可教

教學不限於專家；專業度不是二元，而是多維向量——這正是多元對組織成功至關重要的原因之一（見 ch04）。
  • Office Hours：固定排程面對面答疑。當問題還太模糊（連該問什麼都不知道）或太專、沒文件時特別有用。
  • Tech Talks & Classes：engEDU 與草根的 g2g（Googler2Googler）。課程含演練、更貴，保留給最重要/最難的主題；建好後可靠共用教材擴展。課程最有效的四條件：主題複雜易誤解、相對穩定、受惠於有老師、需求量大到值得定期開。
  • Documentation：第一次學某事是看出文件可改進之處的最佳時機。修錯字到記錄部落知識（見 ch10）；g3doc 讓文件緊鄰原始碼、留可稽核變更史。任何不可被發現/搜尋的文件等於不存在；要有回饋機制。
  • Code：程式碼就是知識；註解傳遞知識給未來的讀者（包括未來的你），但需主動維護否則過時。code review 對雙方都是學習機會（見 ch09）。

規模 Scaling Your Organization's Knowledge 讓組織的知識擴展

組織愈大，確保專業被分享愈難。這節是「次線性擴展」的具體工具箱。
  • 培養文化：尊重——「聰明的混蛋（brilliant jerk）」被容忍既普遍又有害，但當專家和當好人不互斥；「混蛋不是好領導」（高層〈No Jerks〉文件示範）。誘因與表彰——別嘴上一套、獎勵另一套；peer bonus（任何人可給任何人現金獎＋表彰）與 kudos 由下而上肯定貢獻。重點不是獎金，是同儕的肯定。
  • 權威資訊源：集中、全公司、由專家維護的語料庫，對抗孤島。開發指南（專家傳連結省解釋）、go/ 連結（短到能口頭分享、提供 permalink）、codelabs（靜態文件與真人課程的中間點）、靜態分析（加一個檢查，每個用戶都學到，高效擴展，見 ch20）。
  • 保持在狀況內：正式程度依重要性而定。Newsletters——寄得愈少愈有用、engagement 愈好；Testing on the Toilet / Learning on the Loo 貼在廁所隔間（見 ch11）。Communities——跨組織社群避免孤島。

取捨 Readability: Standardized Mentorship Through Code Review 可讀性：透過審查的標準化師徒制

「readability」遠不只程式好不好讀；它是全公司標準化的師徒制流程，傳播某語言的最佳實務（慣用法、結構、API 設計、函式庫、文件、測試）。
  • 起源與規模：始於一人——Craig Silverstein（員工編號 #3）逐行審每位新人的第一個重要 commit。如今約 20% 工程師參與 readability，約 1–2% 是審查者（全為志願者）。
  • 流程：強制 code review，每個 CL 都需 readability 核可。審查者被held 到最高標準：深厚語言專業＋教學能力，且必須當成師徒與合作、而非守門。
  • 為何（取捨核心）：程式碼被讀遠多於被寫。中央化做了重大取捨：只能線性擴展（依賴真人），換來易強制一致、避免孤島與規範漂移。全 codebase 一致讓讀者專注「程式在做什麼」、利於大規模變更（見 ch22）與換隊。
  • 成本與爭議：沒 readability 成員的團隊摩擦增加、可能多輪 review、線性擴展劣勢。短期成本與長期收益不在同一時間尺度（前提：程式碼被寫來活數年/數十年；短命程式如 experimental/、Area 120 豁免）。它確實有爭議。
  • 證據：內部 EPR（工程生產力研究）團隊研究（見 ch07）顯示 readability 對速度淨正面：有 readability 的作者 CL 顯著更快被審查與提交、自評品質更高、絕大多數覺得值得且改變了行為。
工程意涵：readability 是教科書級的「用線性成本換次線性一致性收益」案例，並用資料而非直覺驗證取捨——正是 ch01「工程是要有理由的決策」的縮影。

關鍵字補充

Psychological Safety（心理安全感）
讓人敢冒險、敢犯錯、敢承認不懂而不怕被罰的環境。Google 研究指出它是高效團隊最重要的因子，也是知識分享文化的地基。
Tribal Knowledge（部落知識）
存在於「個人知道」與「已被文件化」之間縫隙的未成文知識。寫下並維護就從單點解放成人人可取用。
SPOF（單點失效）
關鍵資訊只在一人手上的瓶頸；與 ch02 的 bus factor 同源，常因「我來幫你就好」的善意而生。
All-or-Nothing Expertise（全有全無的專業度）
團隊只有「全懂的專家」與「新手」、中間真空；專家全包不帶人會自我增強此問題。
Haunted Graveyard（鬧鬼墓園）
因恐懼與迷信而沒人敢碰的程式區域。與「鸚鵡學舌」（模仿而不理解）並列為兩種知識缺口症狀。
Chesterton's Fence（柴斯特頓的籬笆）
改變或移除某物前，先理解它為何存在的原則。重構與接手 legacy 程式碼的決策安全閥。
Canonical Source of Information（權威資訊源）
集中、全公司、由專家維護的資訊語料庫，標準化並傳播知識、對抗孤島；建立成本高、效益廣。
go/ links（go/ 連結）
Google 內部短網址（與 Go 語言無關）。短到可口頭分享、提供穩定 permalink，是低摩擦知識分享的關鍵基礎建設。
Readability（可讀性制度）
透過 code review 標準化的全公司師徒制；每個 CL 需其核可。刻意用「線性擴展」換 codebase 一致性與工程師成長，經 EPR 研究證實淨正面。
Asymmetrical Trade-off（非對稱取捨）
少數人投入時間、多數人長期受惠的投資結構（如寫文件）。對組織整體有利，但需要好誘因才能促成。

延伸提問

  1. 盤點你團隊的關鍵系統，哪一塊最接近 all-or-nothing expertise 或 SPOF？若那位專家明天放長假，最先垮的是什麼？你能用什麼最小成本（office hours？一篇 g3doc？一場 tech talk？）開始把它變成「可分散的知識」？
  2. 你的團隊頻道，比較像合作模式還是對抗模式？最近一次有新人問「基本問題」時，發生了什麼？Recurse Center 四條社交規則，哪一條你的團隊最常違反？
  3. 你手上有沒有一座 haunted graveyard——「能動但沒人敢碰」的程式碼？套上 Chesterton's fence：你真的知道它為何長這樣嗎？要回答，你該問誰、查哪份文件？
  4. 你上次學會新流程後，有沒有回頭修「Getting Started」文件裡讓你卡住的那一步？若沒有，是什麼擋住你（沒權限？覺得不是我的事？沒回饋機制）？
  5. readability 用「線性擴展換一致性」。你的組織有沒有哪個流程也在做類似取捨（強制 review？中央化平台團隊？）？它的短期成本 vs 長期收益落在什麼時間尺度？你會怎麼像 EPR 一樣用資料驗證它值不值得？

Part II — Culture
CHAPTER 03

Knowledge Sharing

Move the answers from the few who know to everyone who needs them
by Nina Chen & Mark Barolak PDF p.71–96 Culture Scale Trade-offs
"Your organization should be able to answer most of its own questions." In plain terms: the answers already live inside your company — the job is distribution, not invention.

Summary

This chapter is about moving knowledge out of experts' heads and into the hands of everyone who needs it. The opening is blunt: your organization understands its own domain better than any stranger online, so it should be able to answer most of its own questions — given both experts who know the answers and mechanisms to distribute that knowledge. Those range from the trivial (ask; write it down) to the structured (tutorials, classes), but the crucial precondition is a culture of learning, which requires psychological safety — the safety to admit you don't know.

It first names six pathologies of non-flowing knowledge (lack of psychological safety, information islands, SPOF, all-or-nothing expertise, parroting, haunted graveyards), then offers a spectrum: knowledge sharing must scale sublinearly. From one-to-one human experts (high bandwidth, doesn't scale), through groups / mailing lists / Q&A (more scalable but scarier), to org-wide canonical sources (developer guides, go/ links, codelabs, static analysis). Written and tribal knowledge complement each other — no silver bullet. It closes with Google's readability program: mentorship standardized through code review, deliberately trading linear scaling for consistency, shown net-positive for velocity by internal EPR research.

Why read this

Your team has an "only-they-understand-it" person and you can't name the danger. This gives you the vocabulary — SPOF, bus factor, all-or-nothing expertise — to frame it as a manageable engineering problem, not "we'll deal with it when they leave."
You (or your new hires) freeze instead of asking. "Let me try harder first" is the beginner's biggest mistake. The chapter makes daring to ask the top factor in team effectiveness (psychological safety is the #1 predictor) and shows how to preserve it in large groups.
You're inheriting legacy code nobody dares touch. The "rewrite because I can't read it" urge gets dismantled by Chesterton's fence: understand why it's there before removing it — directly raising refactoring quality.
You want to argue that docs and teaching are worth it. The chapter frames sharing as an asymmetrical trade-off — a few invest, many benefit — and shows the institutional incentives (peer bonuses, readability) that make it happen.

Key details

TRADE-OFF Challenges to Learning

Without a learning culture, a recognizable set of pathologies emerge — and they get worse as the company scales. These six words are a stethoscope for org health.
  • Lack of psychological safety: fear of risk/mistakes in front of others; shows up as a culture of fear and avoidance of transparency.
  • Information islands: groups don't communicate — producing fragmentation (incomplete picture), duplication (reinvented wheels), skew (same thing done differently, possibly conflicting). The book's image: not one global maximum, but many local maxima.
  • SPOF: critical info in one person; same as Ch.2's bus factor, often born of "let me just handle that for you."
  • All-or-nothing expertise: only total experts and novices, nothing in between. Experts doing everything self-reinforces it.
  • Parroting: mimicry without understanding — mindlessly copying code.
  • Haunted graveyards: code nobody dares touch out of fear and superstition.

SCALE Philosophy

Software engineering = the multiperson development of multiversion programs. People are the core; code is a small part of building a product. Every expert was once a novice.
  • One-to-one advice: invaluable and tailorable, but doesn't scale — the expert leaves and the team is stranded.
  • Documented knowledge: scales org-wide, at the cost of being generalized, less applicable to individuals, and carrying maintenance cost.
  • Tribal knowledge: lives in the gap between what individuals know and what's written; document it = freed from a single point to available to all.
Takeaway: even perfect docs still need humans — experts synthesize, judge applicability and freshness, and know where to find things (or who does). Tribal and written knowledge complement each other; the best mix varies by org and changes as it grows.

CULTURE Setting the Stage: Psychological Safety

Psychological safety is critical to learning; to learn you must first admit you don't understand. Google's research: it's the single most important part of an effective team.
  • Mentorship: a Noogler gets a mentor — deliberately not a teammate, manager, or tech lead — whose job includes answering questions and a safety net for "I don't know who else to ask." Mentors are volunteers with 1+ years at Google.
  • Safety in large groups: asking a crowd of strangers is scary (questions get archived for years), so the need is amplified. Keep interactions cooperative, not adversarial.
Recommended (cooperative)Antipattern (adversarial)
Basic questions/mistakes guided the right wayBasic questions/mistakes picked on; asker chastised
Explanations aim to help the asker learnExplanations aim to show off one's knowledge
Responses are kind, patient, helpfulResponses are condescending, snarky, unconstructive
Shared discussions to find solutionsArguments with "winners" and "losers"
Recurse Center social rules: no feigned surprise, no well-actuallys, no back-seat driving, no subtle -isms. Takeaway: antipatterns emerge unintentionally; pinning that table to your channel beats any "be nice" slogan.

CULTURE Growing Your Knowledge

Sharing starts with yourself. If you take one thing: always be learning; always be asking questions. Ramp-up takes ~six months.
  • Ask Questions: the beginner's biggest mistake is not asking when stuck. There's no magic day you suddenly know everything — even veterans have gaps, and that's OK. Leaders must model it: the more you know, the more you know you don't. Patience and kindness when answering make it safe to ask.
  • Understand Context: learning includes understanding decisions behind existing design. Don't rush to rewrite hard legacy — apply Chesterton's fence: before removing/changing, understand why it's there.
Takeaway: engineers reach for "this is bad!" too fast. Seek context, then judge: if your change still makes sense, make it; if not, write down your reasoning for future readers. Many style guides include the rationale so readers understand rather than memorize (see ch08).

SCALE Scaling Your Questions: Ask the Community

One-to-one is high-bandwidth but limited. Do future-you a favor: write down what you learn. Do future newcomers a favor: share it. Three media, each with trade-offs:
  • Group chats: ask many at once, quick back-and-forth, onlookers learn, often archived/searchable. Topic-driven (open, fast) vs team-oriented (small, safer). Downside: little structure, hard to extract later.
  • Mailing lists: topic-users@. Easy to share widely, searchable archives, more structure, indexed by Moma. Found your answer? Post it back. Trade-offs: great for complex context-heavy questions, clumsy for quick exchanges; old answers' relevance is hard to judge.
  • YAQS: Google's internal Stack Overflow — link to code, discuss confidential info; helpful answers promoted, editable to stay fresh.
Takeaway: choosing a medium is choosing a trade-off. The chapter is candid about Google's email-heavy culture: it defaults to email mostly from habit, not because it's better — a warning when you pick tools.

SCALE Scaling Your Knowledge: You Always Have Something to Teach

Teaching isn't limited to experts; expertise isn't binary but a multidimensional vector — one reason diversity is critical to org success (see ch04).
  • Office hours: scheduled face-to-face Q&A. Best when a problem is too ambiguous to even know what to ask, or so specialized there's no doc.
  • Tech talks & classes: engEDU and grassroots g2g (Googler2Googler). Classes have exercises, cost more, reserved for the most important/difficult topics; scale via shared materials. Work best when: complex enough to cause misunderstanding, relatively stable, benefit from live teachers, and have enough demand to run regularly.
  • Documentation: the first time you learn something is the best time to improve the docs. Typo fixes to documenting tribal knowledge (see ch10); g3doc puts docs next to source with auditable history. Undiscoverable docs might as well not exist; ensure a feedback path.
  • Code: code is knowledge; comments transmit it to future readers (including Future You) but go stale without upkeep. Code review teaches both sides (see ch09).

SCALE Scaling Your Organization's Knowledge

Bigger orgs make sharing harder. This section is the concrete toolbox for sublinear scaling.
  • Cultivating culture: Respect — tolerance/reverence of the "brilliant jerk" is pervasive and harmful, but expert ≠ unkind; "jerks are not good leaders" (the top-down "No Jerks" doc). Incentives — don't reward behavior that violates your stated values; peer bonuses (any Googler → any Googler, cash + recognition) and kudos recognize contributions bottom-up. It's not the bonus — it's the peer acknowledgement.
  • Canonical sources: centralized, expert-maintained corpuses that counter islands. Developer guides (experts share a link instead of re-explaining), go/ links (short enough to say aloud; permalinks), codelabs (halfway between docs and classes), static analysis (add one check, every user learns it — scales efficiently, see ch20).
  • Staying in the loop: formality matches importance. Newsletters — less often + more useful = better engagement; Testing on the Toilet / Learning on the Loo posted in toilet stalls (see ch11). Communities — cross-org groups avoid islands.

TRADE-OFF Readability: Standardized Mentorship Through Code Review

"Readability" means far more than readable code — it's a company-wide, standardized mentorship process for spreading a language's best practices (idioms, structure, API design, libraries, docs, tests).
  • Origin and scale: started with one person — Craig Silverstein (employee #3) reviewed every new hire's first major commit line-by-line. Today ~20% of engineers participate, ~1–2% are reviewers (all volunteers).
  • The process: code review is mandatory, and every CL needs readability approval. Reviewers are held to the highest bar: deep language expertise plus teaching ability, treating it as mentoring and cooperation, not gatekeeping.
  • Why (the trade-off): code is read far more than written. Centralizing makes a major trade-off: it scales only linearly (human-driven), but makes consistency enforceable and avoids islands and drift. Corpus-wide consistency lets readers focus on what code does, eases large-scale changes (ch22) and team switches.
  • Costs & controversy: more friction for teams without a readability member, possible extra review rounds, linear-scaling disadvantage. Short-term costs and long-term benefits are on different timescales (premise: code lives for years/decades; short-lived code like experimental/ and Area 120 is exempt). It is genuinely controversial.
  • The evidence: the internal EPR (Engineering Productivity Research) team (see ch07) found readability net-positive for velocity: CLs by readability authors are reviewed/submitted significantly faster, self-rated quality is higher, and a strong majority find it worthwhile and changed their behavior.
Takeaway: readability is a textbook case of trading linear cost for sublinear gains in consistency, validated with data, not intuition — ch01's "engineering is decisions made with reasons" in miniature.

Key terms

Psychological safety
An environment where people dare to take risks, err, and admit they don't know — without fear of punishment. The top predictor of an effective team and the foundation of the sharing culture.
Tribal knowledge
Unwritten knowledge in the gap between what individuals know and what's documented. Write it down and it goes from a single point to available to all.
SPOF (single point of failure)
A bottleneck where critical info lives in one person; same as Ch.2's bus factor, often born of "let me just handle that for you."
All-or-nothing expertise
A team split into total experts and novices with nothing between; experts doing everything self-reinforces it.
Haunted graveyard
Code nobody dares touch out of fear and superstition. Paired with "parroting" (mimicry without understanding).
Chesterton's fence
Understand why something exists before removing/changing it. A safety valve for refactoring and inheriting legacy code.
Canonical source of information
A centralized, expert-maintained corpus to standardize and propagate knowledge and counter islands; expensive to build, broad in benefit.
go/ links
Google's internal URL shortener (unrelated to the Go language). Short enough to say aloud and a stable permalink — key low-friction sharing infrastructure.
Readability
A company-wide mentorship program standardized through code review; every CL needs its approval. Trades linear scaling for consistency and growth; EPR research shows it's net-positive.
Asymmetrical trade-off
An investment where a few spend the time and many benefit long-term (e.g., docs). Good for the org overall, but needs good incentives to happen.

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Inventory your team's critical systems. Which is closest to all-or-nothing expertise or a SPOF? If that expert took a long leave tomorrow, what breaks first? What minimal investment (office hours? one g3doc? one tech talk?) starts turning it into distributable knowledge?
  2. Does your team channel look more cooperative or adversarial? What happened the last time a newcomer asked a "basic question"? Which of the Recurse Center's four rules do you break most?
  3. Do you have a haunted graveyard — code that "works but nobody dares touch"? Apply Chesterton's fence: do you actually know why it's shaped that way? Who would you ask, which doc would you read?
  4. The last time you learned a new process, did you go back and fix the "Getting Started" doc at the step that tripped you up? If not, what stopped you (no access? not my job? no feedback path?)?
  5. Readability trades linear scaling for consistency. Is there a process in your org making a similar trade-off (mandatory review? a centralized platform team?)? On what timescales do its costs and benefits land? How would you validate it with data, EPR-style?

Part III — Processes ｜ 流程（末章）
CHAPTER 15

Deprecation

棄用
作者 Hyrum Wright PDF p.339–352 Time 時間 Trade-offs 取捨 Scale 規模
"Removing things is often more difficult than building them to begin with." 移除一個東西，往往比當初建造它還難——因為既有使用者經常以超出它原始設計的方式在用它。

摘要

所有系統都會老。 即使位元不腐，新技術與生態演進也會讓舊系統過時——而它們需要持續維護、艱澀專業、隨生態偏離愈來愈費工。Deprecation（棄用）就是「有序遷移離開、並最終移除過時系統」的流程，屬軟體工程（管理跨時間）而非單純程式設計。根本前提呼應 ch09：程式碼是負債，不是資產——帶來價值的是功能；該最大化的是「每單位程式碼交付多少功能」，而移除不再需要的系統正是最容易的辦法之一。

為何這麼難？ ①Hyrum's Law（移除＝終極變更，抖出意外依賴）②新系統必然不同（一對一對應罕見）③情感依附（「我喜歡這段程式！」）④政治與經費（苟延殘喘的成本不易觀察）。故原地漸進演進通常比完全替換便宜。

棄用最好在設計時就規劃（像核電廠把除役納入）。它是一個光譜：advisory（無期限無強制，「hope is not a strategy」） ↔ compulsory（有期限與強制機制、且必須配人力做到完成）。管理：要有明確 process owner、設漸進里程碑、用三類工具（discovery／migration／backsliding prevention）。棄用警告必須同時 actionable 與 relevant，否則造成 alert fatigue。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你的程式碼庫塞滿「沒人敢關、也沒人維護」的舊系統。 本章說明為何該關（程式碼是負債、多系統並存拖慢新系統演進）與為何難關（Hyrum's Law、情感依附、政治經費）。「移除」會變成正當的一招。
你只在舊系統上貼個 deprecated 警告就走人。 直接點破：「hope is not a strategy」——advisory 鮮少真的讓人遷移；要完成得靠 compulsory＋專門團隊配人力。
你被派去做沒人想做、看不到進度的清理專案。 方法：設漸進里程碑（刪一個關鍵子元件就慶祝）、集中專業到一個團隊、當「重要但不緊急」的 20% 時間題材。
你在設計新系統。 少見卻關鍵的視角：為未來的除役而設計（像核電廠）；謹記「別啟動組織不打算支撐其預期壽命的專案」。

細節重點

為何 Why Deprecate? 程式碼是負債

起點是 「程式碼是負債，不是資產」。系統老不等於過時（LaTeX）；棄用最適合「可證明已過時、且有功能相當替代品」者。多系統並存的維護成本長期大幅成長、且拖慢新系統演進（仍要相容）。該最大化「每單位程式碼的功能」——移除過時系統是最容易的辦法之一。
但有上限：組織能同時進行的棄用工作有限（道路鋪設比喻：全封施工誰都動不了，集中火力反而更快）——審慎選、並承諾做完。

為何難 Why Is Deprecation So Hard? 四個阻力

  • Hyrum's Law：使用者愈多愈可能有人以未預期方式依賴；移除是終極變更，抖出意外依賴者。
  • 新系統必然不同：「更好」也意味「不同」（否則沒遷移誘因）→一對一對應罕見。
  • 情感依附：「我喜歡這段程式！」——對策：repo 可搜尋歷史（移除的程式找得回來，見 ch17）。
  • 政治與經費：苟延殘喘的成本不易觀察 →用 ch07 研究方法提供證據。
故：原地漸進演進（in-place refactoring）通常比完全替換便宜；整體遷移極貴且常被低估。設計時就規劃棄用（像核電廠把除役納入）——問「使用者多好遷移？能否漸進替換？」並謹記「別啟動組織不打算支撐其預期壽命的專案」。

類型 Advisory ↔ Compulsory 棄用的光譜

棄用不是單一流程，而是一個光譜——從「希望某天關掉」到「明天就消失，客戶最好準備好」。

警告 Deprecation Warnings actionable＋relevant

「標了就 hope 它消失」行不通；警告累積（尤其遞移情境）會造成 alert fatigue（警告疲勞）。好警告需兩性質：actionability（可行動——告訴下一步） 與 relevance（相關——在使用者正要做該動作時浮現）。Google 用 ErrorProne／clang-tidy 把警告限定在新改動的行（見 ch20），更具侵入性的只給強制棄用。

管理 Managing the Process owner・里程碑・三類工具

沒有明確 process owner，棄用幾乎不會有進展（替代方案：永不棄用＝永遠維護；或委派給使用者＝退化成 advisory）。被遺棄的專案仍需棄用專家、以移除為主要目標（「重要但不緊急」的 20% 時間題材）。別把「完全移除」當唯一里程碑——設漸進里程碑並慶祝（如刪掉一個關鍵子元件）。

關鍵字補充

Deprecation（棄用）
有序遷移離開、並最終移除過時系統的流程；屬軟體工程（管理跨時間）而非程式設計。
Code Is a Liability（程式碼是負債）
帶來價值的是功能而非程式本身；應最大化「每單位程式碼交付的功能」，移除過時系統是最容易的辦法之一（呼應 ch09）。

棄用適合「可證明已過時且有功能相當替代品」者；LaTeX 般歷久彌新雖老卻非過時。
Advisory Deprecation（建議性）
無期限、無強制、非高優先；擅長宣傳新系統、輕推，但不能指望它完成遷移（好處須變革性）。
Compulsory Deprecation（強制性）
帶移除期限與強制機制；須由專門團隊配足人力做到完成，否則淪為「無經費的強制令」。
Hope Is Not a Strategy
SRE 名言；只貼 deprecated 警告而不主動遷移，鮮少奏效。
In-place Refactoring vs Replacement
含 turndown 成本後，原地漸進演進通常比換新系統便宜；整體遷移極貴且常被低估。
Actionability & Relevance
好棄用警告的兩性質：能據以採取行動、且在對的時機浮現；否則造成 alert fatigue。
Alert Fatigue（警告疲勞）
警告累積到使用者全部忽略；故 Google 把警告限定在新改動的行（ErrorProne/clang-tidy）。
Backsliding Prevention（防回潮）
防止新增對「正在移除之物」的用法；微觀 Tricorder＋@deprecated，宏觀 build 系統 visibility whitelist。
Process Owner（流程負責人）
棄用必須有明確 owner、以移除為主要目標；否則退化成 advisory 或永久維護。
Discovery / Migration / Backsliding
三類棄用工具：Code Search/Kythe＋全域測試（發現）、LSC（遷移）、Tricorder/whitelist（防回潮）。

延伸提問

  1. 盤點你程式碼庫裡「沒人敢關、也沒人明確擁有」的舊系統：它們真的「過時」（有替代品）還是只是「老」？用「每單位程式碼的功能」這把尺，移除哪一個投報率最高？對照 ch09。
  2. 你最近一次推遷移，是只貼警告就走人（advisory），還是有期限＋專門團隊配人力（compulsory）？「hope is not a strategy」在你身上應驗了嗎？舊系統的「概念拉力」有多強？
  3. 你的棄用警告可行動且相關嗎？有告訴使用者下一步、且在他正要寫用到它的程式時浮現嗎？還是淪為被全部忽略的 alert fatigue？能不能限定在新改動的行？
  4. 你正在設計的新系統，有沒有為未來的除役而設計？問核電廠的兩個問題：使用者要遷走多容易？能否漸進替換各部分？你的組織真打算支撐它的預期壽命嗎？
  5. 假設你被派一個棄用專案：你會設哪些漸進里程碑？用哪三類工具（discovery/migration/backsliding）？能不能把遷移專業集中到一個團隊並爭取強制權與人力？對照 ch22。

Part III — Processes (final chapter)
CHAPTER 15

Deprecation

Deleting is harder than building — people depend on it in unforeseen ways
by Hyrum Wright PDF p.339–352 Time Trade-offs Scale
"Removing things is often more difficult than building them to begin with." Deleting is harder than building — because people depend on the system in ways it was never designed for.

Summary

All systems age. Even though bits don't rot, new tech and an evolving ecosystem render old systems obsolete — demanding ongoing maintenance, esoteric expertise, and ever more work as they diverge. Deprecation is the orderly migration away from, and eventual removal of, obsolete systems — software engineering (managing over time), not programming. The foundational premise echoes ch09: code is a liability, not an asset — value comes from functionality, so maximize functionality per unit of code, and removing systems no longer needed is one of the easiest ways.

Why so hard? ① Hyrum's Law (removal = the ultimate change, shaking loose unexpected dependents) ② a new system is necessarily different (one-to-one matches are rare) ③ emotional attachment ("I like this code!") ④ politics and funding (the cost of limping along isn't observable). So evolving in place is usually cheaper than full replacement.

Deprecation is best planned at design time (like a power plant designed for decommissioning). It's a continuum: advisory (no deadline, no enforcement — "hope is not a strategy") ↔ compulsory (a deadline with enforcement, staffed to completion). Management: have an explicit process owner, set incremental milestones, use three tool categories (discovery / migration / backsliding prevention). Warnings must be both actionable and relevant, or they cause alert fatigue.

Why read this

Your codebase is full of systems nobody dares turn off and nobody maintains. This explains why to turn them off (code is a liability; coexistence slows the new system) and why it's hard (Hyrum's Law, attachment, politics). "Removal" becomes a legitimate move.
You slap a deprecation warning on the old system and walk away. Blunt: "hope is not a strategy" — advisory rarely makes people migrate; finishing needs compulsory + a staffed team.
You're assigned a thankless cleanup project with no visible progress. How: set incremental milestones (celebrate deleting a key subcomponent), centralize expertise in one team, treat it as 20%-time "important-not-urgent" work.
You're designing a new system. A rare but crucial lens: design for eventual decommissioning (like a power plant); remember "don't start projects your org isn't committed to support for its lifespan."

Key details

WHY Why Deprecate? code is a liability

The starting point is "code is a liability, not an asset." Old ≠ obsolete (LaTeX); deprecate the demonstrably obsolete with a comparable replacement. Coexisting systems' maintenance costs grow substantially and slow the new system's evolution. Maximize functionality per unit of code — removal is one of the easiest ways.
But there's a limit: an org can only do so much deprecation at once (the road-paving analogy — close every road and nobody moves; focused crews finish faster) — choose carefully and commit to finishing.

WHY HARD Why Is Deprecation So Hard? four sources of resistance

  • Hyrum's Law: more users, more unforeseen dependence; removal is the ultimate change, shaking loose unexpected dependents.
  • A new system is necessarily different: "better" means "different" (or no migration incentive) → one-to-one matches are rare.
  • Emotional attachment: "I like this code!" — mitigation: a repo searchable historically (removed code is findable, see ch17).
  • Politics and funding: the cost of limping along isn't observable → use ch07 research methods for evidence.
So: in-place refactoring is usually cheaper than full replacement; wholesale migrations are extremely expensive and underestimated. Plan deprecation at design time (a power plant accounts for decommissioning) — ask "how easily can users migrate? can I replace incrementally?" and remember "don't start projects your org isn't committed to support for its lifespan."

TYPES Advisory ↔ Compulsory the deprecation continuum

Deprecation isn't a single process but a continuum — from "we hope to turn this off someday" to "this goes away tomorrow, be ready."

WARNINGS Deprecation Warnings actionable + relevant

"Mark it and hope" doesn't work; warnings accumulate (especially transitively) into alert fatigue. A good warning needs two properties: actionability (tells the next step) and relevance (surfaces when the user does the action). Google uses ErrorProne/clang-tidy to limit warnings to newly changed lines (see ch20); more intrusive warnings only for compulsory deprecations.

MANAGING Managing the Process owner · milestones · three tools

Without an explicit process owner, deprecation rarely progresses (alternatives: never deprecate = maintain forever; or delegate to users = degrade to advisory). Abandoned projects still need experts with removal as the primary goal (great 20%-time work). Don't make "complete removal" the only milestone — set incremental milestones and celebrate them (deleting a key subcomponent).

Key terms

Deprecation
The orderly migration away from, and eventual removal of, obsolete systems; software engineering (managing over time), not programming.
Code is a liability
Value comes from functionality, not code itself; maximize functionality per unit of code, with removal one of the easiest ways (echoes ch09).
Obsolete ≠ old
Deprecate the demonstrably obsolete with a comparable replacement; an enduring system like LaTeX is old but not obsolete.
Advisory deprecation
No deadline, no enforcement, not high priority; good for advertising and nudging, but can't be counted on for migration (benefits must be transformative).
Compulsory deprecation
A removal deadline with an enforcement mechanism; must be staffed to completion, or it's an "unfunded mandate."
Hope is not a strategy
An SRE maxim; merely posting a warning rarely causes migration.
In-place refactoring vs replacement
Including turndown costs, evolving in place is usually cheaper; wholesale migrations are extremely expensive and underestimated.
Actionability & relevance
A good warning's two properties: it enables an action and surfaces at the right moment; else alert fatigue.
Alert fatigue
Warnings accumulate until users ignore them all; hence limit warnings to newly changed lines (ErrorProne/clang-tidy).
Backsliding prevention
Stopping new uses of what's being removed; micro via Tricorder + @deprecated, macro via build-system visibility whitelists.
Process owner
Deprecation needs an explicit owner with removal as the primary goal; otherwise it degrades to advisory or perpetual maintenance.
Discovery / migration / backsliding
Three tool categories: Code Search/Kythe + global tests (discovery), LSC (migration), Tricorder/whitelists (backsliding prevention).

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Inventory the old systems "nobody dares turn off and nobody clearly owns": truly *obsolete* (with a replacement) or merely *old*? By "functionality per unit of code," which has the best ROI to remove? Per ch09.
  2. Was your last migration push just a posted-and-abandoned warning (advisory), or a deadline + a staffed team (compulsory)? Did "hope is not a strategy" prove true? How strong was the old system's "conceptual pull"?
  3. Are your warnings actionable and relevant? Do they tell the next step and surface *while writing code using it*? Or are they ignored alert fatigue? Can you limit them to newly changed lines?
  4. Is the new system you're designing designed for eventual decommissioning? Ask the power-plant questions: how easily can users migrate? can parts be replaced incrementally? Is your org committed to support its lifespan?
  5. Assigned a deprecation project: what incremental milestones would you set? Which three tool categories (discovery/migration/backsliding)? Can you centralize expertise in one team and win the authority and staffing? Per ch22.

CHAPTER 11

Testing Overview

測試總覽
作者 Adam Bender PDF p.235–258 Time 時間 Scale 規模 Trade-offs 取捨
"Automated testing is foundational to enabling software to change." 自動化測試是「讓軟體能夠改變」的根基——你要是喜歡它，當初就該為它寫一個測試。

摘要

這是測試三部曲（ch11→ch12→ch13→ch14）的開場。核心論點翻轉直覺：測試的首要目的不是「抓 bug」，而是「支撐改變的能力」——有了穩固測試，你不必懼怕改動，反而能擁抱它。寫測試本身還會改善系統設計（測試是程式的第一個客戶，逼你面對耦合與邊界）。

關鍵案例 Google Web Server（GWS）：2005 年一度超過 80% 的生產推送含有需回滾的、影響使用者的 bug；導入「工程師驅動、自動化測試、新程式都附測試並持續執行」後，一年內緊急推送減半，今日數萬測試、幾乎每天發布。教訓：光靠個人能力無法避免缺陷——100 人團隊就算人人每月只寫一個 bug，每個工作日仍冒出 5 個。

兩個正交維度：Size（規模）＝小（單行程）／中（單機）／大（多機），管速度與確定性；Scope（範圍）＝窄（unit）／中（integration）／廣（e2e），管驗證了多少程式。建議混合約 80% 單元／15% 整合／5% 端到端（金字塔），並警告 ice cream cone 與 hourglass 兩反模式。其餘：Beyoncé Rule、flaky 測試昂貴、coverage 不是金標準、把測試當生產程式碼對待。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你覺得「寫測試拖慢開發」。 GWS 的真實數字反駁：測試不是為抓 bug 而抓，而是讓你敢改、能快速迭代——能更快迭代的公司能更快適應技術與市場。測試從「成本」變成「速度的前提」。
你分不清 unit／integration／e2e。 兩把更精準的尺：Size（小/中/大＝單行程/單機/多機，管速度與確定性）與 Scope（窄/中/廣＝驗證多少程式）——正交。所以 Google 偏好「範圍可廣、size 要小」（用 test double 換掉昂貴依賴）。
你的套件又慢又愛亂掛（flaky）。 這正是最危險的失敗模式：不被信任的套件比沒有更糟。硬指標（接近 1% 就失去價值、Google ~0.15%）與藥方（hermetic、禁 sleep() 改輪詢、當生產程式碼）。
你被要求覆蓋率到 80%。 為何那是糟糕的目標：coverage 只量「某行被執行」不量「驗證了什麼」；80% 的地板會變天花板。該問「我顧客期待能動的，我有信心都會動嗎？」

細節重點

為何 Why Do We Write Tests? 支撐改變的能力

最簡單的測試＝一個被測行為＋一個特定輸入＋一個可觀察輸出＋一個受控環境。「抓 bug」只是動機一部分；同等重要的是支撐改變——你愈想頻繁快速地改，就愈需要快速測它的辦法。
GWS 故事：2005 年 >80% 生產推送需回滾 →導入自動測試政策 →一年內緊急推送減半。洞見：不能只靠個人能力——100 人團隊每人每月一個 bug＝每工作日 5 個新 bug。自動測試把「集體智慧」變成「全隊共享資源」。測試的價值來自工程師對它的信任——壞套件比沒有更糟。

流程 Write, Run, React 寫、跑、反應

三個活動：寫測試（分攤給全工程團隊，工程師親自為自己的程式寫）、跑測試（頻繁地跑＝CI；機器不累不膩，一天數千次；表達成程式碼故易跨環境）、對失敗做出反應（最關鍵——失敗後幾分鐘內修好，別讓失敗堆積、瞬間摧毀價值）。

效益 Benefits of Testing Code 測試的六項效益

  • 更少除錯：提交時與終其一生缺陷都更少（程式一生被改數十次，測試持續配息）。
  • 對改變更有信心：重要行為被持續驗證，鼓勵重構（純重構理想上不需動既有測試）。
  • 更好的文件：清晰的測試是可執行的文件——想知道某情況做什麼就看那個 case 的測試（呼應 ch10）。
  • 更簡單的審查：附測試讓審查者不必在腦中走過每個 case（見 ch09）。
  • 深思熟慮的設計：難測常因職責太多或依賴難管理；及早修＝日後少返工。
  • 快速、高品質的發布：數百工程師、每天數千變更的大專案也能每天發布。

維度 Test Size 小/中/大：管速度與確定性

早期教訓：大型系統級測試更慢、更不可靠、更難除錯；逐步寫小測試發現它們更快、更穩、更不痛。每個測試有兩正交維度：Size（資源）與 Scope（驗證了哪些程式）。最看重速度與確定性。
Flaky 很昂貴：0.1% × 每天 10,000 個測試＝每天查 10 個 flake；接近 1% 即失去價值，Google 壓在 ~0.15%。共通特質：hermetic（自足封閉）、「一看就懂」（因測試本身沒測試、靠人工審查）、勸阻條件與迴圈——「寫出你自己想讀的測試」。

維度 Test Scope 窄/中/廣：測試金字塔

scope＝驗證了多少程式：窄＝unit（單一 class/method）、中＝integration（少數元件互動）、廣＝e2e/system。「窄」指被驗證的程式，不是被執行的程式。size 與 scope 相關但非綁定（可寫 scope 廣卻 size 小的測試）。
兩個目標：工程生產力與產品信心。偏重單元＝快速、早期、高信心；較大測試是 sanity check，不是抓 bug 的主要手段。

哲學 Beyoncé Rule、Coverage、規模陷阱 測你不想壞掉的一切

  • Beyoncé Rule：「If you liked it, then you shoulda put a test on it.」測你不想壞掉的一切；基礎設施變更通過你所有測試卻仍弄壞產品，責任在你補測試。為失敗而測：主動模擬 RPC 錯誤/延遲、Chaos Engineering。
  • Code Coverage：只量「行被呼叫」不量「結果」，不是金標準；80% 地板易淪天花板（建議只從小測試量）。該問「被測的行為」而非單一數字。
  • Google 規模：monorepo、20 億行、每週近 2,500 萬行變更、幾乎不用分支、提交到 head、靠 TAP（見 ch23）。
  • 大型套件陷阱：brittle（脆弱）測試抵抗改變、mock 濫用（「no more mocks！」）、慢（禁 sleep() 改主動輪詢）。祕訣：把測試當生產程式碼對待、減少需支援的框架數。
  • 歷史：2005–06 測試革命；Testing Grouplet 三計畫（Orientation Classes／Test Certified 五級・1500+ 專案／Testing on the Toilet）；不強制——成功的點子會自己擴散；今由 pH 接棒。極限：品質判斷、探索式測試留給人，找到後再補自動測試。

Part III — Processes
CHAPTER 11

Testing Overview

Tests are what let software change
by Adam Bender PDF p.235–258 Time Scale Trade-offs
"Automated testing is foundational to enabling software to change." Tests are what let software change — and you test everything you don't want to break.

Summary

This opens the testing trilogy (ch11→ch12→ch13→ch14). The central claim inverts intuition: testing's primary purpose isn't to "catch bugs" but to support the ability to change — with solid tests you needn't fear change; you embrace it. Writing tests also improves design (a test is your code's first client, forcing you to confront coupling and boundaries).

The anchor case, Google Web Server (GWS): in 2005, over 80% of production pushes contained user-affecting bugs that had to be rolled back; after "engineer-driven automated testing, all new code includes tests, run continuously," emergency pushes halved within a year — today tens of thousands of tests, releasing almost daily. Lesson: you can't avoid defects on programmer ability alone — a 100-person team writing one bug each per month still produces 5 new bugs every workday.

Two orthogonal dimensions: Size = small (single process) / medium (single machine) / large (multiple machines), governing speed and determinism; Scope = narrow (unit) / medium (integration) / broad (e2e), governing how much code is validated. Recommended mix ≈ 80% unit / 15% integration / 5% e2e (the pyramid), avoiding the ice cream cone and hourglass. Plus: the Beyoncé Rule, the expense of flaky tests, why coverage isn't the gold standard, and treating tests like production code.

Why read this

You think "tests slow development." GWS's numbers rebut it: tests let you change boldly and iterate fast — faster-iterating companies adapt faster. Tests become a precondition for speed, not a cost.
You can't tell unit/integration/e2e apart. Two sharper rulers: Size (small/medium/large = single process/machine/multi-machine, governing speed and determinism) and Scope (narrow/medium/broad) — orthogonal. So Google prefers tests broad in scope but small in size (doubles swap out expensive dependencies).
Your suite is slow and flaky. The most dangerous failure mode: an untrusted suite is worse than none. Hard numbers (value erodes near 1%; Google ~0.15%) and remedies (hermetic, ban sleep() for polling, treat as production code).
You're told to hit 80% coverage. Why it's a bad goal: coverage measures "invoked," not "verified"; an 80% floor becomes a ceiling. Better: "am I confident everything customers expect will work?"

Key details

WHY Why Do We Write Tests?

The simplest test = one behavior + a specific input + an observable output + a controlled environment. "Catching bugs" is only part of the motivation; equally important is supporting change — the more and faster you change, the more you need a fast way to test.
The GWS story: 2005, >80% of pushes needed rollback → automated-testing policy → emergency pushes halved within a year. Insight: you can't rely on ability alone — 100 people, one bug each per month = 5 new bugs every workday. Tests turn "collective wisdom" into a "team-wide asset." A test's value comes from the trust engineers place in it — a bad suite is worse than none.

FLOW Write, Run, React

Three activities: write tests (shared across all engineers, for their own code), run tests (frequently = CI; machines never tire, thousands of runs/day; tests-as-code modularize across environments), react to failures (the crux — fix a break within minutes; letting failures pile up destroys their value).

BENEFITS Benefits of Testing Code the six

  • Less debugging: fewer defects at submission and throughout life (code is modified dozens of times; a test keeps paying dividends).
  • Increased confidence in changes: important behaviors continuously verified, encouraging refactoring (a pure refactor ideally needs no test changes).
  • Improved documentation: clear tests are executable documentation — read the test for a case to know what code does (echoes ch10).
  • Simpler reviews: tests spare the reviewer from mentally walking each case (see ch09).
  • Thoughtful design: hard-to-test code often has too many responsibilities; fixing early means less rework.
  • Fast, high-quality releases: even large projects with thousands of daily changes release every day.

DIMENSION Test Size small/medium/large: speed & determinism

Early lesson: large system-scale tests are slower, less reliable, harder to debug; smaller tests proved faster, more stable, less painful. Every test has two orthogonal dimensions: Size (resources) and Scope (which code is validated). The prized qualities: speed and determinism.
Flaky is expensive: 0.1% × 10,000 tests/day = 10 flakes/day; value erodes near 1%, Google holds ~0.15%. Common properties: hermetic, "obvious upon inspection" (tests aren't themselves tested, so human review checks them), discourage conditionals/loops — "write the test you'd like to read."

DIMENSION Test Scope narrow/medium/broad: the pyramid

Scope = how much code a test validates: narrow = unit (one class/method), medium = integration (a few components), broad = e2e/system. "Narrow" means code validated, not executed. Size and scope correlate but aren't bound (a broad-scope test can still be small).
Two goals: engineering productivity and product confidence. Favoring unit tests gives fast, early, high confidence; larger tests are sanity checks, not the primary bug-catcher.

PHILOSOPHY Beyoncé Rule, Coverage, Scale pitfalls test everything you don't want to break

  • Beyoncé Rule: "If you liked it, then you shoulda put a test on it." Test everything you don't want to break; an infra change passing all your tests yet breaking your product puts you on the hook to add the test. Test for failure: simulate RPC errors/latency, Chaos Engineering.
  • Code coverage: measures "invoked," not "result," and isn't the gold standard; an 80% floor becomes a ceiling (measure only from small tests). Ask about behaviors tested, not one number.
  • Google scale: monorepo, 2B lines, ~25M lines of change/week, almost no branching, commit to head, managed via TAP (see ch23).
  • Large-suite pitfalls: brittle tests resist change, mock abuse ("no more mocks!"), slowness (ban sleep() for active polling). The secret: treat tests like production code, reduce frameworks to support.
  • History: 2005–06 testing revolution; the Testing Grouplet's three initiatives (Orientation Classes / Test Certified, 5 levels, 1,500+ projects / Testing on the Toilet); no mandate — successful ideas spread; succeeded today by pH. Limits: quality judgment and exploratory testing stay human; add an automated test once a human finds the issue.

Which lines tests exercise; measures "invoked," not "result"; not the gold standard; an 80% floor easily becomes a ceiling (measure only from small tests).
Test doubles
Fakes/stubs/mocks replacing heavyweight dependencies with lightweight in-process ones; mock abuse is a top cause of brittle tests (see ch13).
TAP (Test Automated Platform)
The key component of Google's CI system, validating every change against the monorepo head (see ch23).

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Surface your team's mental model of testing: a "bug-catching gate" or a "safety net that lets you change"? Recall not refactoring for fear of breaking things — would solid tests have changed it? Per the Time theme.
  2. Tag recent tests by size (S/M/L) and scope (narrow/medium/broad): are any "unnecessarily large" (a double could swap out network/disk)? Is your suite near a pyramid, or an ice cream cone or hourglass?
  3. Measure your suite's flaky rate: near 1%? Have engineers started "ignoring failing tests" or "skipping on submit"? Are you fixing root causes or deferring with "auto-rerun"?
  4. Is your coverage target acting as a floor or a ceiling? If reframed to "am I confident everything customers expect will work?" and "can I catch breaking changes in dependencies?", would your assessment change?
  5. Apply the Beyoncé Rule: in your last launch, is there a behavior you care about with no automated test (performance? security? failure handling?)? Do you test for failure? Which tasks should stay human (exploratory testing, quality judgment)?

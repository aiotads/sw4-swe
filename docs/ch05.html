CHAPTER 05

How to Lead a Team

如何領導團隊
作者 Brian Fitzpatrick PDF p.109–134 Culture 文化 Scale 規模 Trade-offs 取捨
"Traditional managers worry about how to get things done, whereas great managers worry about what things get done." 傳統經理人煩惱「事情要怎麼做」；偉大的領導者煩惱「該做哪些事」。

摘要

前幾章談團隊的文化與組成，這章把鏡頭轉向那個最終讓一切運轉起來的人——領導者。Google 區分兩種角色：Manager 領導「人」、Tech Lead（TL）領導「技術」，小團隊由同一人兼任即 TLM。沒有船長的船只是一間漂流的候診室；軟體專案也一樣，沒人掌舵，工程師就只是在燒掉寶貴時間。

核心心法是僕人式領導（Servant Leadership）：新手經理最大的誘惑是「去管理」，但最好的建議是「首先，抵抗想去管理的衝動」。領導者的工作是服務團隊——移障礙、建共識、用 HRT 營造心理安全感，而非源自工業革命、對創造性工作有害的「胡蘿蔔加棒子」。作者用一長串反模式對照正向模式，最後用「人就像植物」收束：每個成員需要的「水（激勵）」與「光（方向）」不同，領導者要分辨並給對。激勵核心是內在動機三要素——自主、精熟、目的。

為什麼要讀這一篇

你是 IC，卻莫名被吸進領導位子（manageritis）。 不論你想不想，只要做得夠好，職涯某刻你很可能就站到掌舵位置。這章讓你知道「真的發生時該怎麼辦」，也順便讓你更懂自己的主管。
你剛升 TL／TLM，每天結束都覺得「我今天什麼都沒做」。 它直球面對轉職失落——你不再能指著一堆 code 說「這是我今天做的」。別再「種香蕉卻數蘋果」：管理的產出是「讓團隊快樂且有產能」。
你手上有低績效者，你一直在「希望他會變好」。 用 SRE 格言「希望不是策略」戳破拖延，給你兩個月、可量測的「help up or out」流程，並解釋為何放著不管會逼走高績效者。
你想當讓人願意留下、願意冒險的領導者。 從「放下自我並真誠道歉」、齒輪放大的「leader is always on stage」、到「人就像植物」的差異化激勵——把抽象的領導力翻成你下次 1:1 就能用的動作。

細節重點

角色 Managers, Tech Leads (and Both) 管理者、技術主管與兼任

兩種角色規劃技能相似，但「人的技能」天差地遠。
  • Engineering Manager：Google 要求其工程經理有工程背景。對團隊每人（含 TL）的績效、產能與快樂負責，同時滿足業務需求——兩者不總對齊，常陷兩難。
  • Tech Lead（TL）：負責技術面（決策、架構、優先序、velocity）。多為 IC，常要在「自己快快做」與「委派（更慢）」間抉擇——團隊成長時委派才對。
  • TLM：小團隊預設（一人兼兩面）；大團隊傾向拆成一對搭檔避免 burnout，並需資深 TLM 的 mentoring。

規模 Influencing Without Authority 沒有職權的影響力

讓組織外的人做該做的事，是你能培養的最強領導特質之一。
案例：Jeff Dean 只領導一小部分團隊，影響力卻遍及全公司。作者的 Data Liberation Front 用不到半打工程師，讓 50+ 個產品透過 Google Takeout 匯出資料、且無高層指令——做法是辨識策略需求、連結公司使命、做一個讓人易整合的工具。影響力不靠頭銜，靠「對齊使命＋降低摩擦」。

心態 IC → Leadership & The Fear 從 IC 到領導，與恐懼

產品要前進總得有人坐上駕駛座；你可能不知不覺被吸進去（manageritis）。
  • 寫更少 code、產出難量化：別「種香蕉卻數蘋果」——讓團隊快樂且有產能本身就是度量。
  • Peter Principle（彼得原理）：人傾向升到不能勝任的層級；Google 用「晉升前先在更高層級『超出期待』」防範。
  • 值得當領導的理由：① 擴展你自己（一支好團隊能寫多少 code）；② 你可能真的很擅長。
警示：別硬推沒意願的人進管理職——你會失去一個好工程師、得到一個爛經理。

文化 Servant Leadership 僕人式領導

許多經理染上「病」——對下屬做當年主管對他做的爛事（微管理、無視低績效者、雇軟柿子）。解藥是大量施用僕人式領導：像管家一樣服務團隊。
最佳建議（工程總監 Steve Vinter）：「最重要的是，抵抗想去管理的衝動。」僕人式領導者營造 HRT、移除障礙、必要時建議，但不怕弄髒手。他唯一要「管」的是團隊的技術健康與社交健康——後者更難管，卻同等重要。

取捨 The Engineering Manager: 歷史與今日 經理：從趕騾子到信任

「經理」概念承襲軍隊與工業革命的監工——「胡蘿蔔加棒子」式趕騾子管理。但研究一再顯示它對創造性工作者無效且有害：流水線工人能幾天換掉，工程師卻要數月才上手，需要滋養與空間。
  • 信任產生正向壓力：經理明確表現信任，員工會感到「不辜負信任」的壓力。Jerry 早走 15 分鐘的故事——「只要你把事做完，我不在乎你幾點走」。需要看小孩才肯工作，那才是真問題。
  • Failure Is an Option：用心理安全感讓人敢冒更大的險。「追求不可能而失敗，通常比成功於你本就會做的事成就更多」。成功當眾表揚、失敗私下給建設性批評；重大故障做 postmortem（學習而非究責）。

模式 Antipatterns ↔ Positive Patterns 反模式 vs 正向模式

✗ 反模式（別做）

  • 雇軟柿子：團隊離了你就動不了。反之要雇比你聰明、能取代你的人。
  • 無視低績效者：「希望不是策略」；設兩個月可量測目標，help up or out。
  • 忽視人的問題：Jake 在家帶新生兒卻被 Pablo 逼回辦公室——少了同理心。
  • 當大家的好朋友：別把友誼和溫和領導搞混；握有職權時對方會被迫假裝回報。
  • 降低招聘門檻：「A 級雇 A 級，B 級雇 C 級」；趕時間最易中招。
  • 把團隊當小孩：永久微管理＝招聘失敗；連文具自取都體現信任。

✓ 正向模式（要做）

  • 放下自我：培養集體團隊自我；犯錯就真心道歉（贏得尊重，不會讓你脆弱）。
  • 當禪師：保持冷靜（齒輪鏈放大）、leader is always on stage；用問問題引導對方找到自己的答案。
  • 當催化劑：建立共識（無職權也能領導；不必追求 100%）。
  • 移除路障：「知道對的人，往往比知道對的答案更有價值」。
  • 當老師與導師：最重要是判斷 mentee 需要多少幫助。
  • 設清楚目標：全員往同一個「北方」拉；寫一份精煉使命宣言。
  • 誠實：反對「讚美三明治」；仁慈但直接（Tim 與 Dean「攔火車」的故事）。
  • 追蹤幸福感：1:1 結尾問「你需要什麼？」；把隱性職涯目標明確化。

訣竅 Other Tips and Tricks 其他訣竅

  • 委派，但也弄髒手：新領導要努力委派（即使更慢）；接手新團隊就主動扛沒人想做的髒活。
  • 設法取代自己：雇比你聰明的人、給他們責任與偶爾領導的機會。
  • 知道何時興風作浪：難題別騙自己「等一下就好」——拖愈久傷害愈大，立刻處理。
  • 擋住混亂、提供空中掩護：混亂一直都在，是你前一個主管替你擋住的。
  • 做得好時要說：別只忙著處理缺點而忘了正向回饋。
  • 對「容易撤銷」的事容易說好：兩天的實驗 vs 要支援十年的產品，審慎度完全不同（見 ch22）。

總結 People Are Like Plants 人就像植物

小孩像植物：仙人掌要少水多陽光、番茄給點肥料就超棒。給六個小孩一樣的水光肥是平等，卻可能沒有一個得到他真正需要的。團隊成員也一樣——差別在激勵（motivation）與方向（direction）的配比。
Intrinsic vs Extrinsic：提升內在動機比砸錢更有效，靠三要素——Autonomy 自主（給方向、讓他決定怎麼到）、Mastery 精熟（技能像刀刃，不磨會鈍）、Purpose 目的（讓工作有意義，例如轉發客戶感謝信給團隊）。

延伸提問

  1. 回想你最近一次忙完一天卻覺得「今天什麼都沒做」。你是不是在「種香蕉卻數蘋果」？若改用「我的團隊今天有沒有更快樂、更有產能」計分，答案會不會不同？
  2. 你的團隊裡有沒有一個你正在「希望他會變好」的低績效者？把 SRE 那句話套上去——你願不願意給他一個兩個月、小而可量測、每週 check 的「up or out」計畫？拖下去，你的高績效者會付出什麼代價？
  3. 你上次給出的「硬回饋」，是不是包了一層讚美三明治？對方真的聽到「要改的是什麼」了嗎？用「仁慈但直接」重寫一遍。對照 ch02「你不是你的程式碼」。
  4. 把你的直屬團隊每個人想成一株植物：誰缺「水（激勵）」、誰缺「光（方向）」、誰兩者都缺（adrift）？你最近一次是給了他們真正需要的，還是齊頭式一視同仁？
  5. 列出團隊近期最在意的一個目標。每個成員真的都往「同一個北方」拉嗎？你們有沒有一句能寫下來的使命宣言？對照 ch06 規模化領導與 ch07 用指標衡量方向。

Part II — Culture
CHAPTER 05

How to Lead a Team

A great leader picks the what and trusts the team with the how
by Brian Fitzpatrick PDF p.109–134 Culture Scale Trade-offs
"Traditional managers worry about how to get things done, whereas great managers worry about what things get done." In plain terms: a weak manager controls the how; a great leader picks the what and trusts the team with the how.

Summary

Earlier chapters covered the culture and composition of teams; this one turns to the person ultimately responsible for making it all work — the leader. Google recognizes two roles: a Manager leads *people*, a Tech Lead (TL) leads *technology*; on small teams one person does both — a TLM. A boat without a captain is just a floating waiting room; a software project is the same — without a pilot, engineers burn valuable time.

The core philosophy is servant leadership: the new manager's biggest urge is to "manage," but the best advice is "above all, resist the urge to manage." A leader's job is to serve the team — remove obstacles, build consensus, create psychological safety via HRT — not apply the industrial-era "carrot and stick" that's harmful to creative work. The author runs through antipatterns against positive patterns, closing with "people are like plants": each member needs a different amount of "water" (motivation) and "light" (direction). Motivation comes down to the three drivers of intrinsic motivation — autonomy, mastery, and purpose.

Why read this

You're an IC sucked into leadership (manageritis). Want it or not, if you're good you'll likely steer at some point. This is about what to do when it happens — and it helps you understand your own leaders.
You just became a TL/TLM and feel "I did nothing today." It confronts the loss — you can't point at a pile of code anymore. Stop "counting apples while growing bananas": management's output is a happy, productive team.
You have a low performer you keep "hoping will improve." The SRE motto "hope is not a strategy" punctures it, with a two-month, measurable "up or out" process — and why ignoring it drives high performers away.
You want a leader people stay for and take risks for. From "lose the ego and apologize," through the gear-chain "always on stage," to "people are like plants" — abstract leadership turned into things you'll use in your next 1:1.

Key details

ROLES Managers, Tech Leads (and Both)

Two roles with similar planning skills but wildly different *people* skills.
  • Engineering Manager: Google requires an engineering background. Responsible for the performance, productivity, and happiness of everyone (incl. the TL) while meeting business needs — which don't always align, so it's a hard spot.
  • Tech Lead (TL): owns the technical side (decisions, architecture, priorities, velocity). Usually an IC too, choosing between doing it fast or delegating (slower) — delegating is right as the team grows.
  • TLM: default on small teams (both at once); large teams prefer a pair of leaders to avoid burnout, with senior-TLM mentoring.

SCALE Influencing Without Authority

Getting people *outside* your org to do what needs doing is one of the most powerful leadership traits you can develop.
Case: Jeff Dean led only a fraction of the team yet influenced the whole company. The author's Data Liberation Front got 50+ products to export data via Google Takeout with no executive directive — by identifying a strategic need, linking it to the mission, and building a tool that made integration easy. Influence comes from alignment + lowering friction, not a title.

MINDSET IC → Leadership & The Fear

Someone has to get in the driver's seat; you may be sucked in (manageritis).
  • Less code, hard-to-quantify output: don't "count apples while growing bananas" — a happy, productive team is itself the measure.
  • Peter Principle: people rise to their level of incompetence; Google guards against it by requiring you to "exceed expectations" at the higher level *before* promotion.
  • Good reasons to lead: ① scale yourself (what could a great team write under you?); ② you might just be good at it.
Warning: don't force the unwilling into management — you lose a great engineer and gain a crappy manager.

CULTURE Servant Leadership

Many managers catch a "disease" — doing to reports the awful things their managers did to them (micromanaging, ignoring low performers, hiring pushovers). The cure: serve the team like a butler tends a household.
Best advice (director Steve Vinter): "Above all, resist the urge to manage." A servant leader creates HRT, removes obstacles, advises, but isn't afraid to get their hands dirty. The only thing they "manage" is the team's technical *and* social health — the latter is harder but just as important.

TRADE-OFF The Engineering Manager: history & today

"Manager" carries over from military/industrial supervisors — the "carrot and stick" mule-driver style. But studies repeatedly show it's ineffective and harmful for creative workers: assembly-line workers were replaceable in days; engineers take months to ramp up and need nurturing and space.
  • Trust creates positive pressure: show you trust an employee and they live up to it. The Jerry story — "as long as you get your job done, I don't care what time you leave." Needing babysitting to work is the real problem.
  • Failure Is an Option: psychological safety lets people take bigger risks. "Failing at the impossible usually achieves more than succeeding at what you already could." Praise success in public, criticize in private; postmortems for learning, not blame.

PATTERNS Antipatterns ↔ Positive Patterns

✗ Antipatterns (avoid)

  • Hire pushovers: the team can't move without you. Instead hire people smarter than you who can replace you.
  • Ignore low performers: "hope is not a strategy"; set a two-month measurable plan, help up or out.
  • Ignore human issues: Jake, a new dad working from home, forced back by Pablo — no empathy.
  • Be everyone's friend: don't confuse friendship with a soft touch; power makes reciprocity feel forced.
  • Compromise the hiring bar: "A people hire A people; B people hire C people"; worst under time pressure.
  • Treat the team like children: permanent micromanagement = a hiring failure; even self-serve supplies show trust.

✓ Positive patterns (do)

  • Lose the ego: cultivate a collective team ego; apologize sincerely when wrong (earns respect).
  • Be a Zen master: stay calm (the gear chain amplifies you), always on stage; ask questions to lead them to their own answer.
  • Be a catalyst: build consensus (lead without authority; don't chase 100%).
  • Remove roadblocks: "knowing the right person beats knowing the right answer."
  • Be a teacher/mentor: the key skill is gauging how much help they need.
  • Set clear goals: everyone pulling the same "north"; write a concise mission statement.
  • Be honest: avoid the compliment sandwich; kind but direct (the Tim and Dean "train" stories).
  • Track happiness: end 1:1s with "What do you need?"; make implicit career goals explicit.

TIPS Other Tips and Tricks

  • Delegate, but get your hands dirty: new leaders must work to delegate (even if slower); on a new team, take a grungy task to earn respect.
  • Seek to replace yourself: hire people smarter than you, give them responsibility and occasional leadership.
  • Know when to make waves: don't tell yourself "it'll work itself out" — act now, waiting causes more damage.
  • Shield from chaos / give air cover: the chaos was always there; your previous manager shielded you.
  • Say when they do well: don't get so caught up in shortcomings you forget positive feedback.
  • Easy to say yes to what's easy to undo: a two-day experiment vs a ten-year product deserve different scrutiny (see ch22).

SUMMARY People Are Like Plants

Kids are like plants: cacti need little water but lots of sun; tomatoes excel with a little fertilizer. Give six kids identical water/light/fertilizer and that's equal — but none gets what it actually needs. Team members are the same — the variables are motivation and direction.
Intrinsic vs Extrinsic: raising intrinsic motivation beats cash, via three things — Autonomy (give direction, let them choose the how), Mastery (skills are a blade that dulls without sharpening), Purpose (make work meaningful, e.g., forward customer thank-yous to the team).

Key terms

Servant leadership
The leader's most important job is to serve the team (remove obstacles, build consensus, create HRT), not control it. Mnemonic: "resist the urge to manage."
Manager vs Tech Lead vs TLM
A Manager leads *people*, a TL leads *technology*, a TLM does both (default on small teams; large teams split into a pair to avoid burnout).
Influence without authority
Getting people outside your org to do what needs doing; via alignment to the mission + lowering friction (e.g., the Data Liberation Front / Takeout).
Counting apples while growing bananas
Measuring management work by an IC's output. Management's output is the team's happiness and productivity.
Peter Principle
Employees tend to rise to their level of incompetence. Google guards against it by requiring you to "exceed expectations" at the higher level before promotion.
Carrot and stick
The factory-era mule-driver style; ineffective and harmful for creative engineers who take months to ramp up.
Hope is not a strategy
SRE motto, applied to low performers: don't *hope* they improve — set measurable goals and help them up or out.
Compliment sandwich
Criticism wrapped in two compliments. Strongly discouraged: the key message gets drowned out; use "kind but direct."
The leader is always on stage
A leadership position is constantly watched; your visible attitude is amplified through the "gear chain" and spreads to the team.
Autonomy / Mastery / Purpose
Dan Pink's three drivers of intrinsic motivation (from Drive); raising intrinsic motivation beats throwing money.

Questions to push your thinking

  1. Recall a day you finished feeling "I did nothing today." Were you "counting apples while growing bananas"? If you scored "did my team get happier and more productive?", would the answer change?
  2. Is there a low performer you're "hoping will improve"? Apply the SRE line — will you give a two-month, small, measurable, weekly-checked "up or out" plan? What's the cost to your *high* performers of waiting?
  3. Was your last hard feedback wrapped in a compliment sandwich? Did they hear *what needs to change*? Rewrite it "kind but direct." Per ch02's "you are not your code."
  4. Picture each direct report as a plant: who lacks "water" (motivation), who lacks "light" (direction), who lacks both (adrift)? Did you give what they needed, or treat everyone identically?
  5. Name a goal your team cares about. Is everyone really pulling the same "north"? Do you have a writable mission statement? Per ch06 (leading at scale) and ch07 (measuring direction).
